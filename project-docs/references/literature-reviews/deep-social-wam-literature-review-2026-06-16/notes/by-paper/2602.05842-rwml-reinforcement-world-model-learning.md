## Primary-source facts (what the paper states)

- Goal: teach an LLM agent to model environment dynamics (predict action consequences) via a **self-supervised** objective, with no expert data, no stronger LLM, and no task-success reward.
- Method (WMRL): the same LLM policy is also asked to predict the next state. Given history and action, it generates `(reason, s_hat_{t+1})`. The reward compares the predicted next state to the REAL observed next state in embedding space: a **binary** reward `r^WM = 1 if d(s_hat_{t+1}, s_{t+1}) < tau_d else 0`, where `d = 1 - cos(E(s_hat), E(s))` from an off-the-shelf embedding model. Optimized with GRPO. The paper calls this signal the **sim-to-real gap reward** (RLSR).
- The training signal is grounded in the environment's observed next state, not in any model's self-judgment. Data: the target model rolls out its own trajectories, converted to `(history, action, next-state)` triplets; "too-easy" samples are subsampled out to focus learning.
- Stated failure modes of alternatives (the thesis-critical contribution):
    - **Next-state token prediction** (SFT to reproduce exact wording) overemphasizes surface-form fidelity, biases toward predictable low-entropy trajectories, and "can lead to model collapse."
    - **RL with LLM-as-a-judge rewards** is "susceptible to reward hacking, achieving high judge scores by exploiting linguistic priors rather than learning transferable action-conditioned dynamics." WMRL is "empirically less susceptible to reward hacking than LLM-as-a-judge."
    - **Binarized** rewards are found more robust to hacking than graded ones (their ablation).
- Evaluation: ALFWorld (text embodied household tasks) and tau^2-Bench (tool-use customer-service with a simulated user). Reported: significant gains over the base model from the self-supervised WM objective alone; combined with task-success reward, it beats direct task-success-reward RL by 6.9 (ALFWorld) and 5.7 (tau^2-Bench) points and matches expert-data training.

## Interpretation (flagged as inference)

- RWML is the engineering instantiation of the wave-4 thesis's central guardrail: "the agent must never score its own success." It replaces a self-judged reward (LLM-as-judge) with one grounded in the environment's observed next state (sim-to-real gap). The paper's empirical result that this is less hackable than LLM-as-judge is direct evidence FOR using the runtime verifier (not the actor's own CycleJudgment prose) as the success signal in any Minecraft autoresearch loop.
- The "next-token prediction causes collapse / surface-form trap" finding is a second argument for the repo's STRUCTURED-state framing: predicting typed deltas against a verifier is closer to RWML's semantic-distance reward than to token-level reproduction, and avoids the collapse mode.
- The binarized-reward-is-more-robust result is a small, transferable design note: a coarse "did the predicted delta match the verifier or not" signal may resist gaming better than a finely graded similarity score.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful**: the sim-to-real gap reward is precisely portable. In the repo, the WAM predicts a typed next-state delta; the runtime verifier produces the real next state; the reward (for refining the WAM or for an RL signal) is the grounded match between predicted and verifier-observed delta, NOT an LLM's opinion of the prediction. This is the cleanest published recipe for "verifier-grounded, advisory, no self-scoring." The too-easy-subsampling trick (drop transitions the model already predicts) is a cheap curriculum that pairs naturally with curiosity (collect the hard ones).
- **What to avoid / overclaim**: RWML's measured contribution is single-digit-point gains on two text-agent benchmarks; do not present it as a general proof. It also trains LLM weights via GRPO, which the repo's no-paid-training, advisory posture does not adopt; the borrow is the REWARD DESIGN (environment-grounded, binary, semantic-distance), not the weight-training pipeline.

## Limitations (theirs + spotted)

- Theirs: relies on an off-the-shelf embedding model and a distance threshold tau_d (a tuned hyperparameter); evaluated on two text environments only; the "easy" subsampling needs a separately SFT'd helper.
- Spotted: embedding-space cosine similarity is itself a learned proxy and could be gamed by predictions that are semantically near but causally wrong; the threshold tau_d hides a precision/recall tradeoff; no notion of multi-step compounding error in the WM (single-step transitions only).
