# VideoPhy - physical-commonsense benchmark for text-to-video (best model 39.6%)

- **title**: VideoPhy: Evaluating Physical Commonsense for Video Generation
- **authors**: Hritik Bansal, Zongyu Lin, Tianyi Xie, Zeshun Zong, Michal Yarom, Yonatan Bitton, Chenfanfu Jiang, Yizhou Sun, Kai-Wei Chang, Aditya Grover (UCLA; Google Research)
- **year**: 2024
- **venue/source**: arXiv (NeurIPS 2024 template)
- **arxiv_id**: 2406.03520
- **urls**: paper https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.03520 ; code https://github.com/Hritikbansal/videophy ; LaTeX at `papers/latex/2406.03520/`
- **source availability**: latex (full, deep-read)

This is the survey's `bansal2024videophy`, the representative early physics-commonsense
benchmark, deep-read for its definition and numbers.

## Primary-source facts (what the paper states or shows)

What it introduced and why it matters (plain): a benchmark that asks whether
text-to-video (T2V) models generate videos that obey intuitive physics, separately
from whether the videos look good. It established physical commonsense as a distinct,
measurable axis and showed that even the best models fail it most of the time.

- **Physical commonsense** (plain definition): the intuitive understanding humans
  have of how matter behaves (a ball rolls down a slope, water poured into a glass
  raises the level, a rigid object does not deform), learned from experience without
  formal physics.
- Dataset: **688 high-quality, human-verified captions** describing interactions
  between states of matter: solid-solid, solid-fluid, fluid-fluid. Built by an LLM
  generating candidate captions, human verification, and difficulty annotation.
- Protocol: generate videos from **12 T2V models** (open: OpenSora, SVD,
  VideoCrafter2, CogVideoX; closed: Pika, Lumiere, Gen-2, Dream Machine). Then
  collect **binary human annotations** on two axes:
  1. **Semantic adherence**: does the video follow the caption?
  2. **Physical commonsense**: does the video obey physical laws intuitively?
- Headline result: existing T2V models "severely lack the capability to follow
  caption accurately and generate videos with physical commonsense." The best model,
  **CogVideoX-5B, jointly satisfies both (text-adherent AND physically plausible)
  for only 39.6% of instances**.
- Fine-grained failure: models are worst on **solid-solid interaction** (ball
  bouncing on the floor, hammer hitting a nail) and "often struggle to accurately
  identify individual objects and comprehend their material properties" (e.g. a
  rigid body should not deform over time).
- Auto-evaluator: **VideoCon-Physics**, a fine-tuned video-language model (built on
  VideoCon) that scores semantic adherence and physical commonsense automatically;
  reported +9 points (semantic) and +15 points (physical) over Gemini-Pro-Vision-1.5
  on unseen prompts, and generalizes to unseen generators.
- Methodological note the authors flag (verbatim spirit): you cannot easily use a
  physics simulator as ground truth, because building accurate 3D geometry +
  material parameters + lighting from a single video is itself unsolved, and "physical
  simulations are not equivalent to ground truth ... merely numerical solvers." So
  they rely on human perception as the referent.

## Interpretation (my reading, labeled inference)

- VideoPhy is the clean early proof of the lane's recurring theme: a video can be
  visually convincing yet physically wrong, and the two must be measured separately.
  39.6% best-case joint accuracy is the concrete number to quote when explaining why
  pixel/perceptual metrics (PSNR/SSIM/LPIPS/FVD) are insufficient for "world
  understanding."
- It also illustrates the harder meta-problem the repo escapes: VideoPhy must fall
  back on expensive human annotation because there is no cheap programmatic ground
  truth for "did the pixels obey physics." The repo's Minecraft runtime has the
  opposite property: physical truth (inventory, position, block, container deltas)
  is exactly and cheaply observable via the verifier, so the repo never needs a
  VideoPhy-style human physics panel for its physical layer. (This contrast belongs
  to `data-and-training-feasibility.md`; here it is one-line relevance only.)

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful**: the two-axis split (semantic adherence vs physical
  commonsense, scored independently) as a template for separating "did the action do
  what was asked" from "did the world stay physically coherent." For the repo this
  maps onto verifier-checked physical deltas (coherence) vs judged
  progress/instruction-following (adherence), already separate in the runtime.
- **What would be overclaim**: importing VideoPhy's 39.6% as if it bounded a
  structured Minecraft predictor. It is a T2V pixel-video result. Cite it as the
  canonical fidelity-vs-physics datapoint, not as evidence about this repo.
