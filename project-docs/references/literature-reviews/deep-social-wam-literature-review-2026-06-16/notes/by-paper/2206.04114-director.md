## Plain-language: what it introduced and why it mattered

Director is the clean answer to a long-standing failure in hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL):
prior HRL methods needed a human to hand-specify the subgoal space (for example global XY map
coordinates, or robot poses) or a set of pre-training subtasks. Director removes that requirement by
choosing subgoals *inside the learned latent space of a world model*, so the agent invents its own
goal space from pixels with no domain knowledge.

The shape is the textbook manager/worker (feudal) hierarchy, but realized inside a Dreamer world
model:
1. A **world model** (the RSSM from PlaNet/DreamerV2) compresses pixel history into a compact latent
   and predicts forward in latent space.
2. A **goal autoencoder** compresses the 1024-dim continuous latent into a small discrete code (an
   8x8 grid of categoricals, the DreamerV2 vector-of-categoricals trick), so the manager faces a small
   discrete choice instead of a 1024-dim continuous control problem.
3. A **manager** policy selects a new latent goal (as a discrete code) every fixed K=8 steps,
   maximizing task reward plus an exploration reward.
4. A **worker** policy produces primitive actions to reach the decoded latent goal, rewarded by a
   "max-cosine" similarity between its current latent and the goal latent.

Why it mattered: it is the first practical, general method to learn hierarchical behavior end-to-end
from pixels with no hand-specified goal space, and it beat flat (non-hierarchical) agents on very
sparse-reward, long-horizon tasks (including egocentric 3D maze navigation with a quadruped), while
also working across Atari, DMControl, DMLab, and Crafter. It is the most direct academic precedent for
"plan a subgoal in a learned latent, then act," which is exactly the structure a hierarchical world
model needs.

## Definitions (first use)

- **Hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL)**: an agent split into a higher level that issues
  abstract goals/commands at a slow time scale and a lower level that executes primitive actions at the
  fast clock rate of the environment.
- **Manager / worker (feudal) hierarchy**: the two-level form where a manager sets a goal and a worker
  achieves it; the manager does not say *how*, only *what*. (Roots: Dayan-Hinton feudal RL 1993; FeUdal
  Networks 1703.01161; see those notes.)
- **Subgoal / latent goal**: a target state the worker should reach. In Director the goal is a point in
  the world model's latent space, decoded from a small discrete code.
- **Goal autoencoder**: an autoencoder mapping the high-dim latent state to a small discrete code and
  back, so the manager's action space is a compact set of categoricals (here 8 one-hot rows over 8
  options each).
- **RSSM (Recurrent State-Space Model)**: the latent dynamics backbone introduced by PlaNet, with a
  deterministic recurrent path and a stochastic latent path (see `notes/by-paper/1811.04551-planet-rssm.md`).
- **Temporally-abstract exploration**: rewarding the *manager* for choosing novel goals (high
  reconstruction error under the goal autoencoder), so exploration happens at the goal time scale rather
  than per primitive step.
- **Imagined rollout**: a trajectory the policies are trained on, generated inside the world model
  (Dreamer's "learn in imagination"), not in the real environment.

## Primary-source facts

- **World model**: RSSM with representation model, dynamics model, decoder (reconstruction as a
  training signal), and reward predictor, optimized by the Dreamer variational objective
  (`method.tex`, eq. wmloss). Latent states are 1024-dim continuous vectors.
- **Goal autoencoder**: encodes a latent state into a matrix of 8x8 logits, samples one one-hot per
  row (8 of 64 dims active), straight-through gradients; trained by reconstruction + KL to a uniform
  prior so all codes get used. Trained alongside the world model but contributes no gradients to it.
- **Manager**: outputs a vector of categoricals in the discrete code space every K=8 steps; goals are
  decoded into latent states before being handed to the worker. Maximizes discounted task reward
  (weight 1.0) + exploration reward (weight 0.1), each with its own critic, returns normalized by EMA
  std. "No off-policy correction is needed because the imagined rollouts are on-policy" (`method.tex`),
  an explicit contrast to HIRO.
- **Exploration reward**: novelty as goal-autoencoder reconstruction error on the next latent
  (`method.tex`, eq. explrew).
- **Worker**: conditioned on current latent + decoded goal, produces primitive actions; reward is the
  "max-cosine" similarity (cosine, but both vectors normalized by the larger magnitude so the worker
  matches angle and magnitude). By default the worker maximizes *only* the goal reward (an ablation adds
  task reward and improves further).
- **Interpretability**: latent goals are decoded into images for visualization, so the chosen subgoals
  are human-inspectable.
- **Results**: solves two new sparse-reward suites that flat RL fails (including Egocentric Ant Maze
  with only proprioception and an egocentric camera, no global position); learns across Atari, Control
  Suite, DMLab, Crafter.
- **Stated limitations (`discussion.tex`)**: (1) the manager treats its action space as a black box,
  unaware its actions are states; (2) the fixed every-K-steps goal switch is rigid (no adaptive
  termination); (3) goals are single points in latent space, not distributions or masks over only the
  relevant state parts; (4) extending beyond two levels would need *temporally-abstract dynamics* to
  avoid exponentially longer training batches.
- **Related-work taxonomy (`related.tex`)**: the paper organizes HRL into three sub-threads, useful as
  a map: (a) pre-training low-level skills on easier tasks; (b) discovering latent skills by
  mutual-information maximization (VIC, DIAYN, DADS, and others); (c) training the low level as a
  goal-conditioned policy (HER, HAC, HIRO). It also notes FeUdal Networks "did not demonstrate clear
  benefits over an LSTM baseline" and HAC "required semantic goal spaces."

## Interpretation (labeled inference)

- Director is the single best academic template for the project's "plan in a learned latent, then
  act" idea, but with a crucial caveat for this repo: Director's manager *acts* (it selects the goal
  that drives behavior), whereas this repo's WAM must stay **advisory** (it predicts and scores
  consequences, never selects the action). So Director is borrowed as a *structural analogy* for
  multi-level latent prediction, not as a control loop to adopt. (Cross-ref the project rule in
  `hierarchical-wam-for-minecraft-societies.md` section 7 and the repo SPEC observe-only policy.)
- Director's "no off-policy correction needed because rollouts are on-policy" line is a tidy statement
  of why imagination-based HRL sidesteps the non-stationarity that HIRO had to patch (the lower level's
  drift changes the higher level's action space). It is a maturity signal: even the cleanest deep HRL
  agents are built around a known instability.
- Director's limitation (4), that more than two levels needs temporally-abstract dynamics, is exactly
  the gap THICK (ICLR 2024) and the "limits of hierarchical world models" paper (2406.00483) push on,
  and it foreshadows why deep multi-level hierarchy is still unsolved (see the theme file maturity
  section).

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful (engineering this repo could borrow)**: the *vocabulary and structure* of
  subgoal-conditioned latent prediction (a high level names a target state, prediction/evaluation is
  conditioned on that subgoal), and the *discrete-code goal space* idea (compress a large state into a
  small typed set of choices) as an analogy for the repo's typed subgoal/CycleGoal records. The
  interpretability move (decode the latent goal so a human can read it) maps to the repo rule that
  predictions must be evidence-inspectable.
- **Research contribution (NOT to adopt as runtime authority)**: Director's actual contribution, an
  end-to-end RL agent whose manager and worker are *trained by RL in imagination to act*, is out of
  scope. The repo's LLM proposes actions and the runtime owns truth; the repo does not train a manager
  policy to select goals, and the WAM must not select, fill args, mark progress, or override verifiers.

## Relevance to this repo (one line)

The canonical proof that a high-level policy can pick a subgoal in a learned latent and a low-level
policy can reach it; this repo borrows the subgoal-conditioned-prediction structure as an *advisory*
analogy, not the act-by-RL control loop.

## Which WAM layer(s) it informs

Cross-layer and Institutional/Settlement. Director is the architectural template for the *dependency
across temporal resolutions* (a slow high-level goal over fast low-level primitives) that the repo's
4-layer stack needs for multi-cycle routines and post-goal continuation. No material/social content of
its own (it is single-agent control); it informs the *form* of hierarchy, not the social *content*.
