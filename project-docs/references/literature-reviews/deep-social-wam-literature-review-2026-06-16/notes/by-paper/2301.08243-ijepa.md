## What it introduced and why it mattered (plain language)

I-JEPA is the first concrete, working instance of the non-generative camp's idea: do not
predict pixels, predict representations. Given one visible "context" block of an image,
I-JEPA predicts the learned features (not the pixels) of several masked "target" blocks of
the same image. The motivation: a lot of pixel detail is unpredictable and irrelevant
(exact texture, exact leaf positions), so a model that spends capacity reconstructing it
wastes effort; predicting in an abstract feature space lets the model keep what is semantic
and drop what is noise. It mattered because it turned LeCun's 2022 manifesto (predict in
representation space) into a method that beat pixel-reconstruction (MAE) on ImageNet linear
probing while being far cheaper to train. It is the conceptual seed of V-JEPA and V-JEPA 2,
the non-generative answer to "what should a world model predict."

Newcomer terms:
- JEPA (Joint-Embedding Predictive Architecture): predict the embedding of a target signal
  from the embedding of a context signal, with the loss computed in embedding space (not
  pixel space).
- Representation / latent space: the model's internal feature vectors, as opposed to raw
  pixels.
- Representation collapse: the failure mode where the encoder outputs the same constant
  embedding for everything (trivially "predictable"); JEPA avoids it with an asymmetric
  encoder design (EMA target-encoder).
- EBM (Energy-Based Model) framing: a unifying view where training pushes "compatible"
  inputs to low energy; the paper uses it to contrast three families (below).

## Primary-source facts

- Three architecture families contrasted (their Background, via the EBM lens):
  (1) Joint-Embedding Architecture (JEA): two views, similar embeddings, relies on hand-
  crafted augmentations and risks collapse. (2) Generative Architecture: reconstruct the
  signal `y` from a corrupted `x` in input/pixel space (e.g. MAE). (3) JEPA: like generative
  but the loss is in embedding space, not input space.
- Method: context-encoder (ViT) sees one large context block; target-encoder (EMA of the
  context-encoder) produces target features; a narrow predictor ViT, conditioned on masked-
  position tokens, predicts target-block features. Multi-block masking: sample ~4 target
  blocks (scale 0.15-0.2), one context block (scale 0.85-1.0), remove overlap. Loss is L2 in
  feature space. Targets are masked on the encoder OUTPUT, not the input, which the paper says
  is crucial for high semantic level.
- Input/output: image in; predicted target-block representations out. No action space (this is
  representation pretraining, not a control model).
- Results: ViT-H/14 on ImageNet in < 1200 GPU-hours; >2.5x faster than iBOT ViT-S/16, >10x more
  efficient than MAE ViT-H/14. Beats MAE on ImageNet-1K linear probing, semi-supervised 1%,
  and semantic transfer; competitive with view-invariant methods and better on low-level tasks
  (object counting, depth).
- Verbatim thesis: "Compared to generative methods that predict in pixel/token space, I-JEPA
  makes use of abstract prediction targets for which unnecessary pixel-level details are
  potentially eliminated, thereby leading the model to learn more semantic features";
  "Predicting in representation space significantly reduces the total computation needed."
- Stated limitations (theirs/spotted): a single-image static model (no time, no actions, no
  control); semantic quality depends heavily on the masking strategy; not yet a world model in
  the predictive-dynamics sense (V-JEPA / V-JEPA 2 add time and actions).

## Interpretation (inference, labeled)

- I-JEPA is the cleanest one-paper statement of the non-generative camp's core claim, and it
  carries empirical weight: predicting features beat reconstructing pixels on the metrics
  measured, at much lower cost. For the debate's "two-column" treatment, this is the anchor
  citation for "you do not need to generate pixels to learn a useful predictive model."
- The leap from I-JEPA (static images, no actions) to a world model is exactly what V-JEPA 2
  (2506.09985) completes by adding time and an action-conditioned predictor. I-JEPA alone is
  not a WAM; it is the representation-prediction principle the WAM camp builds on.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Mechanically useful: the principle "predict only the predictable part, in an abstract space,
  and drop un-predictable detail" is directly relevant to a structured-state social WAM, whose
  whole bet is that typed social-material state is the predictable, decision-relevant part and
  pixels are the wasteful part. I-JEPA gives the project a citable, empirically-backed reason to
  not predict pixels. (The repo's "abstract space" is typed schema state, an even more explicit
  structure than I-JEPA's learned latents.)
- Avoid / overclaim: I-JEPA is image SSL, not a control or social model; do not present its
  ImageNet wins as evidence that a social WAM will work. The transfer is the principle, not the
  numbers. Also note its own collapse caveat: any predict-in-latent-space design needs a
  collapse-avoidance mechanism; a structured-schema WAM sidesteps this by predicting typed
  values rather than free latents, which is a relevant design advantage to state plainly.
