# Open Problems in Cooperative AI

- title: Open Problems in Cooperative AI
- authors: Allan Dafoe, Edward Hughes, Yoram Bachrach, Tantum Collins, Kevin R. McKee, Joel Z. Leibo, Kate Larson, Thore Graepel
- year: 2020 (arXiv); NeurIPS 2020 Cooperative AI workshop foundation document
- venue/source: arXiv 2012.08630
- urls: paper https://arxiv.org/abs/2012.08630 ; hf https://huggingface.co/papers/2012.08630 (verified)
- source availability: LaTeX read (`papers/latex/2012.08630/main.tex`, 926 lines).

## Primary-source facts (what the paper states)

- Thesis: name and scope a research field, "Cooperative AI", whose object is the
  many aspects of the problem of cooperation, where agents (machine and/or human)
  seek ways to jointly improve welfare. Explicitly NOT the union of MARL, game
  theory, social choice, HCI/alignment, NLP, but an independent bet on a unifying
  agenda and vocabulary.
- The cooperation-failure decomposition (citing the rationalist-war literature,
  Fearon 1995, Powell 2006): cooperation between rational agents fails for two
  reasons, informational problems and commitment problems (a third, issue
  indivisibility, often reduces to a prior commitment problem). This two-cause
  split organizes the whole paper.
- Four cooperative capabilities (Section 4):
  1. Understanding: predict others' behavior, beliefs, intentions (theory of mind,
     opponent modeling). [Covered by the repo's theory-of-mind theme; not re-derived here.]
  2. Communication: share information credibly, build common ground, signaling.
  3. Commitment: make credible threats and promises (the focus of Section 4.3).
  4. Institutions: the social structure (beliefs, norms, rules) that scaffolds the
     other three (Section 4.4).
- Common vs conflicting interests + the planner vs individual perspective (Sec 3):
  the "individual perspective" asks how a single agent should behave to promote
  cooperation; the "planner perspective" asks how to design the rules/environment so
  cooperation emerges. Mechanism design and institutions sit on the planner side.
- Commitment (Sec 4.3): a commitment device compels fulfillment either by a soft
  incentive change (a penalty for non-compliance) or a hard pruning of the action
  space ("burning one's boats"). Devices are classified on two axes:
  - unilateral (one agent can execute) vs multilateral (needs others' consent;
    legal contracts are the canonical multilateral device);
  - unconditional vs conditional. A unilateral conditional commitment "play C iff
    the other plays C" alone solves the one-shot Prisoner's Dilemma; an
    unconditional one does not.
  - Four device families, each needing social infrastructure: reputation, delegation
    to a social planner/third party, contracts, and trusted hardware/smart contracts.
- Institutions (Sec 4.4): a system of beliefs, norms, or rules that fixes the "rules
  of the game", producing stable patterns of behavior. Cooperative institutions
  resolve coordination problems and align incentives in social dilemmas. The paper
  layers the vocabulary: conventions (common-interest coordination) -> norms
  (mixed-motive, reinforced by social reward/sanction) -> institutions (thicker,
  formal allocation of roles, responsibilities, power, resources).
  - Decentralized institutions and norms: no central authority; structures emerge
    from interaction over time; norms encode sanctions/social consequences for
    transgression and can be read as equilibria of strategic games (cites Bicchieri).
    Open problems: design distributed institutions with good global properties;
    predict which norms have the best properties; understand norm devising/enforcement
    in mixed human-machine groups; generalize norms across circumstances and co-players.
  - Centralized institutions: social choice theory (preference aggregation, voting,
    Arrow's impossibility), fairness protocols (cut-and-choose, maximum Nash welfare,
    Shapley value for credit division), and mechanism design (design rules so honesty
    and a socially optimal outcome are in each agent's interest; impossibility results
    of Gibbard-Satterthwaite, "islands of possibility" for single-peaked preferences
    [median mechanisms] and quasi-linear utility [Groves/VCG, auctions]).
  - Trust and reputation systems are called out as a distinct institutional family
    (reputation as a collateral asset that aligns incentives in transient encounters).
- Downsides (Section 5), three categories: (1) cooperative competence itself can
  harm excluded parties and enable collusion (cooperation among criminals, dark-web
  reputation systems); (2) cooperative capabilities may improve coercive ones
  (deception); (3) cooperation often depends on coercion (pro-social punishment) and
  competition, so they are hard to separate.

## Interpretation (my reading, labeled inference)

- This is the field-definition document for lane 33. Its capability taxonomy gives
  a clean place for every other source: Understanding (the theory-of-mind theme),
  Communication, Commitment (contracts/commitment-device papers), Institutions
  (norm emergence, mechanism design, reputation). The repo's 4-layer stack maps onto
  it: the Social layer is mostly Understanding + Communication + Commitment between
  actors; the Institutional layer is the Institutions capability.
- The informational-vs-commitment dichotomy is a useful diagnostic for the repo:
  the repo's verifier and structured state attack the *informational* problem (it
  makes "who did what to whom, with what evidence" common knowledge). The harder,
  less-solved half is the *commitment* problem (can an actor's promise to return a
  borrowed tool be made credible), which is exactly the obligation/credit ledger's
  job. The repo records commitments and their fulfillment as evidence; it does not
  enforce them, which is the honest limit.
- The planner-vs-individual split is the cleanest statement of the repo's advisory
  rule: the repo's WAM lives on neither side as an actor. It does not act as a
  planner (it never sets rules or transfers) and it does not act as an individual
  (it never picks actions). It predicts and scores both perspectives' consequences.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Mechanically useful:
  - The capability taxonomy as the organizing scaffold for the repo's social and
    institutional layers, and as a checklist for what a Social/Institutional WAM
    must be able to predict (will a commitment be made, kept, sanctioned).
  - The commitment-device taxonomy (unilateral/multilateral, conditional/
    unconditional; reputation/delegation/contract/hardware) as a typology for the
    repo's promise/obligation events: a `lend_item -> return_item` loop is a
    multilateral conditional commitment; a one-shot hard-to-reverse deposit into
    shared storage is a unilateral unconditional commitment.
  - The downsides section as a built-in honesty section for the repo: predicting and
    measuring exclusion/collusion (a cooperating subset imposing costs on outsiders)
    is itself an admissible Institutional-layer prediction target, not just a risk.
- Research contribution (what the repo could add):
  - Cooperative AI is framed almost entirely in abstract or MARL-gridworld terms
    (matrix games, Markov games, voting over abstract alternatives). The paper never
    grounds commitment or institutions in a verified, physical, material substrate.
    A WAM that predicts whether a *physical* commitment (a tool actually returned,
    a shared store actually replenished) was kept, scored against verifier evidence,
    instantiates the Commitment and Institutions capabilities on ground truth the
    field's own examples lack.

## WAM layer(s) informed

- **Social WAM** (Understanding, Communication, Commitment between actors) and
  **Institutional / Settlement WAM** (Institutions: conventions, norms, mechanism
  design, reputation, fairness/credit division). Frames the whole lane.
