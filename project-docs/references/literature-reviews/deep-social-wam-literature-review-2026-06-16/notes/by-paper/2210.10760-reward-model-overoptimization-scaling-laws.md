## Primary-source facts

- **Research question**: in RLHF you optimize a policy against a LEARNED reward model (RM)
  that is an imperfect proxy for the true objective. Optimizing the proxy too hard
  eventually HURTS the true objective (Goodhart's law); the authors call this
  **overoptimization**. They measure its size and how it scales, so you can predict how
  far a learned model can be safely optimized against.
- **Method (the key trick that made this measurable)**: a SYNTHETIC setup. A fixed large
  "gold" RM (the 6B InstructGPT RM) plays the role of humans and provides the labels used
  to train smaller PROXY RMs (3M to 3B params). They then optimize the policy against the
  proxy and watch the GOLD score. This removes the cost of human labels and gives a clean
  ground-truth signal. Setup is InstructGPT-style: GPT-3 SFT policies, PPO for RL, best-of-n
  (BoN) for the sampling method.
- **The two empirical functional forms** (gold score R as a function of the distance
  `d := sqrt(KL(pi || pi_init))`, KL from the initial to the optimized policy):
  - Best-of-n: `R_bon(d) = d (alpha_bon - beta_bon d)`.
  - RL (PPO): `R_rl(d) = d (alpha_rl - beta_rl log d)`.
  - Both have `R(0) = 0`, both rise then fall (the gold score "initially increases and
    later decreases"), and the two methods have DIFFERENT functional forms. KL is used as a
    distance budget because it grows monotonically during RL and is analytic for BoN
    (`KL_bon = log n - (n-1)/n`).
- **Smooth coefficient scaling (the "scaling law")**: the `alpha` and `beta` coefficients
  vary smoothly (approximately logarithmically) with the number of proxy-RM PARAMETERS.
  `alpha_rl` is nearly INDEPENDENT of RM size; the whole RM-size effect lives in
  `beta_rl`. This lets you predict the peak attainable gold score for an unseen RM size.
  The BoN form, hypothesized on data up to n=1000 (KL ~6 nats), correctly predicted an
  advance experiment out to n=60000 (KL ~10 nats): a genuine extrapolation.
- **Goodhart decomposition (their interpretation of the two terms)**: the `alpha` term
  (linear) is read as **regressional Goodhart** (selecting on a proxy also selects on the
  proxy's noise; the gap between the proxy slope and the gold slope); the `beta` term is
  read as **extremal Goodhart** (the proxy-gold relationship breaks down at extreme
  optimization), and is "mostly responsible" for the eventual unbounded loss of gold
  utility. The smooth decrease of `beta` with RM size is read as a smooth improvement in
  RM robustness.
- **KL penalty is ineffective (a striking negative result)**: adding an explicit KL
  penalty in RL lets the policy reach a higher PROXY score for a given KL, but it does NOT
  move the gold-score-vs-KL frontier; its effect on the gold score is "akin to early
  stopping." A KL penalty even gives a strictly LARGER proxy-gold gap, so they set the KL
  penalty to 0 for all other RL runs. (They flag this could be hyperparameter-sensitive.)
- **Weak dependence on policy size**: larger policies start higher and gain less from
  optimizing the RM, but they overoptimize by a SIMILAR amount: the proxy-gold gap and the
  KL at peak gold score are nearly the same across policy sizes. Larger policy does not
  mean faster or worse hacking here. (Only two policy sizes tested, 1.2B and 6B.)
- **RL is far less KL-efficient than BoN**: RL "consumes" much more KL for the same
  optimization (KL grows roughly quadratically in RL steps without a penalty, vs ~log(n)
  for BoN). Implication: KL distance is an inadequate single metric for "amount of
  (over)optimization" because it is not comparable across methods.
- **More data helps**: larger RM training datasets give better gold scores and less
  goodharting (the data-size scaling of alpha/beta is less clean than RM-size scaling).
- **Iterated/online RLHF (analytic extension)**: under additivity-of-d and
  constant-coefficient assumptions, doing k smaller RL iterations (refit the RM between
  them) raises the final gold score by `beta_rl * d * log(k)`, but does NOT touch the
  `alpha_rl` (regressional) term. So periodically refreshing the RM mitigates the extremal
  part of overoptimization but not the regressional part.

## Interpretation (labeled inference)

- The load-bearing object in this paper is the GAP between a learned proxy and the truth.
  The whole phenomenon exists BECAUSE the scorer is a learned model. A deterministic
  verifier has no proxy-gold gap to open up, so the `beta` (extremal) collapse mechanism
  this paper measures does not have a learned-proxy surface to act on. This is the single
  cleanest argument in the literature for "deterministic verifier over learned reward
  model" when a deterministic checker exists.
- The "KL penalty only does early stopping" result is a caution against believing that a
  trust-region knob fixes reward hacking; it buys time, it does not change the frontier.
- The iterated-RLHF result is the academic shape of the repo's "refresh the held-out
  scenarios / refit between cycles" intuition: refitting helps the extremal term, not the
  regressional one.

## Relevance to this repo (mechanical import vs research contribution)

- **Mechanical import**: (1) the proxy-gold gap is a usable hacking DETECTOR. If the repo
  ever trains a learned advisory predictor or a learned social score, it should track the
  gap between that learned score and the deterministic verifier outcome; a widening gap
  under more optimization is overoptimization in progress. (2) Treat KL/optimization budget
  as a spendable resource and expect non-monotonic true performance, do not optimize an
  imperfect score to convergence. (3) If a learned signal is ever used, refit it
  iteratively (the `log k` benefit) rather than optimizing one frozen model hard.
- **Why the learned RM is the more hackable choice (direct tie)**: this paper is the
  quantitative demonstration that optimizing a LEARNED reward proxy drives true performance
  down past a peak, with a smooth, predictable, parameter-scaling severity. The repo's
  deterministic runtime verifier is exactly the "gold" side of this experiment, not the
  "proxy" side. Replacing it with a learned reward model would import this entire
  overoptimization failure surface.
- **Research contribution it is NOT**: the scaling laws are not a result the repo
  reproduces or extends; they are SUPPORT for keeping the verifier deterministic. The
  finding is a reason for an architectural choice, not a deliverable.
- **What it does not cover**: the synthetic gold RM is itself a model, not ground truth, so
  the absolute numbers are setting-specific; results are RLHF-on-text, not embodied;
  social/multi-step verification is out of scope. The transfer to the repo is the
  MECHANISM (learned proxy overoptimizes), not the coefficients.
