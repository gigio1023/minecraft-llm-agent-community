# Large Language Models Fail on Trivial Alterations to Theory-of-Mind Tasks

- title: Large Language Models Fail on Trivial Alterations to Theory-of-Mind Tasks
- authors: Tomer D. Ullman (Harvard)
- year: 2023
- venue/source: arXiv 2302.08399 (widely cited workshop/preprint)
- arxiv_id: 2302.08399
- urls: paper https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.08399 ; not in HF papers feed
- source availability: LaTeX (`papers/latex/2302.08399/main.tex`), read in full.
- relation: this is the direct critique/rebuttal of Kosinski "Theory of Mind May Have
  Spontaneously Emerged in LLMs" (2302.02083). Cite the two together as the balanced pair.

## Primary-source facts (what the paper states)

- Target: Kosinski (2302.02083) reported that GPT-3.5 passes classic false-belief
  vignettes (unexpected-contents "Smarties" task; unexpected-transfer "Sally-Anne" task)
  at a level comparable to 9-year-old children, and posed a dilemma: either accept the
  measures and concede LLMs have ToM, or reject the measures.
- Method: take Kosinski's exact vignettes/prompts (same setup, completion probabilities)
  and apply *principle-preserving perturbations*, small changes that do not violate ToM,
  and check whether the model still answers correctly.
- Findings: the reported successes flip under trivial alterations. Examples:
  - Unexpected contents: make the opaque bag **transparent** (so the contents are
    visible) -> GPT-3.5 still attributes the false belief that the bag is full of
    chocolate (P_chocolate = 95%), i.e. it ignores that the agent can now SEE inside.
  - Variants where the character **cannot read** the label, where a **trusted friend**
    told the character the true contents, or where the character **filled the bag
    herself** -> all flip the model to the wrong belief attribution.
  - Unexpected transfer (Sally-Anne): make the containers **transparent** glass; change
    "in" to "on"; add truthful testimony; query the belief of the person who moved the
    object -> all cause failure.
- Methodological thesis (the transferable part):
  - The "zero-hypothesis" (null) for ToM in machines should be **skeptical**: presume
    strongly that the model does NOT have ToM until shown robust behavior.
  - **Outlying failures should outweigh average success rates.** Analogy: a calculator
    that answers "5*5=25" 100 times but fails "213*261" has not learned multiplication;
    a robust algorithm survives simple input changes. Do not average 20 successes against
    1 principled failure and declare a pass.
  - **Benchmark-gaming risk**: as soon as a systematic generator of ToM variations or a
    test-suite is published, an LLM can ingest enough such data to pass it, without
    answering what it has actually learned. (Ullman flags his own paper as partly
    "shooting future researchers in the foot" by publishing failure modes.)
  - **Evaluate the algorithm, not just I/O**: people attribute mental states based on the
    likely *process* generating a behavior, not only input-output. So one can hold ToM
    tests valid for human children while remaining skeptical of an LLM that passes them.
  - Ullman is explicitly NOT a mystic: he says ToM can in principle be built in silicon
    (cites Bayesian inverse-planning models), and recommends *integrating* such models
    with LLMs rather than expecting ToM to emerge from more text.

## Interpretation (my reading, labeled inference)

- This is the balance anchor for the whole lane: it is why every LLM-ToM claim must be
  stated as "performs well on benchmark X" rather than "has theory of mind." The flips
  under transparency/perception perturbations are especially relevant to an embodied
  project, because the project's hardest ToM cases are perception-grounded ("Bob did not
  see the chest move").
- The "outlying failures outweigh averages" and "evaluate the process" principles are the
  same epistemics the project already enforces with verifiers: a fluent declaration is not
  evidence; world-anchored, perturbation-robust behavior is.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Mechanically useful for this repo (as evaluation discipline, not code):
  - When the repo builds any ToM/Social-WAM evaluation, design **principle-preserving
    perturbations** (transparent vs opaque container, can/cannot perceive, truthful
    testimony, self-caused state) and require robustness, not average pass rate.
  - Score worst-case / outlier failures, not means. This matches the repo's analysis-scope
    rule (worst-case, transfer-checked) and verifier-first stance.
  - Avoid publishing a fixed, enumerable ToM test-suite as the sole metric; prefer
    generative, world-anchored, verifier-checked scenarios that cannot be memorized.
- Research-contribution caution:
  - Use this paper to *bound claims*: a repo Social WAM that predicts another actor well in
    one scenario family has not demonstrated "theory of mind." The honest claim is bounded
    predictive accuracy under stated perturbations, scored against verifier evidence.
