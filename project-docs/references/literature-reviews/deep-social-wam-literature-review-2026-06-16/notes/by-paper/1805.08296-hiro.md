# HIRO: Data-Efficient Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning (Nachum, Gu, Lee, Levine, 2018)

The data-efficient goal-conditioned HRL agent: a higher-level policy proposes *state goals* to a
lower-level policy, both trained off-policy, with an "off-policy correction" that relabels stale
high-level goals because the lower level keeps changing. Source: LaTeX deep-read at
`papers/latex/1805.08296/main.tex`.

## Plain-language: what it introduced and why it mattered

HIRO is the most-cited *goal-conditioned* HRL method: the higher-level policy, every fixed number of
steps, outputs a **goal that is a target (relative) state** for the lower-level policy; the lower level
is rewarded for reaching that state. The key problem HIRO solved is **non-stationarity**: because the
lower-level policy is still learning, the *meaning* of a given high-level goal keeps changing (the same
goal produces different behavior over training), so the higher level is trying to learn against a
moving action space. HIRO fixes this with an **off-policy correction**: when reusing an old transition
from the replay buffer to train the higher level, it relabels the high-level goal to the one that would
best explain the low-level actions that were actually taken.

Why it mattered: it made HRL work off-policy (hence sample-efficient, a few million steps for complex
simulated-robot behaviors like push-and-reach) and it made the goal space *generic* (a learned target
state, not a hand-built semantic space), substantially beating prior HRL methods. It is the canonical
goal-conditioned-HRL reference and the clearest illustration of the non-stationarity problem that
plagues two-level RL hierarchies.

## Definitions (first use)

- **Goal-conditioned policy**: a policy that takes a goal (here a target state) as an extra input and
  is rewarded for reaching it; one policy can pursue many goals.
- **Off-policy training**: learning from stored past experience (a replay buffer) generated by an older
  policy, rather than only from fresh on-policy rollouts; far more sample-efficient.
- **Non-stationarity (in HRL)**: because the lower level keeps improving, a fixed high-level goal maps
  to different low-level behavior over time, so the higher level's effective transition dynamics drift,
  breaking naive off-policy learning.
- **Off-policy correction (goal relabeling)**: HIRO's fix; when training the higher level on a stored
  transition, replace the originally-issued goal with the goal that maximizes the likelihood of the
  low-level actions that were actually observed, so the stored data looks on-policy to the current
  lower level.

## Primary-source facts

- "Lower-level controllers are supervised with goals that are learned and proposed automatically by the
  higher-level controllers" (`main.tex` abstract); goals are (relative) states, not a hand-designed
  semantic space.
- Both levels trained off-policy; "changes to the lower-level behaviors change the action space for the
  higher-level policy, and we introduce an off-policy correction to remedy this challenge" (`main.tex`).
- Named HIRO; "generally applicable and highly sample-efficient"; learns complex simulated-robot
  behaviors (push an object and use it to reach a target) "from only a few million samples."
- Substantially outperforms prior state-of-the-art HRL methods in their comparison.

## Interpretation (labeled inference)

- HIRO is the precise reason Director (2206.04114) can boast "no off-policy correction is needed because
  the imagined rollouts are on-policy": Director sidesteps HIRO's central headache by training in
  imagination rather than off a replay buffer. Reading HIRO and Director together is the cleanest way to
  see *why* imagination-based hierarchical world models are attractive, the world model makes the
  higher level's data on-policy by construction.
- For this repo the load-bearing lesson is a *caution*, not a recipe: any two-level scheme where a
  higher level's "goal" is interpreted by a lower level that is itself changing has a built-in
  non-stationarity problem. The repo avoids this entirely because it does not *train* a hierarchy by RL;
  the LLM proposes and a deterministic runtime executes. But it is exactly why an advisory WAM should
  predict *typed verifiable deltas* (whose meaning is fixed by the verifier) rather than learned latent
  goals (whose meaning drifts).

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful**: the idea that a high-level target is a *state to be reached* and the low
  level is scored on reaching it maps to the repo's "predicted post-action state" vs "verified observed
  state." The relabeling insight is a reminder that goal meaning must be pinned to observable evidence,
  which in the repo is done by the verifier rather than by relabeling.
- **Research contribution (NOT to adopt)**: off-policy HRL with goal relabeling is an RL training
  technique; the repo neither trains policies off a buffer nor relabels goals. Cited as the
  non-stationarity cautionary tale and the goal-conditioned-HRL reference.

## Relevance to this repo (one line)

The canonical goal-conditioned HRL method and the clearest statement of HRL's non-stationarity problem;
it argues for pinning predicted-goal meaning to fixed verifier evidence rather than to drifting learned
goals, and explains why imagination-based hierarchies (Director) are cleaner.
