# DAgger - the canonical compounding-error result for sequential prediction

- **title**: A Reduction of Imitation Learning and Structured Prediction to No-Regret Online Learning
- **authors**: Stephane Ross, Geoffrey J. Gordon, J. Andrew Bagnell (Carnegie Mellon University)
- **year**: 2011
- **venue/source**: AISTATS 2011 (PMLR v15); arXiv
- **arxiv_id**: 1011.0686
- **urls**: paper https://arxiv.org/abs/1011.0686 ; PMLR https://proceedings.mlr.press/v15/ross11a.html ; LaTeX at `papers/latex/1011.0686/`
- **source availability**: latex (full, single-file `noregret.tex`, deep-read)

This is a foundational pre-WAM source. It is not a world model. It is the cleanest
proof of the phenomenon that makes long world-model rollouts drift, so the lane
cites it as the canonical reference for compounding error.

## Primary-source facts (what the paper states or proves)

Definitions used here (plain first):
- **Imitation learning**: train a policy (a classifier or regressor) to copy an
  expert's action given the observation. "Behavior cloning" is the plain
  supervised version.
- **Teacher forcing / training under the expert distribution**: the policy is
  trained only on states the expert visits. "Exposure bias" is the resulting
  mismatch: at test time the policy visits states it never trained on.
- **Compounding error**: once the policy makes one mistake, it lands in an
  unfamiliar state, which makes the next mistake more likely, and errors
  accumulate over the horizon.

The central result (the part the lane needs, verbatim spirit):
- "a classifier that makes a mistake with probability epsilon under the
  distribution of states/observations encountered by the expert can make as many
  as **T^2 epsilon mistakes** in expectation over T-steps under the distribution
  of states the classifier itself induces." (Section "INTRODUCTION", citing Ross
  2010.)
- Mechanism, verbatim: "as soon as the learner makes a mistake, it may encounter
  completely different observations than those under expert demonstration,
  leading to a **compounding of errors**."
- Formalized: traditional supervised imitation finds `pi_sup = argmin_pi
  E_{s ~ d_pi*}[loss]` (loss measured under the EXPERT's state distribution
  `d_pi*`), but the quantity that matters is the loss under the policy's OWN
  induced distribution `d_pi`. Theorem 1: with per-step surrogate loss epsilon
  under `d_pi*`, the cost satisfies `J(pi) <= J(pi*) + T^2 epsilon`. The bound is
  tight: there exist problems where the excess cost grows as Theta(T^2 epsilon)
  (Kaariainen 2006 sequence-prediction example; Ross 2010 imitation example).
- Why quadratic: T comes from the horizon length, and a second T comes from each
  early mistake shifting the entire downstream state distribution.

The fix the paper introduces (DAgger = Dataset Aggregation):
- Iterative algorithm. Iteration 1: collect trajectories under the expert, train
  a policy. Iteration n: run the CURRENT learned policy to collect new states,
  query the expert for the correct action on those states, ADD them to the
  aggregated dataset, retrain on the union. Optionally mix in the expert with a
  decaying probability `beta_i` (the parameter-free `beta_i = I(i=1)` version
  "often performs best").
- Interpreted as a Follow-The-Leader no-regret online-learning algorithm: at each
  step pick the best policy in hindsight over all states seen so far.
- Guarantee (Theorem, infinite-sample): for N = O~(T) iterations there is a policy
  in the sequence with loss `<= epsilon_N + O(1/T)` under its own induced
  distribution. In words: training the model on the states IT actually visits
  reduces the error growth from quadratic (T^2 epsilon) to **linear in T**.
- Demonstrated on Super Tux Kart steering, Super Mario Bros., and OCR sequence
  labeling, outperforming supervised cloning, forward-training, and SMILe.

## Interpretation (my reading, labeled inference)

- DAgger is about imitation policies, but the result is modality-agnostic and is
  exactly the failure mode of autoregressive world-model rollouts. A video or
  latent world model is typically trained by **teacher forcing**: predict frame
  `t+1` given GROUND-TRUTH frames up to `t`. At inference it is rolled out
  **autoregressively**: it must consume its OWN predicted frames. That is the same
  train-test distribution mismatch (exposure bias) DAgger formalizes, so prediction
  error compounds and the rollout drifts. The lane uses DAgger as the
  imitation-learning name for the same phenomenon the WAM survey calls
  "distributional drift in world model predictions accumulates over long rollouts"
  (survey `070-oppo.tex`).
- The DAgger remedy (train on the learner's own induced distribution) is the
  conceptual ancestor of the engineering trick DreamZero uses at inference:
  replace predicted frames with ground-truth observations in the KV cache "to stop
  error accumulation" (see `2602.15922-dreamzero.md`). Both attack the same
  distribution-shift problem from different ends (training-time data aggregation
  vs inference-time ground-truth re-grounding).

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful for this repo**: the result tells the repo NOT to evaluate
  an advisory predictor only on single-step accuracy under logged (on-policy)
  states, because multi-step error is where drift hides. If the repo ever trains a
  small transition model on its own runtime logs (Option E in
  `data-and-training-feasibility.md`), the DAgger lesson is to keep retraining on
  states the predictor's own use induces, not only on states the live runs already
  visited. The repo's structural advantage softens this: short, gated,
  multi-second Actor Turns with the runtime re-grounding state every turn mean the
  repo never runs an ungrounded long autoregressive rollout, so it largely sidesteps
  the regime where T^2 bites.
- **What would be overclaim**: DAgger is a theorem about imitation cost bounds; do
  not cite its specific T^2 number as if it were measured on a social-material
  predictor. It is the canonical *reference* for the phenomenon, not evidence about
  this repo's models.
