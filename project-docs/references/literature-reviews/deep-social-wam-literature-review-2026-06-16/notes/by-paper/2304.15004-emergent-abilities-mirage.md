# Are Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models a Mirage?

- title: Are Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models a Mirage?
- authors: Rylan Schaeffer, Brando Miranda, Sanmi Koyejo (Stanford University)
- venue/year: NeurIPS 2023 (outstanding paper award)
- arxiv_id: 2304.15004
- urls: paper https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.15004 ; hf https://huggingface.co/papers/2304.15004
- source read: LaTeX (papers/latex/2304.15004/): 00_main.tex (abstract), 02_analytical_model.tex
  (the metric-choice mechanism); 03_gpt.tex / 04_emergent_paper.tex at section level.
- reproducibility status: self-reported and well-cited (a fixed-outputs re-analysis of public
  InstructGPT/GPT-3 and BIG-Bench results plus a constructed vision demonstration). Not re-run here.

## Primary-source facts

- Claim under examination: "emergent abilities" of LLMs, abilities absent in small models that appear
  in large ones, interesting because they look SHARP (instant on) and UNPREDICTABLE (at unforeseeable
  scales).
- Alternative explanation (the paper's thesis): for a fixed set of model outputs, "emergent abilities
  appear due to the researcher's choice of metric rather than due to fundamental changes in model
  behavior with scale." NONLINEAR or DISCONTINUOUS metrics manufacture apparent emergence; LINEAR or
  CONTINUOUS metrics show smooth, predictable improvement.
- The mechanism (analytical model): suppose per-token cross-entropy falls smoothly with scale (a
  scaling law), so the per-token probability of a correct token, exp(-L_CE(N)), rises smoothly toward
  1. Then:
  - A metric requiring ALL L tokens correct (Accuracy on L-token output) scales as
    p(single token correct)^L, which bends the smooth per-token curve into a sharp, late-rising,
    "emergent"-looking curve.
  - A DISCONTINUOUS metric (Multiple Choice Grade, a step function) likewise produces apparent
    emergence.
  - Switching to a LINEAR metric (Token Edit Distance) reveals smooth, continuous, predictable
    improvement, ablating the "emergence."
  - Switching the discontinuous metric to a CONTINUOUS proper score (BRIER SCORE) again removes the
    emergent ability and shows smooth improvement.
- Three confounds named for apparent emergence: (1) a nonlinear/discontinuous metric, (2) too little
  resolution to estimate small-model performance (resolution set by 1/test-set-size), (3)
  under-sampling the large-model regime.
- Validation (three ways, per the abstract): (1) make/test/confirm three predictions about metric
  choice on the InstructGPT/GPT-3 family on tasks with claimed emergence; (2) a BIG-Bench
  meta-analysis confirming two metric-choice predictions; (3) construct never-before-seen "emergent"
  abilities in vision models by choosing the metric. Conclusion: "alleged emergent abilities evaporate
  with different metrics or with better statistics."

## Interpretation (my inference, labeled)

- The load-bearing point for the lane is the explicit appearance of the BRIER SCORE as the
  continuous, well-behaved alternative that makes apparent emergence vanish. The Brier score is a
  PROPER SCORING RULE (it is minimized in expectation by the true probability), and proper scoring
  rules / continuous metrics are exactly what the repo should use to track a probabilistic predictor's
  progress, instead of a thresholded "got the delta exactly right" accuracy that can move sharply and
  mislead. This is the metric-design argument the repo's predictor evaluation needs, stated and
  demonstrated.
- The general warning is the same family as [[2108.13264-rliable-statistical-precipice]] and
  notes/by-theme/benchmark-validity-and-evaluation.md: the choice of measurement can manufacture an
  effect (here, a capability; there, a "state-of-the-art" ranking). For the repo, a thresholded social
  metric ("predicted the trust enum exactly") could show a jump that a continuous score (Brier / log
  loss over the predicted distribution, or edit distance over the typed delta) would reveal as smooth.
  The repo should prefer continuous, proper-score reporting and treat any sharp jump in a thresholded
  social metric as a metric artifact until shown otherwise.
- A second-order caution: the paper does not say capability never changes with scale; it says
  observed SHARPNESS/UNPREDICTABILITY is often a metric artifact. So the repo should also not
  over-read smoothness as "nothing is happening." The honest move is: report the continuous proper
  score AND the thresholded task metric, and explain divergences.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Mechanically useful:
  - Concrete metric guidance: track a probabilistic predictor with a proper scoring rule (Brier, log
    loss) or a continuous distance over the typed delta, not only with a brittle exact-match accuracy.
  - A diagnostic: if a social/predictor metric shows a sharp jump, re-plot under a continuous metric
    and check resolution / sampling before claiming a real capability change.
- Research contribution (not the repo's to claim): the metric-choice explanation of emergence and its
  three-pronged validation are the paper's contribution on LLM scaling. The repo borrows the
  metric-design lesson; it does not study emergence. Its surface is applying continuous proper scores
  to verifier-grounded social-material delta prediction.
