## Primary-source facts (what the paper states/shows)

- **Research question**: in a vast (even infinite) task space, even after filtering for *learnable* tasks (those at the agent's frontier), countless learnable-but-*uninteresting* tasks remain (minor variations of solved tasks). How do you prioritize tasks that are both learnable AND interesting, without a hand-crafted interestingness formula?
- **Core proposal**: use a foundation model (FM) as a **Model of Interestingness (MoI)**. FMs trained on human-generated text have internalized human notions of what is interesting vs boring; prompt the FM few-shot to label which candidate tasks are interesting given what the agent already does well. OMNI = a learning-progress (LP) curriculum *plus* an FM MoI filter.
- **Learning-progress curriculum** (the "learnable" half, extending Kanitscheider et al.): sample tasks by *bidirectional* learning progress = absolute change in task success probability (a fast EMA `p_recent` vs a slow EMA `p_gradual`), reweighted to magnify progress on low-success (novel) tasks and to also catch forgetting. Normalized against a random-policy success rate to remove a bias against tasks with chance-high initial success.
- **MoI filter** (the "interesting" half): the FM predicts which high-LP tasks are interesting; "boring" tasks have their sampling weight multiplied by 0.001. Tasks are then renormalized to a probability distribution.
- **Environments**: Crafter (a 2-D version of Minecraft, with a technology tree of prerequisite tasks), BabyAI (2-D grounded-language gridworld), AI2-THOR (3-D embodied robotics; an *infinite* task space where the FM also *generates* tasks in natural language, then code is translated to natural language and back). The Crafter setup deliberately dilutes 15 interesting tasks with 90 "boring" tasks (numerical repeats, e.g. "collect N wood" for N in [2,10]) and 1023 "extremely challenging" distractor tasks (always success-rate 0).
- **Metrics**: average task success rate, and number of tasks with success rate above alpha = 0.2. RL with PPO. 100M steps, 10 seeds.
- **Results**: OMNI (LP + MoI) beats uniform sampling and LP-only on both metrics, *even averaged over all tasks*, despite biasing training toward the interesting subset. LP-only is distracted by the boring numerical-variation tasks; uniform wastes budget on impossible tasks.
- **Stated caution (Goodhart)**: the paper is explicit that hand-crafted novelty/diversity/intrinsic-motivation metrics get gamed: "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure"; an agent will generate "superficially different but ultimately trivial" solutions to satisfy a novelty metric. This is *why* they delegate interestingness to an FM rather than a formula.

## Interpretation (flagged as inference)

- OMNI cleanly separates two filters my lane must keep distinct: **learnability** (frontier of competence; can be grounded in measured success, hence verifier-friendly) and **interestingness** (worthwhile, novel; FM-judged, NOT verifier-grounded). For the wave-4 thesis this is the dividing line: the runtime verifier can supply the learnability signal (did the actor solve it? at what rate?), but interestingness in a social world is an FM/human judgment that the verifier cannot confer.
- Crafter being "a 2-D version of Minecraft" makes OMNI the most direct precedent for an interestingness-filtered task curriculum in (proto-)Minecraft. The "boring = numerical repeats" construction is exactly the failure mode of a naive social-scenario generator: "borrow 1 plank, borrow 2 planks, borrow 3 planks" are not 3 interesting social scenarios.
- The Goodhart caution is the strongest warning OMNI gives the repo: any single hand-coded "social interestingness" score (e.g. count of obligations created) will be gamed; an FM MoI is more robust but is itself an LLM judgment, so it cannot be the success oracle, only a *proposer/filter*.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful**: (1) the **two-filter pipeline** (LP for learnability + FM MoI for interestingness) is directly adoptable for generating social-material scenarios; the repo already emits per-actor success signals, so the LP half is cheap and verifier-grounded. (2) The **bidirectional-LP-with-forgetting** sampler is a concrete, well-tested learnability scorer. (3) The **boring-task construction** is a ready-made negative test: a good social-scenario generator should NOT spend budget on numerical variations of a solved scenario.
- **Not a contribution to copy / overclaim risk**: OMNI's MoI is an LLM judging interestingness; it must stay a *proposer/filter*, never the success label (or the loop launders progress). The repo must keep the verifier as the only authority on whether a scenario was solved. OMNI's domains (Crafter, BabyAI, AI2-THOR) have programmatic success detection; the repo's advantage is its embodied runtime verifier, which is stronger than OMNI's per-task success oracle.
- **Limitation (theirs + spotted)**: interestingness is FM-defined ("I know it when I see it"), so it is contestable and provider-dependent; the AI2-THOR infinite-space results rely on the FM both generating and judging, which risks self-confirmation; success detection is environment-supplied, not learned. None of OMNI's tasks score durable social-material consequence (possession, obligation, weak-commons) - they are competence/skill tasks.
