## Primary-source facts (what the paper states)

- Problem: MARL among self-interested agents produces sub-optimal behavior when
  individual and group incentives diverge (social dilemmas). Replicating human-style
  cooperation in selfish agents is an open MARL problem.
- Mechanism: augment a Markov game with **formal contracts**, defined as voluntary
  commitments to zero-sum, state-action-dependent transfers of reward between agents,
  contingent on **contractible observations**. Properties of the augmentation:
  - Contracts are proposed by an agent and can be vetoed by any single agent
    (participation is voluntary).
  - On acceptance, agents may still take any action; only their rewards are modified
    by the agreed transfer. The contract binds the *payment*, not the action.
- Theory (stated results):
  - With a sufficiently rich contract space and full observability, **every
    subgame-perfect equilibrium (SPE) of every Fully Observable Markov Game becomes
    socially optimal** under the contracting augmentation.
  - For general contract spaces, and even under partial observability, **richer
    contract spaces yield higher welfare** (sum of rewards). Hence "contract space
    design solves an exploration-exploitation tradeoff", sidestepping the incentive
    problem.
  - Worked Prisoner's Dilemma: a contract transferring 1.5 reward from a defector to
    a cooperator makes (C, C) the equilibrium; selfish agents accept it because the
    post-contract game has a (C, C) Nash equilibrium (reward -1 each) preferred to
    the un-contracted (D, D) (reward -2 each).
  - The SPE refinement (over plain Nash) is needed to rule out non-credible threats
    (e.g. "never clean", which would not be a best response once a cleaning contract
    is active).
- Method: Multi-Objective Contract Augmentation Learning (MOCA) handles the
  exploration challenge of learning subgame-perfect behavior, inspired by
  multi-objective RL.
- Experiments: static single-move matrix games plus dynamic domains simulating
  traffic, pollution management, and common-pool-resource management, including
  Cleanup and Harvest (the same SSD substrates used elsewhere in this review).
  Contracting "performs close to, or better than, a joint controller" after a fixed
  number of time periods. (Numbers are per-domain in the experiments section; the
  load-bearing claim is the qualitative match-or-beat-joint-controller result and
  the monotonic welfare-in-contract-richness result.)
- Conclusion discusses real-world enforcement of contracts and fairness concerns as
  open issues.

## Interpretation (my reading, labeled inference)

- This is the formal bridge between "contract" (a mechanism-design object) and the
  "social dilemma" (the Material/Institutional-layer failure mode the repo cares
  about). It proves the strongest version of the lane's central claim: a rich enough
  space of conditional, evidence-contingent reward transfers can make selfish agents
  cooperate optimally, in equilibrium, on the exact Cleanup/Harvest commons the repo
  is conceptually adjacent to.
- The load-bearing dependency for the repo is "**contractible observations**": the
  theory works only when the contract can condition on observations that detect
  deviation from the socially optimal profile. That is precisely what the repo's
  verifier produces, world-anchored, evidence-backed observations of who did what.
  The repo's `MaterialClaimLedgerEntry` / `ObligationLedgerEntry` / weak-commons
  events are exactly the contractible-observation substrate this theory presupposes
  and that abstract MARL has to hand-define.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Mechanically useful:
  - The contracting-augmentation schema maps onto the repo's obligation/credit
    ledger: a proposed contract = a proposed `ObligationLedgerEntry` with a trigger
    condition; acceptance/veto = the request_accepted / refusal events; fulfillment =
    a verified delta that satisfies the trigger; the "transfer" is the credit-slip
    bookkeeping (Blau's exchange, already in the sociology theme).
  - "Welfare rises with contract richness" gives a measurable Institutional-layer
    prediction: as the set of contractible (verifiable) observations grows, the
    achievable cooperative outcome should improve, a hypothesis the verifier can test.
- Research contribution (where the repo diverges and could add):
  - In this paper the transfer is to the RL reward signal and the augmentation
    enforces the payment mechanically. The repo's hard rule forbids the runtime from
    enforcing informal commitments or rewriting outcomes (observe-only Institutional
    layer). The repo's contribution is to *predict and score* whether a voluntary
    commitment was kept against verifier evidence, not to enforce a reward transfer.
    So borrow the contractible-observation theory and the contract-as-obligation
    schema; do not adopt reward-transfer enforcement.
  - The paper's "good" is an abstract reward scalar; the repo grounds the transferred
    value in a physical item/affordance with location and durability.

## WAM layer(s) informed

- **Material / Economic WAM** (obligation/credit transfers contingent on verified
  resource events) and **Institutional / Settlement WAM** (contracts as a mechanism
  that selects cooperative equilibria in a commons).

## Reproducibility

- Self-reported (paper-level). Theory is proved; experiments use standard deep MARL
  (PPO-class) on Sequential Social Dilemma substrates (Cleanup/Harvest) that are
  reproducible in principle. The specific trained policies are not a drop-in for an
  LLM-turn runtime; the transferable content is the theorem and the contracting
  schema, not the weights.
