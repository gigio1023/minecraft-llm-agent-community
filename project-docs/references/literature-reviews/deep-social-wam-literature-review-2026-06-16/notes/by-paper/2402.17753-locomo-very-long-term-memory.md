## Primary-source facts (what the paper states/shows)

- Gap: prior long-term dialogue work evaluates within at most ~5 sessions; very-long-term memory (weeks/months) was unexplored even with long-context LLMs and RAG.
- Dataset (LoCoMo): generated by a machine-human pipeline. Each LLM agent gets a distinct persona and a temporal event graph G (a sequence of causally connected dated life events). Agents converse over up to 35 sessions, conditioning each response on short-term + long-term memory, reflections over retrieved observations, the current session, the persona, and the subset of events in G that fall between the last and current session. Agents can share and react to images (multimodal). Human annotators then edit for long-range consistency and event-graph grounding (annotators edited ~15% of dialog turns and removed/substituted ~19% of images).
- Scale: each conversation averages 300 turns and 9K tokens, over up to 35 sessions.
- Benchmark tasks: question answering (single-hop, multi-hop, temporal, open-domain, adversarial categories), event summarization, multimodal dialogue generation.
- Numbers as stated:
  - QA: best model GPT-4-turbo scores 32.4 overall F1, far behind the human benchmark of 87.9.
  - Long-context hallucination: GPT-3.5-turbo-16k outperforms others overall but its accuracy on adversarial questions collapses to 2.1% (vs 22.1% for Llama-2-Chat and 70.2% for GPT-4-turbo at 4K context). Long contexts make models easy to mislead into hallucination.
  - RAG over observations: ~5% improvement when input is the top-5 relevant observations instead of raw conversation logs, but the gain falters as more observations are retrieved (signal-to-noise matters). RAG over session summaries does not help much despite high recall, because information is lost when dialogs are converted to summaries.
  - Event summarization: long-context model does NOT beat the 4K base model (GPT-3.5-turbo-16k drops 3.0% precision, 8.7% recall vs GPT-3.5-turbo 4K), i.e. long-context models do not use their context well. Summary errors fall into five categories: missing information (failed temporal/causal connection), hallucination, misunderstanding dialog cues (humor/sarcasm), inaccurate speaker attribution, salience errors.

## Reproducibility status

- Self-reported, public dataset and code. Standard automatic metrics (F1, ROUGE, FactScore) plus a stated human benchmark. The human-edited event-graph grounding is a notable quality control. Not environment-verified by me; numbers quoted from the paper.

## Interpretation (my inference, labeled)

- LoCoMo is the canonical "how do we evaluate very-long-term memory, and why is it hard" benchmark for the lane. Its central empirical message is directly relevant to the repo's continuity claim: the gap to human is enormous (32.4 vs 87.9), long context alone makes things worse (hallucination), and summarization loses decisive information. This is independent evidence for the same write-before-query failure TierMem (2602.17913) formalizes.
- The "summaries lose information" and "speaker attribution / temporal-causal errors" findings are exactly the failure modes the repo's evidence-linking rule targets: a distilled social fact that drops its source cannot be re-verified, and attribution errors (who did what) are catastrophic for a relationship ledger.
- The event-graph grounding is the most transferable design idea: LoCoMo grounds dialogues on a temporal event graph and has humans verify alignment. The repo's analog is far stronger, ground every memory on the deterministic verifier's (state, action, next-state) record rather than on a human-checked synthetic event graph, so grounding is automatic and near-$0, not a human annotation pass.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Mechanically useful as a cautionary benchmark and an evaluation template: test recall over very long horizons with adversarial and temporal categories; measure information loss through summarization explicitly; report the human gap. The signal-to-noise finding (too many retrieved observations hurt) is a concrete retrieval-tuning lesson for the actor's recall step.
- Research contribution it is NOT for this repo: LoCoMo is a text-dialogue memory benchmark with no physical/material/social typed state and no consequence verification; its grounding is a synthetic event graph, not an executed world. It tells the repo what to measure (very-long-horizon recall, information loss) and warns that text-only long-term memory is far from solved; it is not an instance of verifier-grounded social memory.

## WAM layer(s) informed

- Evaluation of cross-cycle continuity (the measurement side). Most relevant to Social/Institutional layers, where attribution and temporal-causal connection over many sessions are exactly what LoCoMo shows current systems fail at, motivating the repo's verifier-grounded alternative.
