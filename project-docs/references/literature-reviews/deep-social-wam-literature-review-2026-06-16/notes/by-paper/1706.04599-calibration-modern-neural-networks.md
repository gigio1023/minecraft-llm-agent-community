# On Calibration of Modern Neural Networks

- title: On Calibration of Modern Neural Networks
- authors: Chuan Guo, Geoff Pleiss, Yu Sun, Kilian Q. Weinberger (Cornell University)
- venue/year: ICML 2017
- arxiv_id: 1706.04599
- urls: paper https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.04599 ; hf https://huggingface.co/papers/1706.04599
- source read: LaTeX in full (papers/latex/1706.04599/main.tex, tex/introduction.tex, tex/definitions.tex)
- reproducibility status: self-reported (extensive empirical study across image and document classifiers; widely independently reproduced since 2017, but not re-run in this archive)

## Primary-source facts

- Problem: confidence calibration. A model output h(X) = (Yhat, Phat) where Phat is the predicted
  probability of correctness. "Perfect calibration" is defined formally as
  P(Yhat = Y | Phat = p) = p for all p in [0,1] (eq. perfect_calibration). In words: of all
  predictions made with confidence 0.8, exactly 80 percent should be correct.
- Central empirical finding (verbatim spirit): "modern neural networks are no longer well-calibrated."
  In 2005 (Niculescu-Mizil and Caruana) neural nets produced well-calibrated probabilities; modern
  deep nets are systematically OVERCONFIDENT. The paper's figure compares a 5-layer LeNet
  (well-calibrated, confidence tracks accuracy) against a 110-layer ResNet on CIFAR-100 (average
  confidence substantially higher than accuracy).
- The architectural factors that worsen calibration (measured by ECE): increased depth, increased
  width, and removing weight decay all increase miscalibration; Batch Normalization also affects it.
  Accuracy and calibration move in OPPOSITE directions as networks scale up. Mechanism named:
  networks overfit to NLL (cross-entropy) after they have stopped overfitting to 0/1 error, driving
  confidence up without improving accuracy.
- Calibration metrics defined (this is the load-bearing part for the lane):
  - Reliability diagram: plot expected accuracy as a function of confidence; a perfectly calibrated
    model lies on the identity diagonal. Bins predictions into M equal-width bins.
  - Expected Calibration Error (ECE) = sum over M bins of (|B_m|/n) * |acc(B_m) - conf(B_m)|. A
    weighted average of the per-bin gap between accuracy and confidence. Primary metric used.
  - Maximum Calibration Error (MCE) = max over bins of |acc(B_m) - conf(B_m)|. Worst-case bin gap,
    for high-risk settings.
  - Negative Log Likelihood (NLL) = -sum_i log(pihat(y_i | x_i)); standard probabilistic-quality
    measure, minimized in expectation iff pihat recovers the true conditional pi(Y|X).
- Fix: temperature scaling, a single-parameter variant of Platt scaling. Divide the logits by one
  learned scalar T (tuned on a held-out validation set to minimize NLL) before the softmax. It does
  not change the argmax (so accuracy is unchanged) but recalibrates the confidences. Finding:
  "surprisingly effective" and "often the most effective method" across most datasets, while being
  trivial to implement.

## Interpretation (my inference, labeled)

- This is the canonical reference for the gap between a model's confidence and its correctness, and
  for the two facts the repo's predictor evaluation rests on: (1) a high-accuracy model can be badly
  miscalibrated, and (2) calibration is a SEPARATE axis that must be measured (ECE / reliability
  diagram), not assumed. The repo's advisory social-material World Action Model (WAM) emits, in
  effect, a probability or a graded belief over the next typed delta; whether "I am 0.8 sure the
  pickaxe will be returned" means a 0.8 empirical return rate is exactly the perfect-calibration
  question, measured by binning predicted-confidence vs verifier-observed outcome.
- The "overconfidence grows with scale / capability" finding rhymes with the lane-wide
  "more capability != more fidelity" pattern (SimBench, S3AP, MineExplorer): a bigger LLM used as
  the predictor is not automatically better calibrated, and may be worse. This is a caution against
  trusting a strong model's stated confidence without an ECE measurement against the verifier.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Mechanically useful:
  - ECE and the reliability diagram are directly importable as the repo's calibration report for an
    advisory predictor: bin the predictor's stated confidence in a delta, plot against the fraction
    of those predictions the verifier later confirmed, summarize as ECE.
  - Temperature scaling is a near-free post-hoc recalibration step the repo could apply to a
    predictor's probabilities using a held-out scenario set, with no change to its point prediction.
- Research contribution (not the repo's to claim): the empirical discovery that depth/width/weight
  decay drive miscalibration is a 2017 vision/NLP-classifier result. The repo does not re-derive it;
  it borrows the metric and the warning, and applies them to a new object (verifier-labeled social
  deltas), which is where the novelty is.

## WAM layer(s) informed

- Cross-cutting (measurement). Applies to any layer where the predictor emits a probability over a
  typed next-state delta: Physical (block/inventory delta), Material (possession/obligation delta),
  Social (trust/relationship enum move). It says nothing about WHAT to predict, only how to score
  whether the stated confidence is honest. Companion: [[2107.07511-conformal-prediction-tutorial]]
  (distribution-free coverage as an alternative to a point calibration number).
