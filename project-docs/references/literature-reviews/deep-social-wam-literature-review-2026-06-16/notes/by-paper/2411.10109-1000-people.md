# Generative Agent Simulations of 1,000 People

- title: Generative Agent Simulations of 1,000 People
- authors: Joon Sung Park, Carolyn Q. Zou, Aaron Shaw, Benjamin Mako Hill, Carrie Cai, Meredith Ringel Morris, Robb Willer, Percy Liang, Michael S. Bernstein
- year: 2024
- venue: arXiv (Stanford / Northwestern / DeepMind)
- arxiv_id: 2411.10109
- urls: paper https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.10109 ; hf https://huggingface.co/papers/2411.10109
- source: arXiv e-print source was a PDF (papers/pdf/2411.10109.pdf); facts below from abstract + web-confirmed numbers. Flagged: PDF/abstract-level, not LaTeX.

## Primary-source facts

- Research question: can generative agents replicate the attitudes and behaviors of *specific real individuals*?
- Method: build agents for **1,052 real individuals** by applying an LLM to a 2-hour qualitative interview about each person's life; then measure how well each agent reproduces that person's responses.
- Findings:
  - Agents replicate participants' **General Social Survey (GSS)** responses **85% as accurately as the participants replicate their own answers two weeks later** (the human test-retest reliability ceiling is the normalizer).
  - Comparable performance predicting Big Five personality and replicating experimental-economics outcomes.
  - Interview-grounded agents **reduce accuracy bias across racial and ideological groups** vs agents given only demographic descriptions.

## Interpretation (my inference, labeled)

- This is the strongest existing *individual-fidelity* result, and it sets a concrete, sober ceiling: even the best individual simulators reach ~85% of human self-consistency, and only on static survey/personality items - not on interactive, embodied, multi-step behavior. It directly supports the lane thesis: matching a person's questionnaire answers (plausible self-report) is a *different and easier* claim than reproducing what they would *do* in an interactive world.
- The "interview-grounded beats demographic-stereotype" finding echoes Concordia's algorithmic-fidelity caution (LLMs default to stereotypes of groups) and shows richer grounding helps - but the result is still self-report prediction, not verified consequence.
- For the repo: this paper is a useful citation for "why we do NOT claim human fidelity." The repo has no per-actor real-human ground truth and operates in interactive embodied settings, so it should claim grounded *behavioral* trajectories, not individual human replication. 1000 People marks the frontier of the claim the repo is deliberately not making.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Mechanically useful: the "normalize against human test-retest reliability" idea is a rigorous way to express any fidelity claim (compare model-to-human against human-to-self, not against an absolute). If the repo ever compares traces to human play, use this normalization.
- Avoid / overclaim: do not import individual-human-replication as a goal; it requires real-human data the repo doesn't have and is a self-report (non-interactive) claim.
