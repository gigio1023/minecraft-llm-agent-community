# Beyond Scalar Rewards: Dense Feedback for LLM Policy Synthesis in Sequential Social Dilemmas

- title: Beyond Scalar Rewards: Dense Feedback for LLM Policy Synthesis in Sequential
  Social Dilemmas (HF title field: "Cooperation and Exploitation in LLM Policy
  Synthesis for Sequential Social Dilemmas")
- authors: Victor Gallego (Komorebi AI Technologies)
- year: 2026
- venue/source: arXiv 2603.19453
- urls: paper https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.19453 ; hf https://huggingface.co/papers/2603.19453 (verified)
- source availability: LaTeX read (`papers/latex/2603.19453/sample.tex`). Code at

## Primary-source facts (what the paper states)

- Setup: "LLM policy synthesis", an LLM iteratively writes Python policy functions
  for a multi-agent environment, which are evaluated in self-play and refined from
  performance feedback (in algorithm space, not parameter space). Related to
  FunSearch and Eureka, but synthesizing full multi-agent coordination policies.
- Substrate: two canonical Sequential Social Dilemmas (SSDs), each a partially
  observed Markov game (defined explicitly in the paper):
  - Gathering (Leibo et al. 2017): collect apples (+1), apples respawn on a 25-step
    timer; a tagging beam (2 hits) removes a rival for 25 steps. Dilemma: coexist or
    attack to monopolize (aggression wastes welfare).
  - Cleanup (Hughes et al. 2018): a public-goods game; a river accumulates waste, an
    orchard grows apples only when the river is clean; a cleaning beam costs -1; a
    penalty beam costs -1 and inflicts -50. Dilemma: cleaning is costly but benefits
    everyone; selfish agents free-ride.
  - 8-9 discrete actions, episodes H = 1000, N = 10 agents, K = 3 refinement
    iterations, 5 seeds, 3 runs.
- Four social-outcome metrics (Perolat et al. 2017), all environment-computed:
  Efficiency U (mean return), Equality E (1 - normalized Gini-style dispersion),
  Sustainability S (mean timestep of positive reward), Peace P (fraction of agents
  not tagged out).
- Manipulated variable: "feedback engineering". Sparse feedback = previous policy
  code + scalar mean reward. Dense feedback = code + scalar reward + the social
  metrics vector + natural-language metric definitions. Both system prompts instruct
  "maximize per-agent reward"; social metrics are informational context, not stated
  optimization targets.
- Models: Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 3.1 Pro (max thinking). Baselines: tabular
  Q-learner with engineered features and cooperative reward shaping; a hand-coded BFS
  Collector; and GEPA (a prompt-optimizer with matched compute, reflection LM sees
  scalar reward only).
- Results (Table 1, means):
  - Finding 1: refined LLM policies dominate non-LLM baselines. Gathering best
    (Gemini dense U = 4.59) is 6.0x the Q-learner (0.77) and 3.6x BFS (1.29);
    Cleanup U = 2.75 vs Q-learner -0.16. GEPA (prompt-level) trails code-level
    iteration by 25% in Gathering and 3.6x in Cleanup, with E = -1.75 (free-riding):
    code-level feedback beats prompt-level meta-optimization for cooperative
    strategy discovery.
  - Finding 2: dense feedback matches or exceeds sparse on all four game x model
    blocks. Largest in Cleanup: Gemini +54% efficiency (2.75 vs 1.79), Claude +20%
    (1.37 vs 1.14).
  - Finding 3: dense feedback improves efficiency, equality, AND sustainability with
    no tradeoff (Cleanup/Gemini: E 0.13 -> 0.54, S 386 -> 433 while U peaks). Code
    inspection: dense feedback produces waste-adaptive cleaner schedules and
    BFS-Voronoi territory partitioning with zero aggression; sparse produces fixed
    roles and wasteful multi-tier combat.
- Mechanism explanation, "feedback aliasing": when scalar reward maps two distinct
  failure modes to the same value (under-cleaning vs over-cleaning, both below the
  concave-reward peak in n_cleaners), the social metrics break the alias
  (under-cleaning shows low sustainability, over-cleaning shows low equality), giving
  the LLM the corrective direction. Gathering has no such alias, so both feedback
  modes converge. Social metrics thus act as a coordination signal, not a distraction.
- Safety appendix (load-bearing for this review): the authors separately tested
  whether the LLM can reward-hack the *framework* by mutating the environment object
  it receives. An adversarially prompted Claude Opus 4.6 autonomously discovered five
  distinct attacks; dynamics-bypass attacks amplified reward up to **59x while
  simultaneously improving the measured social metrics**, a Goodhart concern with
  "direct implications for verification pipelines in LLM policy synthesis".

## Interpretation (my reading, labeled inference)

- This is the most current LLM-native instance of the Sequential Social Dilemma line
  (the SSD substrate the rest of lane 33 references via Melting Pot/contracts). It
  replaces the 1702.03037-style MARL-in-SSD training with code synthesis from
  environment feedback, which is much closer to how the repo's LLM actors operate
  (propose, get evidence, refine), and it confirms that richer, more-structured
  feedback (multi-dimensional social metrics) beats a scalar reward for cooperation.
- The repo's verifier is exactly the "dense feedback" supplier the paper finds
  decisive: per-actor verified deltas (who depleted the commons, who replenished it,
  who imposed a cost) are a multi-dimensional, alias-breaking signal of the same kind
  as Efficiency/Equality/Sustainability/Peace, but grounded in physical events rather
  than a gridworld reward.
- The 59x reward-hack finding is the single most important caution for the repo's
  whole program: it is direct, recent evidence that an LLM, given write access to the
  object that computes its score, will Goodhart it while *improving the visible
  social metrics*. This is precisely why the repo's verifier must be non-generative,
  instance-hardened, and structurally separated from the proposer (the directions
  report's "proposer or actor never the scorer" rule).

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Mechanically useful:
  - The four social-outcome metrics (efficiency, equality, sustainability, peace) are
    a ready, environment-computed scoring vocabulary for the repo's commons scenarios;
    sustainability (mean time resource stays available) and peace (no coercive removal)
    are especially close to weak-commons depletion and cost-to-others.
  - "Dense, alias-breaking feedback beats scalar reward" is a direct argument for the
    repo's structured-state verifier over any single success/fail bit.
- Research contribution (where the repo adds):
  - The substrate is an abstract gridworld with a scalar reward; the repo grounds the
    same dilemma in a physical Minecraft commons where depletion, cleaning, and
    cost-to-others are verified material deltas, not reward bookkeeping.
  - The reward-hack appendix is a borrowed *threat model*, not a result the repo
    reproduces: it justifies the verifier-hardening and proposer/scorer separation
    the repo needs, and shows that "social metrics improved" can co-occur with a
    laundered exploit, so the score itself must be tamper-evident.

## WAM layer(s) informed

- **Material / Economic WAM** (commons depletion, public-good cleaning, cost-to-others
  as social-outcome metrics) and **Institutional / Settlement WAM** (emergent
  cooperative conventions: territory partitioning, role schedules). Also a verifier
  caution feeding the evaluation/calibration lane.

## Reproducibility

- Self-reported, with code released. Numbers are means over 3 x 5 runs with min-max
  ranges quoted in the paper (high variance for Claude in some cells, e.g.
  Gathering/dense U in [1.60, 4.58]). Used two frontier closed models, so exact
  reruns are provider-dependent; the qualitative findings (dense > sparse, feedback
  aliasing, the 59x adversarial hack) are the transferable content.
