# Benchmarking World-Model Learning (WorldTest / AutumnBench)

- **title**: Benchmarking World-Model Learning (protocol: WorldTest; suite: AutumnBench)
- **authors**: Archana Warrier, Thanh Dat Nguyen, Michelangelo Naim, Moksh Jain, Yichao Liang, Karen Schroeder, Cambridge Yang, Joshua B. Tenenbaum, Sebastian Vollmer, Kevin Ellis, Zenna Tavares (Basis Research Institute, DFKI, MIT, Cornell)
- **year**: 2025 (ICML 2026 accepted)
- **venue/source**: arXiv preprint / ICML 2026
- **arxiv_id**: 2510.19788
- **urls**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.19788
- **source availability**: latex (tarball extracted, `papers/latex/2510.19788/`)

## Primary-source facts (what the paper states)

- Argument: current world-model evaluation is anchored to next-frame prediction and to reward maximization in the SAME environment; this tests only what is measurable from observed interactions, not whether the agent learned dynamics that support many tasks.
- WorldTest protocol: separate a **reward-free interaction (exploration) phase** from a **scored test phase in a DIFFERENT but related environment**. It is open-ended (the agent must support tasks unknown ahead of time) and representation-agnostic (scored by behavior, no required output format).
- AutumnBench instantiation: 43 interactive 2D grid-world environments (17 stochastic, 26 deterministic), 129 tasks, across THREE task families:
    1. **Masked-Frame Prediction (MFP)**: reconstruct masked regions of an observation (multiple choice).
    2. **Change Detection (CD)**: detect WHEN the environment's dynamics changed (earliest divergence timestep; partial credit for late detection).
    3. **Planning**: produce an action sequence to reach a target configuration without reset.
- Comparison: 517 human participants (Prolific) vs FIVE frontier reasoning models (Claude 4 Sonnet, Gemini 2.5 Pro, Gemini 2.5 Flash, o3, Qwen3-235b-thinking). Humans aggregate ~90.2%; best model (o3) ~27.5%; all five fail across environments and task types (a post-hoc Claude Opus 4.6 reached ~39.9%, still far below humans).
- Scaling: more compute helps in only ~25 of 43 environments; ~42% of environments do not benefit from additional compute.
- Behavioral diagnostics: humans use resets as hypothesis-testing tools (reset ~12.5% of actions; high Longest-Common-Subsequence structure around resets, ~0.637); models treat resets as ordinary actions (<7% combined reset+no-op; LCS 0.19-0.36) and often ignore contradictory test-phase evidence, relying on interaction-phase rules.
- Observation: 2D color grid; actions {left, right, up, down, click(x,y), no-op} plus reset (interaction phase) and go-to-test. Underlying environments are defined in the Autumn DSL.
- Limitations (theirs/spotted): grid-world is simplified (no physics/robotics/multi-agent); prompt-sensitivity (gap persists across variants); single model trajectory per problem (cost) vs 20 human attempts; not saturated (12 environments score zero across all models) but newer models approach saturation.

## Interpretation (flagged as inference)

- WorldTest is the evaluation backbone the lane needs: it operationalizes "did the agent actually LEARN the dynamics?" separately from "did it maximize task reward in the training environment?" by testing transfer to derived challenge environments after reward-free exploration. Its third task family (predicting CHANGES to the causal dynamics) is exactly the capability an autoresearch-improved WAM would need: notice when the world's rules shifted.
- The headline result (humans ~90% vs best frontier model ~27.5%, compute helps in under 60% of environments, models fail strategic experimentation and belief-updating) is the strongest available caution against assuming a frontier LLM can autonomously DISCOVER a good world model by exploration. The very capability the lane's thesis relies on (run experiments, update your model from contradictory evidence) is where current models most visibly fail in this benchmark.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful**: the WorldTest pattern (reward-free explore in scene A, then score on derived challenges in scene B) is a directly adoptable evaluation design for any Minecraft WAM-improvement loop: do not score the WAM only on the scenes it was refined on; hold out a related scene and test whether the improved WAM predicts there. The three task families (predict masked state, detect a dynamics change, plan) are concrete WAM-quality probes the repo could mirror against verifier ground truth. Change-detection in particular is a useful WAM health check (did the world's rules change under the WAM's feet).
- **What to avoid / overclaim**: AutumnBench is grid-world and single-agent; its numbers are not Minecraft or social. The borrow is the PROTOCOL (explore-then-test-on-derived-environments, behavior-based, representation-agnostic) and the empirical humility (frontier models are weak world-model LEARNERS), not the suite itself.
