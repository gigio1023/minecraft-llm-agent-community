## Primary-source facts

- **Research question**: standard LLM verifiers are trained as **discriminative**
  classifiers (assign a scalar `r(x,y) in [0,1]`), which throws away the LLM's text-
  generation ability (no CoT, no instruction-tuning transfer, no inference-time compute).
  Can verification be done as next-token prediction instead?
- **Method (GenRM)**: represent solution correctness as the LLM's probability of a single
  text token.
  - **Direct GenRM**: train on the question "Is the answer correct (Yes/No)?"; score =
    `p(Yes | x, y, I)`.
  - **Unified generation+verification**: co-train the verification loss with the
    standard SFT loss for generating correct solutions (a `lambda`-weighted mixture),
    yielding positive transfer between "how to solve" and "whether a solution is correct".
  - **GenRM-CoT**: generate a verification chain-of-thought (`Let's verify step by step.`)
    *then* emit Yes/No; score conditions on the self-generated rationale. CoT catches
    subtle errors a direct verifier misses.
  - **Inference-time compute**: sample K verification rationales and average the Yes
    probability (majority voting, default K=32). Discriminative verifiers cannot do this.
  - Training rationales can be human- or LLM-generated; synthetic rationales are improved
    by conditioning on a *reference solution* during data generation only (not at test, to
    avoid train/test mismatch).
- **Headline results** (fixed generator, only the verifier differs): GenRM (esp.
  GenRM-CoT + majority voting) beats discriminative RMs, DPO verifiers, LLM-as-a-Judge,
  and self-consistency. Best-of-N gains: algorithmic string tasks ~5% -> 45.3%; GSM8K
  73% -> 93.4% (Gemma2-9B verifier on Gemini 1.0 Pro solutions, surpassing GPT-4 and
  Gemini 1.5 Pro); easy-to-hard MATH500 28% -> 44.6% (solves 17% more competition problems
  at Best-of-32); MMLU abstract algebra 37.9% -> 53.5%. GenRM scales favorably with model
  size and inference-time compute.
- **The illustrative example** (Figure, GSM8K): a solution that ignores the word "each"
  and mis-bundles a cost. The discriminative RM scores it **0.999** (confidently wrong);
  GenRM-CoT reasons "Step 3 is incorrect, expected ...", emits "No", majority-vote score
  0.0015. The verifier needs to *reason*, not just classify, to catch convincing errors.

## Interpretation (labeled inference)

- GenRM is the strongest evidence that a **verifier that generates an explicit rationale
  before deciding** beats one that only outputs a number, and beats a bare prompted judge.
  This is the technical core of the project's advisory WAM: it should not emit a bare
  trust scalar; it should *predict the expected state and articulate the comparison*, then
  signal trust, which is verbalizable and inspectable.
- The discriminative-RM-says-0.999-but-wrong example is the verifier-side analogue of the
  project's whole reason for existing: a plausible-but-false claim must be caught by
  reasoning against evidence, not rubber-stamped. In the repo, the "evidence" is the
  runtime artifact (inventory/container/chat), so the GenRM rationale becomes "predicted
  delta X; observed delta Y; mismatch on item count."
- Important boundary: GenRM is still a *learned* judge graded for solution correctness on
  tasks that have an answer key. The project's advisory predictor for *social* deltas has
  no answer key, so GenRM's mechanism (reason-then-verdict, inference-time compute,
  unified gen+verify) transfers, but its reliability numbers do not.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful**:
  1. Reason-then-verdict verifier shape (verbalized rationale, then a trust signal) for
     the advisory WAM, so its output is inspectable rather than an opaque scalar.
  2. Inference-time compute for verification (sample K judgments, aggregate) as a cheap
     reliability knob when a single judgment is noisy, with the caution that this is
     compute the project may not want to spend on every cycle.
  3. The "convincing wrong" framing as a test-design principle: build adversarial cases
     where the actor's prose is plausible but the world refutes it, and check the
     advisory WAM catches them.
- **NOT the repo's research contribution**: GenRM is reward-model engineering (support).
  Also, GenRM is a *generative judge of correctness*; the validity theme warns an LLM
  judge must not be the primary social score. So GenRM informs *how* an advisory predictor
  is shaped, but the primary score in the repo stays the world artifact.
- **Overclaim to avoid**: GenRM's gains are on math/algorithmic tasks with checkable
  answers and a defined positive/negative training set. Do not claim a GenRM-style social
  verifier reaches comparable reliability; embodied social deltas are harder and lack
  ground-truth labels.

## Reproducibility

- **Partial**: method fully specified; data/site released. Uses Gemma/Gemini models;
  exact numbers depend on those, but the method is re-implementable on open models.
