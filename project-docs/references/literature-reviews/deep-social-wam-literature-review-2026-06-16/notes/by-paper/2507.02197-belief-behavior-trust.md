## Primary-source facts

- Research question: do LLM role-playing agents' **stated beliefs** ("what they say" about how a persona would behave) match their **actual role-play behavior** ("how they act")? Framed as a *pre-hoc validity check* - can prompting the model for its beliefs flag misaligned simulations before expensive synthetic-data generation?
- Method: **belief-behavior consistency** metric over the **Trust Game** (Berg et al. 1995; Trustor sends money X, it triples, Trustee returns some) using an augmented GenAgents persona bank.
  - Population level: elicit attribute->behavior rankings and effect sizes; simulate N=50 personas; compare elicited vs simulated via Spearman ρ and effect-size error |η̂² − η²|.
  - Individual level: forecast a single persona's send amounts over R rounds (MAE between forecast ŝ_r and actual s_r).
- Design factors tested: (1) belief type (expected outcomes vs task-relevant attributes), (2) when/how Trust Game context is presented during elicitation, (3) forecast horizon.
- Findings:
  - **Systematic belief-behavior inconsistencies at both individual and population level.** "Even when models appear to encode plausible beliefs, they may fail to apply them in a consistent way."
  - **Explicit task context during belief elicitation does not improve consistency.**
  - Self-conditioning improves alignment in some models; **imposing a researcher's prior tends to undermine it** (controllability concern: a model may keep portraying young people as more generous even when instructed otherwise).
  - **Individual-level forecast accuracy degrades over longer horizons.**

## Interpretation (my inference, labeled)

- This is the precise scientific articulation of the repo's central failure mode. Project Sid's motivating example (an agent verbally agrees to give a pickaxe while its action module does something else) and this paper's Trust Game inconsistency are the *same class*: the LLM's stated/elicited disposition does not predict its enacted behavior. The repo names it "chat/action incoherence," "fake progress," "progress laundering."
- The corrective the repo already enforces is the answer this paper calls for: do not trust the agent's stated belief/intention; verify the *behavior* against an external referent. In the Trust Game that referent is the money sent; in Minecraft it is the inventory/container delta. The repo replaces "did the agent say it would lend the pickaxe" with "did a verified item transfer occur."
- The "imposed prior undermines consistency" finding is a caution against the repo ever using persona/soul text to *force* a social outcome - it must let behavior emerge and then verify it, not script it (matches SPEC: "Social simulation must not be expected from persona text alone").
- "Forecast accuracy degrades over horizon" reinforces Lifelong SOTOPIA's memory-decay result and warns that any advisory social predictor will be least reliable exactly where the repo cares most (multi-cycle obligation closure).

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Mechanically useful:
  - The two-level (population Spearman/effect-size; individual MAE-over-rounds) consistency metric is a ready evaluation design for the repo's advisory predictor: elicit the model's predicted social delta, then measure it against the verified delta. This is a pre-hoc validity gate, not just a post-hoc score.
  - The Trust Game is a clean, cheap analog for a 2-actor Minecraft lend/return scenario (send = lend, return = repay), with the crucial upgrade that Minecraft makes the "send" a *verified* inventory event.
- Avoid / overclaim:
  - Do not present an agent's stated intention as evidence of a social outcome anywhere in reports.
  - Do not assume task context or prompting fixes belief-behavior gaps; the paper shows it does not.

## WAM layer(s) informed

- Social WAM (trust as the construct), and a validity instrument: it gives the repo a metric for "does the predicted social transition match the enacted/verified one," which is the core of an advisory social WAM evaluation.
