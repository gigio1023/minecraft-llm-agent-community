## Primary-source facts (LaTeX-verified)

- **Scope**: an overview of NAS, categorizing methods along THREE dimensions (the canonical NAS taxonomy): search space, search strategy, performance estimation strategy. NAS is positioned as a subfield of AutoML overlapping hyperparameter optimization and meta-learning (abstract, Section 1).
- **Search Space**: "defines which architectures can be represented in principle." Explicit tradeoff stated: incorporating prior knowledge reduces the space and simplifies search, "However, this also introduces a human bias, which may prevent finding novel architectural building blocks that go beyond the current human knowledge." Examples range from chain-structured nets (sequence of layers, conditional hyperparameters) to complex multi-branch/skip-connection and cell-based spaces (Section 1, Section search space).
- **Search Strategy**: how to explore the (often exponentially large or unbounded) space; the exploration-exploitation tradeoff, balancing fast discovery against premature convergence to suboptimal regions (Section 1).
- **Performance Estimation Strategy**: estimating an architecture's performance on unseen data. Naive full train+validate "is unfortunately computationally expensive and limits the number of architectures that can be explored"; much research targets cheaper estimation (Section 1). This is the survey's framing of the NAS cost problem.

## Interpretation (flagged as inference)

- This survey supplies the reference vocabulary my lane's theme uses: every automated learning-system-design method decomposes into (1) what can be represented, (2) how to search it, (3) how to estimate quality cheaply. The same three axes describe an autoresearch loop tuning the repo's WAM recipe: the recipe/config space, the agent's search over it, and the cheap evaluation (the verifier on a fixture).
- The search-space tradeoff stated here (human prior shrinks the space but biases away from novelty) is the same tension AutoML-Zero pushed to the extreme (drop the prior, pay 1-in-10^12 sparsity). It is the honest framing for how much structure to bake into a repo recipe-search space.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful**: the three-axis decomposition as a design checklist for any repo autoresearch loop: define the recipe space, the search strategy, and a CHEAP performance-estimation strategy (the verifier on a small fixture is exactly the "cheap performance estimation" the survey calls for).
- **Research contribution (not ours)**: it is a survey; the repo does not contribute to it.
- **What to avoid / overclaim**: do not skip the "performance estimation strategy" axis. The survey's central cost lesson is that naive full evaluation limits how much you can search; a repo loop must have a cheap, faithful evaluation or it cannot afford to search.
