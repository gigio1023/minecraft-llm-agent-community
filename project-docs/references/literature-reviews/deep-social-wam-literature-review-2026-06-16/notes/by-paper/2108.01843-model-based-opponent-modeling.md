# Model-Based Opponent Modeling (MBOM)

- title: Model-Based Opponent Modeling
- authors: Xiaopeng Yu, Jiechuan Jiang, Wanpeng Zhang, Haobin Jiang, Zongqing Lu (Peking University)
- year: 2021 (arXiv); NeurIPS 2022
- venue/source: NeurIPS 2022; arXiv 2108.01843
- arxiv_id: 2108.01843
- urls: paper https://arxiv.org/abs/2108.01843 ; hf https://huggingface.co/papers/2108.01843
- source availability: LaTeX (`papers/latex/2108.01843/mbom.tex`), read abstract + related work + method (recursive imagination + Bayesian mixing).

## Primary-source facts (what the paper states)

- Research question: how can an agent adapt to *unknown and possibly learning/reasoning*
  opponents, including ones that adapt to the agent itself, rather than assuming a fixed
  or known opponent policy?
- Core idea: **use a learned environment model (a world model) to model the opponent.**
  Inspired by "humans anticipate others' future behavior by simulating interactions in the
  brain after knowing the rules of the environment." MBOM:
  1. Learns an environment model Gamma(s', r | s, a, a^o) from interaction experience and a
     level-0 opponent model (Imagined Opponent Policy, IOP) by maximum likelihood.
  2. **Recursive imagination**: rolls out the environment model to compute the opponent's
     best response to the agent's current policy, then finetunes the opponent model on those
     simulated best responses to get a higher-level IOP; repeating this generates a set of
     IOPs at increasing reasoning levels (level-0, level-1, ...). This is recursive reasoning
     done *through* the predictive model ("imagine the learning and reasoning of opponents").
  3. **Bayesian mixing**: because the opponent's true reasoning level is unknown, MBOM mixes
     the imagined opponent policies weighted by their similarity to the opponent's *real
     observed behavior* (Bayesian update), instead of committing to one level.
- Evaluation: competitive tasks against three opponent types: fixed policy, naive learner,
  reasoning learner. MBOM outperforms strong baselines, especially against naive and
  reasoning learners; ablations confirm both recursive imagination and Bayesian mixing matter;
  also applicable to cooperative tasks.
- Stated context: prior recursive-reasoning RL (PR2, GR2, level-k belief methods) used
  centralized-training/decentralized-execution and trained fixed agents that cannot handle
  diverse opponents at execution time. MBOM relaxes this by learning an environment model and
  doing the recursion at decision time.

## Interpretation (my reading, labeled inference)

- MBOM is the cleanest bridge in the lane between *opponent modeling* and *world models*: it
  performs agent modeling by simulating futures in a learned predictive model
  (imagine-then-respond), which is structurally the WAM idea (`p(o', a | o)`) applied to the
  problem of predicting another agent. The Albrecht-Stone survey (1709.08071) calls this the
  recursive-reasoning / I-POMDP family; MBOM is a modern model-based instantiation.
- The Bayesian-mixing-by-observed-similarity step is the same epistemic discipline as
  Hypothetical Minds (2407.07086) scoring hypotheses by predictive fit: do not commit to one
  model of the other; weight candidate models by how well they match observed behavior.
- For the project, MBOM is evidence (interpretation) that opponent/agent modeling can be done
  via a predictive model rather than a hand-specified type space, supporting the idea that a
  Social WAM can predict another actor by forecasting their behavior and reconciling with
  evidence.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Mechanically useful for this repo (as design pattern, not code):
  - The "maintain several candidate models of the other actor at increasing sophistication,
    weight them by fit to observed behavior" pattern is directly reusable for a Social WAM
    that predicts another actor's next action and updates which model best explains them.
  - Keep recursion shallow: MBOM (and the behavioral-game-theory evidence in the
    Albrecht-Stone survey) indicate little benefit beyond 1-2 levels of nesting.
- Research-contribution caution:
  - MBOM trains neural environment models and opponent policies with RL over many episodes;
    the repo's actors are LLM-proposed turns, not gradient-trained policies. Borrow the
    *imagine-then-respond + mix-by-fit* concept, not the training machinery.
  - MBOM operates in abstract competitive games (no possession, no physical substrate). The
    repo's analogue must keep the "environment model" = the typed Minecraft+social state and
    must keep predictions advisory and verifier-scored.

## WAM layer(s) informed

- **Social WAM** (predicting another actor's policy/next action via a predictive model).
- **Institutional WAM** secondarily (modeling reasoning/adapting opponents underlies role and
  norm dynamics).
- Directly exercises the **Physical WAM** mechanism conceptually: the "environment model" that
  rolls out futures is the world-model the social prediction rides on, mirroring the project's
  physical-before-social dependency.

## Reproducibility

- Partial: method fully specified and standard RL components; transfers to this repo as a
  design pattern (imagine candidate opponent models, mix by observed fit), not as runnable code.
