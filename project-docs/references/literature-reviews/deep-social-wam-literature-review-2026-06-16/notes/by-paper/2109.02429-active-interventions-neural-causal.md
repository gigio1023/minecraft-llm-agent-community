- **arxiv_id**: 2109.02429

- **deconflict**: wave-3 `research-area-affordances-and-causal-world-models` covered PASSIVE causal-dynamics learning (CoDA 2007.02863, CDL 2206.13452). This note covers the ACTIVE / EXPERIMENTAL angle (choose interventions to learn structure) that the wave-3 theme explicitly left open.

## Primary-source facts (what the paper states)

- Research question: how should a differentiable neural causal-discovery method ACTIVELY choose which variable to intervene on, so it identifies the underlying causal DAG with the fewest interventions?
- Setup: a differentiable neural causal model with structural parameters (a soft adjacency matrix `sigma(gamma)`) and per-variable functional MLPs. Built on top of two existing frameworks (SDI for discrete data, DCDI for continuous), so the contribution is the intervention-SELECTION layer, not the base learner.
- Active Intervention Targeting (AIT): at each round, score every candidate single-variable intervention by a ratio `D_k = VBG_k / VWG_k`, where VBG (variance between graphs) measures how much the predicted post-intervention outcome DIFFERS across DAGs sampled from the current belief, and VWG (variance within graphs) is the in-graph sample noise. Pick the intervention with the highest D_k = the one whose outcome most disagrees across current hypothesis graphs = the one that most reduces structural uncertainty. (This is experimental design / "learning to experiment" for causal discovery.)
- Works for both discrete and continuous DAG-learning formulations. Uses an efficient two-phase DAG sampler to draw hypothesis graphs from the soft adjacency.
- Evaluation: synthetic structured graphs (chain, collider, tree, jungle, full) and Erdos-Renyi random graphs (N=5 to 15), plus real-world Sachs (11-var flow cytometry) and Asia (8-var). Metric: structural Hamming distance (SHD) to the ground-truth DAG (missing + extra + reversed edges; lower is better).
- Results: AIT reaches near-zero SHD on structured graphs at N=15 and substantially fewer interventions than random intervention targeting; it keeps selecting informative targets past the Markov-equivalence-class "elbow" (where observational data alone stalls) to orient remaining edges; robust to moderate interventional noise where random targeting fails.
- Limitations (theirs/spotted): assumes causal sufficiency (no hidden confounders) unless the base framework handles them; assumes clean hard interventions on individual variables; limited to small graphs (<= ~15 nodes); the Sachs ground-truth is itself disputed; cost of scoring every candidate via sampled-graph ancestral sampling.

## Interpretation (flagged as inference)

- This is the lane's archetype for the EXPERIMENTAL half of "discover the world model": do not just fit a model to whatever data arrives, choose the intervention that most resolves your uncertainty about the model's STRUCTURE. AIT's "intervene where your hypothesis graphs most disagree" is the causal-structure analog of Plan2Explore's "go where your ensemble most disagrees," and the formal version of WorldLLM's "collect transitions your current theory predicts poorly."
- For the thesis it shows the principled upper bound of intervene-to-learn: with clean interventions and a small graph you can recover causal structure efficiently. CausaLab (2605.26029) is the sobering counterpart: when an LLM agent does this in practice, even in a small clean world, mechanism recovery lags far behind prediction (0.47-0.80 edge-F1). Together they bound the thesis: active intervention is the right idea, but LLM-driven causal discovery is far from solved.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful**: AIT's selection rule (intervene where your current models most disagree about the outcome) is a concrete, well-founded exploration objective for improving a STRUCTURED advisory WAM. If the repo maintains a small set of candidate WAM hypotheses (e.g., competing rules about which state variables gate an Action Card's success), AIT says: have the actor perform the action whose verifier-observed delta would most discriminate between those hypotheses, then update. This is exactly the intervene-then-let-the-verifier-label loop the lane brief asks for, with a principled target-selection criterion.
- **What to avoid / overclaim**: AIT assumes clean hard interventions on isolated variables, full observability, and no hidden confounders. A Minecraft social world violates all three (hidden intent, partner-dependent and entangled social variables, you cannot cleanly hold one social variable fixed). Do not claim AIT's efficiency transfers to social discovery; use its target-selection PRINCIPLE (intervene to discriminate hypotheses) and treat the social-layer transport as unproven research surface. Also: in a social world, "intervene on variable X" can mean taking an action that imposes a cost on another actor, so AIT's value-free criterion must be subordinated to the repo's material-claim / weak-commons constraints.
