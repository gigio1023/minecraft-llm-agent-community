## Plain-language: what it introduced and why it mattered

PlaNet (Deep Planning Network) answered a practical question Ha and Schmidhuber's World
Models left open: can a single agent learn its dynamics model from pixels and then *plan*
well enough to control hard continuous tasks, without ever rendering an image during
planning? Its two lasting contributions:

1. **The Recurrent State-Space Model (RSSM)**, the latent-dynamics architecture that
   became the standard. The key design insight: a latent transition model needs BOTH a
   deterministic memory path and a stochastic path, and you must keep both.
2. **Planning purely in latent space**: evaluate thousands of imagined action sequences
   per step entirely in the compact latent state, never decoding pixels, using
   model-predictive control.

Why it mattered: it solved DeepMind Control Suite tasks from pixels with roughly **200x
less environment interaction** than the model-free A3C baseline, at similar compute. That
data-efficiency gap is the headline argument for model-based RL, and PlaNet made it
concrete in a modern deep-learning setting.

## Definitions (first use)

- **Latent dynamics model**: a model that maps high-dimensional observations (pixels)
  into a compact hidden state and predicts forward in that hidden state instead of in
  pixel space.
- **State-Space Model (SSM)**: the classic structure of a partially observable Markov
  decision process (POMDP): a hidden state `s_t` evolves by a transition model
  `p(s_t | s_{t-1}, a_{t-1})`, and observations `o_t` and rewards `r_t` are emitted from
  `s_t`. PlaNet is a learned, non-linear SSM (the paper calls it "a non-linear Kalman
  filter or sequential VAE").
- **RSSM (Recurrent State-Space Model)**: PlaNet's transition model, which splits the
  latent state into a **deterministic** part `h_t` and a **stochastic** part `s_t`:
    - Deterministic state: `h_t = f(h_{t-1}, s_{t-1}, a_{t-1})` (an RNN).
    - Stochastic state: `s_t ~ p(s_t | h_t)`.
    - Observation model: `o_t ~ p(o_t | h_t, s_t)`; reward model: `r_t ~ p(r_t | h_t, s_t)`.
  The deterministic path lets the model reliably carry information across many steps; the
  stochastic path lets it represent uncertainty and multiple futures. The paper shows
  empirically that BOTH paths are crucial for planning.
- **Cross-Entropy Method (CEM)**: a population-based, gradient-free planner. It keeps a
  Gaussian distribution over action sequences, samples candidates, scores them under the
  model, and refits the distribution to the best-scoring fraction; after a few iterations
  it returns the mean as the action.
- **Model-Predictive Control (MPC)**: plan a short action sequence, execute only the
  first action, then replan after the next observation. PlaNet re-initializes the action
  belief each step to avoid local optima.
- **Latent overshooting**: PlaNet's training trick. The standard objective trains only
  one-step predictions; latent overshooting generalizes the variational bound to penalize
  *multi-step* latent predictions, which improves long-horizon accuracy and is a cheap
  regularizer because it stays in latent space (no pixel decoding).

## Primary-source facts

- **Model**: RSSM with deterministic `h_t` (GRU/RNN) and stochastic Gaussian `s_t`;
  trained as a sequential VAE with a variational lower bound (reconstruction term +
  KL/complexity term), plus latent overshooting for multi-step consistency.
- **Planning**: CEM-based MPC. The observation/decoder model "provides a rich training
  signal but is not used for planning." Because reward is a function of latent state, the
  planner runs purely in latent space, so it can score large batches of action sequences
  fast without generating images. No policy network and no value network are learned;
  the policy *is* the online planner.
- **Experience loop**: start from a few seed episodes of random actions; alternate
  training the model and collecting one new episode every C updates (with small Gaussian
  exploration noise and action repeat R).
- **Result**: solves continuous-control DM Control tasks from pixels; substantially
  outperforms model-free A3C and in some cases D4PG in final performance, with on average
  200x less environment interaction and similar wall-clock compute.
- **Failure modes named (intro, verbatim sense)**: learning dynamics accurate enough for
  planning is hard because of "model inaccuracies, accumulating errors of multi-step
  predictions, failure to capture multiple possible futures, and overconfident
  predictions outside of the training distribution."

## Interpretation (labeled inference)

- RSSM is the single most reused component in this lineage: DreamerV1, V2, V3 and Dreamer
  4 all build on it. If a newcomer learns one architecture from this genealogy, it should
  be "deterministic recurrent path + stochastic latent path, predict forward in latent
  space, decode pixels only for a training signal." (Cross-ref: `2509.24527-dreamer4.md`
  and `game-world-models-family.md` for the later RSSM-to-transformer evolution.)
- PlaNet's "the decoder is a training signal, not used for planning" is the early seed of
  the later realization (MuZero, TD-MPC2, JEPA) that *reconstruction is dispensable for
  control*. PlaNet still reconstructs pixels to learn the latent; MuZero and TD-MPC2 drop
  reconstruction entirely.
- The intro's four named failure modes are the compounding-error vocabulary stated
  cleanly. Lane 10 owns the deep treatment; here they establish that the latent-dynamics
  line was aware of multi-step error accumulation from the start, and that latent
  overshooting was an early mitigation aimed at multi-step prediction accuracy.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful**: RSSM is the canonical structured-latent dynamics model and the
  cleanest reference for "split state into deterministic memory + stochastic uncertainty."
  CEM-in-latent-space is a reusable pattern for cheap imagined-rollout scoring. The "decode
  only for a training signal, plan in latent space" principle is directly relevant to a
  structured-state predictor that should be cheap to query.
- **Research contribution (not this repo's)**: building a learned latent simulator and an
  online planner over it is a research line. For this repo, the borrow is the *shape*
  (compact predicted state, multi-step consistency objective, uncertainty representation),
  not "replace Actor Turn selection with CEM planning over a learned model."

## Relevance to this repo (one line)

Source of RSSM and "plan in latent space, don't decode pixels"; the structured-latent
dynamics shape and multi-step-consistency objective are the reusable lessons, not the
online planner as a runtime action selector.
