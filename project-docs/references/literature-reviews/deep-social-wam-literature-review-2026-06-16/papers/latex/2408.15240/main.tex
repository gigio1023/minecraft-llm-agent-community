\begin{abstract}
\vspace{-0.3cm}
Verifiers or reward models are often used to enhance the reasoning performance of large language models (LLMs). A common approach is the Best-of-N method, where N candidate solutions generated by the LLM are ranked by a verifier, and the best one is selected. While LLM-based verifiers are typically trained as discriminative classifiers to score solutions, they do \emph{not} utilize the text generation capabilities of pretrained LLMs. To overcome this limitation, we instead propose training verifiers using the ubiquitous next-token prediction objective, jointly on verification and solution generation. Compared to standard verifiers, such generative verifiers~(\genV) can benefit from several advantages of LLMs: they integrate seamlessly with instruction tuning, enable chain-of-thought reasoning, and can utilize additional test-time compute via majority voting for better verification. We demonstrate that \genV\ outperforms discriminative, DPO verifiers, and LLM-as-a-Judge, resulting in large performance gains with Best-of-N, {namely $5\% \rightarrow 45.3\%$ on algorithmic tasks and $73\% \rightarrow 93.4\%$ on GSM8K. In easy-to-hard generalization settings, we observe improvements of $28\% \rightarrow 44.6\%$ on MATH}, and $37.9\% \rightarrow 53.5\%$ on MMLU abstract algebra. Furthermore, we find that training \genV\ with synthetic verification rationales is sufficient to pick out subtle errors on math problems.  
Finally, we demonstrate that GenRM scales favorably with model size and test-time compute.
\vspace{-0.4cm}
\end{abstract}

While large language models (LLMs) demonstrate remarkable capabilities, they often confidently make logical and factual mistakes~\citep{zhang2023siren}. These mistakes pose a significant challenge for reasoning problems, where a single mistake can invalidate the solution. A common strategy to address this issue is Best-of-N~\citep{charniak2005coarse, cobbe2021training}: the LLM generates N candidate solutions for a given problem, and a learned reward model, referred to as a ``verifier'', ranks these solutions and picks the most suitable one. The effectiveness of this strategy hinges on how accurate the verifier is, making it crucial to identify better approaches for training verifiers.

LLM-based verifiers for reasoning are typically trained as discriminative reward models~(RMs) to assign numerical scores to candidate solutions, which is then used to classify them as correct or incorrect~\citep{cobbe2021training, lightman2023let, wang2023math}. However, this scoring approach does not utilize the text-generation capabilities that LLMs are fundamentally designed for. 
As a result, discriminative RMs miss out on the inherent strengths of generative LLMs, such as unified instruction tuning~\citep{chung2024scaling}, chain-of-thought~(CoT) reasoning~\citep{wei2022chain}, and utilizing additional inference-time computation for better performance~\citep{ wang2022self, brown2024large}. While LLM-as-a-Judge~\citep{zheng2024judging}, which simply prompts off-the-shelf generative LLMs, also offers the above advantages, it typically underperforms trained LLMs-based verifiers on reasoning tasks, which we also observe in \autoref{fig:overall-result}.

In this work, we propose \emph{training} verifiers with next-token prediction, which we call \textbf{\genV}, to leverage the text generation capabilities of LLMs~(\autoref{cot-verifier-example-main-1}). Concretely, to produce a numerical score for a solution, the verifier now uses a prompt such as `Is the answer correct?', and represents the score as the probability of a single text token (e.g., `Yes' or `No'). \genV\ naturally supports CoT reasoning~\citep{wei2022chain}: it can be trained to reason explicitly by generating a verbalized rationale before predicting correctness using `Yes' or `No' token~(\autoref{fig:gen-rm-figure}), assuming rationales are available during training. We can further boost verification accuracy of CoT verifiers using majority-voting~\citep{wang2022self}: sampling multiple CoT rationales and calculating the average score of the `Yes' token across all rationales, enabling the use of inference-time compute for verification. Moreover, \genV's next-token prediction training enables unifying solution generation with verification, which has been difficult with DPO verifiers~\citep{rafailov2024direct, hosseini2024v}, potentially improving verification through positive transfer from solution. 


Our results show that \genV\ outperforms discriminative RMs, LLM-as-a-Judge, and self-consistency on algorithmic string manipulation and math reasoning tasks~(\autoref{fig:overall-result}). Best-of-N performance further improves with {\genV}-CoT that uses majority-voting, nearly matching performance with oracle verifier on algorithmic tasks. On GSM8K, when using a Gemma2-9B \genV-CoT verifier on solutions from Gemini 1.0 Pro, we observe an improvement from $73\% \rightarrow 93.4\%$ in terms of the number of problems solved, surpassing GPT-4 and Gemini 1.5 Pro. Furthermore, \genV-CoT trained on grade-school math problems exhibit \emph{easy-to-hard} generalization, solving 17\% more high-school competition problems in MATH500~\citep{lightman2023let} with Best-of-32. Moreover, we find that generative verifiers scale more favorably  than discriminative verifiers as we increase model capacity, 
and outperform LLM-as-a-Judge as we scale inference-time compute with majority voting. Overall, generative verifiers hold significant potential for improving the reasoning capabilities of LLMs.

\section{Conclusion \& Future Work}

In this paper, we have introduced Generative Verifiers ({\genV}), which recast verification as next-token prediction. {\genV} is more performant than discriminative verifiers, and unlocks the use of chain-of-thought reasoning and inference-time compute for better verification. {\genV} also unifies generation and verification into a single LLM, and demonstrates that such a unification benefits both generation and verification. Moreover, we show that synthetic model-generated rationales, which can be error-prone, are sufficient to teach {\genV} how to use verification CoT to pick out tricky errors on math reasoning tasks~(see \autoref{cot-verifier-example-main-1}, \ref{math-transfer-cot-verifier-example-main-figure}, \ref{cot-verifier-example-main-3}, and \autoref{sec:app_examples}).

The framework of generative verification offers a solid foundation for future work. Promising directions include extending this framework to broader tasks such as coding, alignment, text-to-image generation~\citep{lin2024evaluating}, and open-ended generation~\citep{besta2024checkembed}. Furthermore, leveraging process-level supervision~\citep{lightman2023let} and training CoT verifiers with reinforcement learning~(RL) can result in more accurate generative verifiers. Given \genV's compatibility with all the existing tools designed to improve LLMs, exploring enhancements through techniques like retrieval-augmented generation \citep{improve-lm-with-rag}, 
many-shot learning~\citep{agarwal2024many}, multi-staged prompting~\citep{tree-of-thoughts}, and tool use~\citep{toolformer} would be interesting. Finally, incorporating generative verifiers into RL pipelines for LLMs warrants further investigation.
