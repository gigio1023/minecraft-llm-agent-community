\subsection{Notation}
\label{subsec:Notation}
Completing tasks in complex, long-horizon environments is typically formulated as a Markov Decision Process of $\left\langle \mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{T}, \mathcal{R}, \gamma \right\rangle$.
In the generic setting of multi-step tasks, an LLM-powered agent $\pi_\theta$ receives a task instruction and an observation\footnote{Technically, any input to the agent from our environments is an observation (as in POMDP). However, to simplify notation we use $s$ to generally denote input data received from the environment.} from the environment $s_{t} \sim \mathcal{S}$, generates an action $a_{t} \sim \pi(\cdot | s_t)$, and receives a new observation $s_{t+1} \sim \mathcal{S}$.
During action generation, the model is often given up to $H$ turns of interaction history $\left\langle s_{t-H}, a_{t-H}, ..., s_t\right\rangle$, and is allowed to think/reason before generating the next action $a_{t}$.
This interaction process is repeated until the task completion or reaching a maximum number of steps, upon which a terminal reward $r_{T} \sim \mathcal{R}(a_T,s_T)$ is returned based on whether the task is failed/completed successfully.
The discounting factor $\gamma \in (0,1]$ is used to discount and propagate future rewards during RL training.
% Since this work involves training an LLM to generate next-states, we denote generated states as $\hat{s}_{t}$ to distinguish them from the real $s_{t}$ from the environment.
Note that since this work trains LLMs as world models, \emph{we denote generated states as $\hat{s}_t$} to distinguish them from real environment states $s_t$.

For example, in environments such as ALFWorld \citep{shridhar2021alfworldaligningtextembodied}, an action $a_{t}$ may be ``go to sidetable 1'', and the resulting state $s_{t+1}$ describes the outcome of that interaction, such as the objects currently available to agent (e.g., ``You arrive at sidetable 1. On the sidetable you see a mug, a pepper shaker, and a tomato.'').
In more complex environments such as $\tau^2$ Bench \citep{barres2025tau2benchevaluatingconversationalagents}, an action $a_{t}$ could be a tool-call or a response to the user, and the next state $s_{t+1}$ returns either a tool response (often in json format), or a natural language response generated by the user simulator (powered by an LLM).
For more details on each environment, please see \Cref{sec:More Details on ALFWorld,sec:More Details on tau2 Bench}, respectively.

\label{subsec:WMRL}
% A key challenge in scaling current agentic post-training methods such as RL is their reliance on learning from accurate task-success reward signals after an interaction is completed.
% While effective, such reward signals are sparse and need to be carefully designed by domain experts/humans \citep{barres2025tau2benchevaluatingconversationalagents,xie2024osworldbenchmarkingmultimodalagents,rawles2025androidworlddynamicbenchmarkingenvironment,zhou2024webarenarealisticwebenvironment}.
% These methods are hence difficult to scale, especially as tasks and environments are becoming more and more complex.
A key challenge in scaling agentic post-training methods such as RL is their reliance on accurate task-success rewards provided at the end of an episode.
While effective, these rewards are sparse and require careful design by domain experts \citep{barres2025tau2benchevaluatingconversationalagents,xie2024osworldbenchmarkingmultimodalagents,rawles2025androidworlddynamicbenchmarkingenvironment,zhou2024webarenarealisticwebenvironment}.
As tasks and environments become more complex, this reliance introduces scaling challenges.

% We introduce \wmrl{} (\wmrlshort{}), a scalable, self-supervised training method where the agent learns accurate world model knowledge from the environment dynamics $\mathcal{T}$, before further finetuning with standard task-success reward RL training.
We introduce \wmrl{} (\wmrlshort{}), a scalable, self-supervised training method where the agent learns accurate world model knowledge from the environment dynamics $\mathcal{T}$, before further finetuning with task-success reward RL.
Intuitively, \wmrlshort{} trains an LLM policy $\pi_\theta$ to \emph{also be able to reason about the consequences} $\hat{s}_{t+1}$ given an action $a_{t}$ and a history $H$ of past interactions:
\[
(\mathrm{reason},\hat{s}_{t+1}) \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot | s_{\le t}, a_t); s_{\le t} \equiv \left\langle s_{t-H}, a_{t-H}, ..., s_t \right\rangle
\]
where ``$\mathrm{reason}$'' denotes reasoning tokens generated by the model before generating the final prediction of the next state $\hat{s}_{t+1}$.
To evaluate the quality of the prediction, we use a simple binary\footnote{Empirically, we find that binarized rewards are more robust and less susceptible to hacking (see \Cref{subsec:ablation}).} reward function that compares the distance between $\hat{s}_{t+1}$ and the ground truth $s_{t+1}$:
\[
r^{\mathrm{WM}}(\hat{s}_{t+1}, s_{t+1}) = \begin{cases}
% 1.0 & \text{if } \textrm{similarity}(\hat{s}_{t+1}, s_{t+1}) \ge \tau, \\
1.0, & \text{if } d(\hat{s}_{t+1}, s_{t+1}) < \tau_{d}, \\
0.0, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\] 
% where we mainly consider embedding-based cosine similarity \citep{karpukhin2020densepassageretrievalopendomain,zhang2025qwen3embeddingadvancingtext}:
where $\tau_d$ is a hyperparameter, and $d$ is implemented mainly using an off-the-shelf embedding model $E(\cdot)$ with cosine similarity \citep{karpukhin2020densepassageretrievalopendomain,zhang2025qwen3embeddingadvancingtext}:
\[
d(\hat{s}_{t+1}, s_{t+1}) = 1 - \cos(E(\hat{s}_{t+1}), E(s_{t+1})).
\] 
% from a pretrained embedding model 
% However, for weak reasoning models, we find such an objective might create thinking patterns too specific to next-state prediction, rather than general reasoning patterns that is grounded on the current and past interaction history.
% To encourage more grounded reasoning, along with $\hat{s}_{t+1}$ we also prompt the model to predict whether $\hat{s}_{t+1}$ will be a terminal state, regardless of success or failure: $(\mathrm{reason},\hat{s}_{t+1} \oplus \hat{\mathbbm{1}}_{\mathrm{terminal}} (\hat{s}_{t+1}) ) \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot | s_{\le t}, a_t)$.
% The reward function then compares $\hat{s}_{t+1} \oplus \hat{\mathbbm{1}}_{\mathrm{terminal}} (\hat{s}_{t+1}$ with the ground truth $s_{t+1} \oplus \mathbbm{1}_{\mathrm{terminal}} (s_{t+1})$, where $\oplus$ denotes concatenation and $\mathbbm{1}_{\mathrm{terminal}} (s_{t+1})$ is a text description of whether $s_{t+1}$ is a terminal state.
To optimize this reward, we use standard GRPO \citep{shao2024deepseekmathpushinglimitsmathematical,deepseekai2025deepseekr1incentivizingreasoningcapability}:
\begin{align}
    % \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}} \left[ 
    % \frac{1}{GT} \sum_{i=1}^{G} \sum_{t=1}^{T} 
    % \min{ \left(\rho_\theta(a^{(i)}_t)A(a^{(i)}_t), \textrm{clip}(\rho_\theta(a^{(i)}_t), 1\pm\epsilon) A(a^{(i)}_t) \right)}  -\beta D_{\textrm{KL}}(\pi_\theta || \pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{ref}}}) \right],\nonumber
    % \mathbb{E}_{\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}} \left[ 
    % \min{ \left(\rho_\theta(a)A(a), \textrm{clip}(\rho_\theta(a), 1\pm\epsilon) A(a) \right)} -\beta D_{\textrm{KL}}(\pi_\theta || \pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{ref}}}) \right],\nonumber
    \mathbb{E}_{\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}} \left[ 
    \min{ \left(\rho_\theta A, \textrm{clip}(\rho_\theta, 1\pm\epsilon) A \right)} -\beta D_{\textrm{KL}}(\pi_\theta || \pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{ref}}}) \right],\nonumber
\end{align}
% $\rho_\theta = \frac{\pi_\theta(y|x)}{\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{ref}}}(y|x)}$
% $A=\frac{r^{\mathrm{WM}}- \textrm{mean}(r^{\mathrm{WM}})}{\textrm{std}(r^{\mathrm{WM}})}$
where $\rho_\theta = \pi_\theta(y|x) / \pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{ref}}}(y|x)$ is the importance sampling ratio, $\beta$ is the KL regularization coefficient, and $A=[r^{\mathrm{WM}}- \textrm{mean}(r^{\mathrm{WM}})] / {\textrm{std}(r^{\mathrm{WM}})}$ is the group-relative advantage using our reward function.
We note that the entire process does not require any expert data, stronger LLMs, or task-success reward signals.

To collect training data for \wmrlshort{}, we directly use the target model $\pi_\theta$ to gather rollouts $(s_0, a_0, s_1, a_1, ... s_T)$ with the environment, and then convert the rollouts into triplets of $\left\langle s_{\le t}, a_t, s_{t+1} \right\rangle$ for all $t$.
To improve coverage and diversity, we perform $N>1$ rollouts per training task.
To help the model focus on learning non-trivial world model knowledge during RL, we follow intuitions from \citep{snell2024scalingllmtesttimecompute,sun2025rlgrokkingrecipedoes} and subsample the portion of the dataset that are ``too easy'' to learn.
% Specifically, given a training and validation split of the \wmrlshort{} dataset, we first use SFT \emph{on the validation split} to train a separate $\pi'_\theta$ capable of generating $s_{t+1}$.
% Then, we use this $\pi'_\theta$ to generate $s_{t+1}$ \emph{on the training split}, subsampling training samples that consistently achieve high reward through $K=10$ attempts:
Specifically, we first use SFT to train a separate LLM $\pi'_\theta$ capable of predicting $\hat{s}_{t+1}$ using 10\% of the full dataset.
Then, we use this $\pi'_\theta$ to generate $\hat{s}_{t+1}$ on the other 90\% of the dataset (\ie{} the training split), subsampling training samples that consistently achieve high reward through $K=10$ attempts:
% \[
% \frac{1}{K}\sum\limits_{K} r^{\mathrm{WM}}(\hat{s}_{t+1}, s_{t+1}) \ge \tau_{\mathrm{easy}},
% \]
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:too_easy_eq}
    \frac{1}{K}\sum\limits_{K} r^{\mathrm{WM}}(\hat{s}_{t+1}, s_{t+1}) \ge \tau_{\mathrm{easy}},
\end{equation}
where $\tau_{\mathrm{easy}}$ is a hyperparameter.
For ``easy'' samples above the threshold, we only include them in the final training split with probability $p=0.1$, prioritizing harder samples while preserving diversity.
This resulting dataset is used for GRPO training in \wmrlshort{}, as described in the previous section.
An overview of the entire process is shown in \Cref{fig:wmrl_algo}.

We use the official training and test splits provided by each benchmark for training and evaluation.


\paragraph{Baselines}
We compare with other policy and world model related training methods from three categories: (1) learning from task-success reward; (2) learning from interaction/transition function $\mathcal{T}$, similar to our method; and (3) learning from expert annotations/stronger LLMs.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Learning from task-success reward}: we consider Reinforced Finetuning (\rft{}) using rejection sampling and standard RL with task-success reward (\policyrl{}). \rft{} first uses the target model to rollout $N$ trajectories per training task and then performs SFT training only on the trajectories that correctly solved the task \citep{touvron2023llama2openfoundation,zelikman2022starbootstrappingreasoningreasoning}. \policyrl{} directly uses GRPO to train the base model $\pi_\theta$ to optimize for task-success reward using online rollouts \citep{feng2025groupingrouppolicyoptimizationllm,yu2025dynamindlearningsimulateexperience}.
  % \item World Model SFT (\wmsftshort{}) which use identical training data as \wmrlshort{} (see below), but trains the model to directly predict $s'$ using SFT.
  \item \textbf{Learning from interaction/transition function}: we consider World Model SFT (\wmsftshort{}) which uses identical training data as \wmrlshort{}, but trains the model to directly predict $s_{t+1}$ using SFT. Note that no reasoning is involved in \wmsftshort{} as only $s_{t+1}$ is available.
  \item \textbf{Learning from expert/strong LLMs}: we consider Implicit World Modeling (\IWM{}) and Self-Reflection (\ICRIT{}) from \citet{zhang2025agentlearningearlyexperience,yu2025dynathinksynergizingreasoningacting}.
  % Using expert rollouts $(s_0, a^{*}_0, s_1, a^{*}_1, ...)$, these methods first augment them with alternative, non-optimal action-state pairs $(s_t, a'_t)$ generated by the target model $\pi_\theta$.
  % Then, SFT is used to either predict $s'_{t+1}$ directly or to predict synthesized reasoning trace generated by a strong LLM that constrasts expert actions with alternative non-optimal actions. 
  % % Finally, they use SFT to jointly train the model to learn the world (predict $s_{t+1}$ or the synthesized reasoning traces) and to learn the expert policy (predict $a^{*}_{t+1}$).
  % Finally, another round of SFT is continued from these checkpoints to directly learn the expert policy (predict $a^{*}_{t+1}$).
  Using expert rollouts $(s_0, a^{*}_0, s_1, a^{*}_1, ...)$, these methods first augment them with alternative, non-optimal action-state pairs $(s_t, a'_t)$ generated by the target model $\pi_\theta$.
  Then, either these data are converted to next-state prediction triplets $\left\langle s_{\le t}, a_t, s_{t+1} \right\rangle$ for WM learning, or a strong LLM is used to synthesize reasoning data (contrasting expert actions with alternative non-optimal actions) for reflection learning.
  Finally, these data are combined with the expert policy data (\ie{} predict $a^{*}_{t+1}$), and SFT is used to train on the combined dataset.
  Since these two methods heavily rely on expert rollouts, we also consider a simpler baseline that directly learns the expert policy using SFT (denoted as ``\distill{}'').
  For more implementation details, please refer to \Cref{subsec:alfworld_other_setup,subsec:tau2_other_setup}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Ablation Studies}
\label{subsec:ablation}

In \Cref{tbl:main_abl} we present an ablation study to investigate the contribution of different components in our \wmrlshort{}.
Specifically, we consider: (1) replacing our embedding-based reward with LLM-as-a-judge \citep{zheng2023judgingllmasajudgemtbenchchatbot}; (2) removing the data subsampling step which subsamples ``too easy'' samples, denoted as ``w/o subsample''; (3) removing the \wmrlshort{} training entirely, denoted as ``w/o training''.
For LLM-as-a-judge, we consider two variants: prompting the LLM to compare the generated $\hat{s}_{t+1}$ with the ground truth $s_{t+1}$ and return a \emph{real-valued reward} $r\in[0,1]$, allowing for partial credits. We denote this as ``w/ LLM-as-a-judge''. Alternatively, we prompt the LLM to return a \emph{binary reward} of either 0.0 or 1.0. We denote this as ``w/ bin(LLM-as-a-judge)''.
In both cases, we use Qwen-3-235B-A22B-Instruct \citep{yang2025qwen3technicalreport} as the judge model as it is a fast, strong, open-source LLM that can be hosted locally.


Results in \Cref{tbl:main_abl} show that all components of our method are important in improving model performance.
Additionally, we find that
(1) weaker models such as Qwen2.5-7B are more susceptible to data quality/noisy reward functions; (2) LLM-as-a-judge is unreliable and can sometimes be hacked during training (see \Cref{sec:More Details on Ablation Studies} for an example); and (3) subsampling ``easy'' training samples is beneficial to further improve model performance.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% We propose \wmrlshort{}, a scalable, self-supervised method that improves LLM-agents environment understanding and decision-making abilities prior to downstream policy RL training.
We propose \wmrlshort{}, a scalable, self-supervised method that enhances the environment understanding and decision-making ability of LLM-based agents prior to downstream RL with task-success reward.
% \wmrlshort{} learns from pure interaction data without any expert annotations or stronger LLMs, training the LLM as action-conditioned world models by aligning simulated next states produced by the model with realized next states observed from the environment in a pre-trained embedding space.
Without expert/strong LLM annotations or task-success signals, \wmrlshort{} trains the LLM as an action-conditioned world model by aligning the simulated next states with observed environment states in a pre-trained embedding space.
% We experiment on two long-horizon agent benchmarks, ALFWorld and $\tau^2$ Bench, and find \wmrlshort{} significantly improves model performance using pure interaction data.
We evaluate \wmrlshort{} on two long-horizon agent benchmarks, ALFWorld and $\tau^2$ Bench, and find significant performance gains while using only interaction data.
% When combined with task-success for policy RL training, our method outperforms direct policy RL on both benchmarks while matching the performance of training with expert data.
When combined with task-success rewards in policy RL, our method outperforms direct policy RL on both benchmarks and matches training with expert data.
% We believe our work opens up new avenues of scalable, self-supervised methods that can further advance the state of the art of LLM-based agents in the era of agentic RL.
We believe our work opens up new avenues for scalable, self-supervised training methods to further advance LLM-based agents in the era of agentic RL.


\section{Impact Statements}
This paper presents work that aims to advance the agentic capabilities of LLM-based agents through a scalable, self-supervised method.
While most LLM-based agent methods are not designed for unethical use, their applications and data collection processes may still pose risks of misuse.
In this work, we propose \shortmethod{}, which improves world modeling in LLM-based agents using interaction data without expert annotations or stronger LLMs, and is trained exclusively on established, isolated benchmarks without real-world impact.
We believe that developing guardrails, such as safety filters \citep{openai-content-filters,inan2023llamaguardllmbasedinputoutput,luo2025agraillifelongagentguardrail}, and using isolated environments like sandboxes \citep{agentinfrasandbox,pan2025training}, is essential for safe AI agent research.
We do not condone the use of \shortmethod{} or its constituent methods for any unlawful or morally unjust purposes.
