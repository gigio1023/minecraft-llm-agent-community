\section{\label{sec:intro}Introduction}

Neural architecture search (NAS) has been applied successfully to design model
architectures for image classification and language
modeling~\citep{nas,nas_dqn,bello2017neural,nas_module,nas_by_transformation}.
NAS however is computationally expensive and time consuming: for
example,~\cite{nas_module} use $450$ GPUs and train for $3$-$4$ days.
Meanwhile, using less resources tends to produce less compelling
results~\citep{deep_arc,nas_dqn}.

%Although less resource-intensive alternatives to NAS exist, these alternatives produce less compelling results~\citep{deep_arc,nas_dqn}.

The main computational bottleneck of NAS is the training of each child model to
convergence to measure its accuracy. We believe that it is very inefficient and
wasteful to train every child model and then throw away all the trained weights
even though the child models have much in common.

The goal of this work is to remove this inefficiency by enabling more sharing
between the child models. This idea is similar to the concept of weight
inheritance in neuro-evolution (e.g.,~\cite{Real17}). To understand our method,
we first need to understand the standard NAS. In standard
NAS~\citep{nas,nas_dqn}, an RNN controller is trained by policy gradient to
search for a good architecture, which is basically a computational graph. Our
observation is that all of the graphs, that NAS has iterated over, can be
viewed as sub-graphs of a larger graph. In other words, we can represent the
space of these graphs as a \emph{single} directed acyclic graph (DAG). As
illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:enas_dag}, a neural network architecture can be
found by taking a subset of edges in this DAG. This design is advantageous
because  it enables sharing parameters among all architectures in the search
space. %This design hence avoids the need to train from scratch every new
architecture found during the search process. 

Our method requires the training of a path within a large model together with the controller. The controller is trained with policy gradient to select a path that maximizes the expected reward on the validation set. Meanwhile the model corresponding to the selected path is trained to minimize a cross-entropy loss. In view of its efficiency, we name our method \textit{Efficient Neural Architecture Search} (\enasnospace).

ENAS is both theoretically sound and empirically efficient. Using Lyapunov functions~\citep{martingale_technique}, we can show that the training of \enas converges almost surely. Empirically, on CIFAR-10, \enas achieves the error rate of $2.89\%$, compared to $2.65\%$ by NAS. On Penn Treebank, \enas discovers an architecture that achieves the perplexity of $57.8$, which outperforms NAS's perplexity of $62.4$~\citep{nas}. In all of our experiments, \enas takes less than $16$ hours to train, whilst running on a single Nvidia GTX 1080Ti GPU. Compared to NAS, our method achieves a 10X reduction of search time and 100X reduction of computational resources.
