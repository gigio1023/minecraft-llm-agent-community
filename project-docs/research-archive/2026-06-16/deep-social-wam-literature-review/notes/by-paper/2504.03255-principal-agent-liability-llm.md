# Inherent and Emergent Liability Issues in LLM-Based Agentic Systems: A Principal-Agent Perspective

- title: Inherent and emergent liability issues in LLM-based agentic systems: a
  principal-agent perspective
- authors: Garry A. Gabison, R. Patrick Xian
- year: 2025
- venue/source: arXiv 2504.03255 (ACL-style template)
- urls: paper https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.03255 ; hf https://huggingface.co/papers/2504.03255 (verified)
- source availability: LaTeX read (`papers/latex/2504.03255/acl_latex_camred.tex`).

## Primary-source facts (what the paper states)

- Frame: analyze liability for delegated LLM agents through principal-agent theory
  (PAT; Eisenhardt 1989, Laffont-Martimort 2002). A principal (human or company)
  delegates a task to an agent under a mutual agreement; PAT studies the relationship
  under information asymmetry, monitoring, and incentives. LLM agents cannot satisfy
  all criteria of a "normal" PAT agent, creating an "agency gap" that yields excess
  unpredictable actions.
- Key PAT machinery applied:
  - Information asymmetry between principal and agent, "usually resolved through
    monitoring and incentive realignment via reward or punishment". Monitoring an LLM
    agent requires designs that let the principal observe and understand what the
    agent is doing; quantifying misalignment remains hard.
  - Incomplete-contracts theory (cites Hadfield-Menell-Hadfield 2019, mapping AI
    alignment onto the principal-agent problem and incomplete contracts).
  - Least-cost-avoidance (Calabresi): assign liability to whoever can avoid the
    accident at lowest cost. Coase Theorem: allocation of liability is best left to
    contracts, but legislators/courts must first clarify rights and liabilities.
  - Legal liability families (US): product liability (design/manufacturing/warning
    defect; often strict liability) vs service liability (negligence inquiry:
    negligent hiring, negligent supervision, vicarious liability). Software straddles
    product and service; as agents become more autonomous they move toward service.
- Single-agent inherent issues: liability from flawed agency (LLM agents lack
  authentic consent), risky delegation (principals avoid delegating high-stakes
  tasks; risk reduced by repeated feedback), task misdelegation (negligent selection),
  compromised oversight (negligent supervision; supervision level scales with risk).
  Behavioral traits (e.g. sycophancy, deception) can upend the principal-agent
  asymmetry so the principal cannot reliably monitor or give informative feedback.
- Multi-agent issues: once an agent selects other agents for subtasks, that selection
  shifts toward negligent-supervision theory; liability is finalized only by
  understanding agent-environment interactions (a sociotechnical-system view).
- Motivates technical-governance method development along interpretability/behavior
  evaluation, reward and conflict management, and detection/fail-safe mechanisms, to
  support auditing, tracing, transparency, and liability attribution.

## Interpretation (my reading, labeled inference)

- This is the principal-agent / contract-theory lens on the lane's "who is
  accountable when a delegated agent imposes a cost" question, and it connects the
  formal contract papers (2208.10469, 2309.09801) to a governance-and-attribution
  use rather than an equilibrium-selection use. For the repo, the core transferable
  idea is the PAT remedy for information asymmetry: monitoring + incentive realignment
  via reward/punishment, which is exactly the sanction/repair + obligation-ledger
  machinery the repo already has, here justified from law-and-economics.
- "Liability is finalized only through agent-environment interactions" is a direct
  argument for the repo's verifier: attribution of a cost to an actor requires a
  ground-truth record of what physically happened, not the actor's self-report. The
  repo's evidence ledger is the attribution substrate this paper says is missing.
- The agency gap (LLM agents lack genuine consent/rationality) is an honesty caveat:
  the repo must not model an actor's obligation as felt or consented; it is a
  behavioral bookkeeping fact (the Blau/Coleman credit-slip framing in the sociology
  theme), which is exactly the line this paper draws for legal agency too.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Mechanically useful:
  - The PAT decomposition (information asymmetry -> monitoring + incentive realignment;
    negligent selection/supervision) is a vocabulary for the repo's delegation and
    obligation scenarios: who delegated, what monitoring evidence exists, who bears
    the cost when the delegated action imposes one.
  - "Monitoring + tracing for attribution" is the law-and-economics case for the
    repo's evidence ledger and audit trail (the same artifact the Institutional-AI
    governance log provides).
- Research contribution (where the repo adds):
  - This is an analysis/position paper (legal frameworks, hypotheticals), not an
    experiment; it provides framing, not a measurable result. The repo could turn its
    monitoring-and-attribution prescriptions into scored predictions: given verified
    evidence, predict which actor a cost should be attributed to (an Institutional/
    Material-layer attribution task).
  - It is grounded in US tort/agency law, not in a physical substrate; the repo
    grounds the delegated action and its imposed cost in verified material deltas.
