# Wow, wo, val! - the IDM Turing Test, where visually convincing world models collapse to near zero

- **title**: Wow, wo, val! A Comprehensive Embodied World Model Evaluation Turing Test
- **authors**: Chun-Kai Fan, Xiaowei Chi, Xiaozhu Ju, et al. (Peking University; Beijing Innovation Center of Humanoid Robotics; HKUST)
- **year**: 2026
- **venue/source**: arXiv (CVPR 2026 template)
- **arxiv_id**: 2601.04137
- **urls**: paper https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.04137 ; LaTeX at `papers/latex/2601.04137/`
- **source availability**: latex (full, deep-read)

This is the survey's `fan2026wow` action-plausibility benchmark, deep-read here for
its concrete numbers. It is the strongest single datapoint for "visual fidelity is
not control."

## Primary-source facts (what the paper states or shows)

What it introduced and why it matters (plain): a benchmark, WoW-World-Eval, that
judges video-generation models AS embodied world models, and two "Turing tests"
that ask whether a generated robot-manipulation video is good enough to (a) fool a
human and (b) be executed by a real robot. The headline finding is that these two
diverge sharply: models that fool humans on appearance still fail real execution.

Setup:
- 609 human-cleaned robot-manipulation samples (each = initial image + instruction
  + ground-truth video + annotated keypoints), drawn from public robot data
  (RoboMIND, DROID), in-house trajectories, and AI-generated out-of-distribution
  images. Task framing = image+text -> video (predict the future given a state and
  an action).
- 22 metrics across five orthogonal dimensions: Perception Understanding,
  Decision-making/Planning, Predictive Reasoning (physics), Interactive Execution,
  Generative Generalization.

Two Turing tests:
- **Human Turing Test**: two-alternative forced-choice (2AFC) from psychophysics.
  13 participants try to tell real from generated; the "deceive-human ratio" is the
  fraction of generated videos that fool them. The benchmark's Overall score
  correlates with this ratio at Pearson r = 0.679; the strongest sub-correlations
  are **Video Quality r = 0.874** and **Physical Law r = 0.753** (humans are
  convinced by appearance and surface physics).
- **Inverse Dynamics Model (IDM) Turing Test** (the important one): an IDM
  (GC-IDM) trained ONLY on real-world execution sequences is run on each generated
  video to infer an action sequence; those actions are then executed on a real
  robot over 9 manipulation tasks; the metric is real-world success rate. The logic:
  if a generated video carries enough true action information, an IDM trained on
  reality should be able to "read out" executable actions.

The collapse (exact numbers, Section "Turing Test", Table "Execution Accuracy"):
- High-appearance models nearly fail the IDM test: **Kling 9.88%, Hailuo 2.47%**,
  and "early open-source models all perform near 0%" (CogVideoX, Cosmos-Predict1,
  Wan2.1 achieve no success).
- Only models trained with real-robot data transfer: **WoW-wan 40.74%**,
  WoW-cosmos2 18.52%.
- Verbatim: "most high-scoring models on WoW-World-Eval still fail this test ...
  revealing that **visual realism alone is insufficient for embodied execution**."
  And: "high benchmark scores do not automatically translate to real-world
  competence."

Other dimension findings:
- **Long-horizon planning is the worst dimension**: the planning-DAG score occupies
  "a markedly lower and more compressed range than others"; the best is Hailuo at
  **17.27** (open-source best Cosmos-Predict2 at 13.41). The authors call planning
  "a primary bottleneck for current world models."
- Best physical-law consistency is Kling at 68.02; open-source WoW-cosmos2 66.18.
- Stated motivation, verbatim: "a model may score well on conventional video
  metrics yet produce physically impossible or contextually incorrect predictions
  in robotic scenarios ... standard video-quality scores correlate poorly with
  human judgments in embodied settings."

## Interpretation (my reading, labeled inference)

- This is the empirical anchor for the survey's "Action Plausibility" axis and for
  the lane's "fidelity != control" open problem. The numeric gap is stark: a model
  can fool humans (high Video-Quality correlation) and still hand an IDM actions
  that fail almost every time on a real robot. The IDM Turing Test is the operational
  way to catch a video that "looks right" but encodes no real action information.
- For this repo the transposition is direct but the repo's design already passes a
  structural version of this test for free. The IDM Turing Test asks "can the
  generated future be turned back into a real, executed action that succeeds?" The
  repo never generates pixels; its actions are selected by the LLM and executed by
  Mineflayer under a verifier. So the repo's analogue of "did the imagined future
  carry real action information" is answered by the verifier directly: an advisory
  predicted social-material delta is scored against the verified delta the runtime
  actually produced. The repo should keep the same discipline this paper enforces:
  never accept a plausible-looking prediction as success; require it to match a
  verified world change (this mirrors `benchmark-validity-and-evaluation.md`'s
  "anchor every score to a verified world delta").

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful**: the IDM-Turing pattern (run a reality-trained reader on a
  generated/predicted future and require real executability) as the conceptual
  template for "prediction must cash out in verified action, not appearance." For
  the repo the verifier already plays the IDM's role at zero cost.
- **What would be overclaim**: treating the 40.74% / near-0% numbers as relevant to
  a social-material predictor. They are robot-manipulation video numbers. Cite them
  as the canonical demonstration of the fidelity-vs-control gap, not as a bound on
  this repo.
