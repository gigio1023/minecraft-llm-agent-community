## Plain-language: what it introduced and why it mattered

This paper builds a **hierarchical model-based RL (HMBRL)** system the "obvious" way: a *stack* of
world models, each simulating the environment at a coarser time scale, and a *stack* of agents that
talk top-down by proposing goals to the agent below. A deliberate design choice is a **static,
environment-agnostic temporal abstraction** (the high level always acts every fixed number of steps,
independent of behavior), which lets every level train at once and keeps the abstract action space
small and low-dimensional.

The honest headline result: despite a careful design, the hierarchical world models **did not
outperform traditional (flat) methods** on final episode returns. The paper then does the valuable
thing, it diagnoses *why*. The central failure is **model exploitation on the abstract level**: the
high-level agent learns to exploit inaccuracies in the high-level (coarse) world model, planning
actions that look good in the imperfect abstract model but do not pay off in reality. The paper argues
this is the key obstacle the field must overcome and lays out the open problem rather than burying the
negative result.

Why it mattered for this lane: it is recent (2024), it is explicitly about *hierarchical world models*
(the exact intersection the original query lives in), and it is candid that the intersection of
hierarchy and world models "still lacks wide adoption" and "produces highly complex and difficult to
control systems." It is the single best citation for "hierarchical world models are promising but
unproven," which the theme file must state plainly.

## Definitions (first use)

- **Hierarchical model-based RL (HMBRL)**: the intersection of HRL (temporal abstraction) and MBRL
  (learn a world model for sample efficiency); here, a hierarchy of world models plus a hierarchy of
  agents.
- **Temporal abstraction (static vs variable)**: how long a high-level decision lasts. *Static* =
  fixed number of low-level steps, independent of behavior (used here, enables concurrent training).
  *Variable* = the high level decides when to stop, which is more flexible but, if unregularized,
  "tends to degenerate to either one-step (i.e. no) temporal abstraction or to abstractions that span
  the whole sequence" (`main.tex`).
- **Model exploitation**: a policy optimized against an imperfect model finds actions that score well
  *in the model* but fail in reality, usually by exploiting states the model predicts wrongly. Here it
  occurs specifically on the *abstract* (coarse, high-level) world model.
- **Moving-target problem**: if a higher level is built on top of a lower level that is still changing,
  the higher level's learning target keeps shifting (the same non-stationarity HIRO patched). The
  static abstraction here is partly chosen to sidestep this.

## Primary-source facts

- "While HMBRL has great potential, it still lacks wide adoption" and "the HMBRL landscape is less
  densely populated compared to model-free HRL, as combining the paradigms of HRL and MBRL produces
  highly complex and difficult to control systems" (`main.tex`).
- The system: hierarchical world models at multiple temporal-abstraction levels train a stack of
  agents that "communicate in a top-down manner by proposing goals to their subordinate agents," with a
  static, environment-agnostic temporal abstraction enabling concurrent training and low-dimensional
  abstract actions.
- Result: "our HMBRL approach did not outperform traditional methods in terms of final episode
  returns," but "it successfully facilitated decision making across two levels of abstraction using
  compact, low dimensional abstract actions" (`main.tex` abstract).
- Diagnosis: "A central challenge ... is model exploitation on the abstract level of our world model
  stack," examined in depth with directions for future work (`main.tex` abstract).
- Background it states cleanly: the options framework fixes action sequences as temporally-extended
  "macro actions," and "the concept of options seamlessly transitions into the concept of skills";
  "reliably identifying a problem's subcomponents and crafting meaningful abstractions ... is still a
  topic of ongoing research"; "Finding expressive and diverse temporal abstractions without manual
  intervention or the injection of domain specific knowledge is still an open research question."

## Interpretation (labeled inference)

- This paper is the empirical counterweight to Director's success: Director showed a *two-level*
  hierarchical world model can beat flat RL on specific sparse-reward tasks, but this 2024 study shows
  that a *stacked* hierarchical world model, built carefully, can fail to beat flat methods and that the
  abstract-level model is where the failure concentrates. Read together they bound the field's maturity:
  hierarchy in world models helps in some regimes and hurts in others, and *deep* multi-level
  hierarchical world models are not yet a solved, reliable tool.
- For this repo, the abstract-level model-exploitation finding is the strongest argument for the
  advisory rule. The repo never lets a learned model *act*; it only predicts and is scored against a
  deterministic verifier. The exact failure this paper documents (a high-level model being exploited by
  a high-level planner) is structurally impossible if the higher-level model is advisory and the
  verifier owns truth. This is a research-literature justification for a project policy that was
  previously argued mostly on first principles.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful**: as a *design constraint*, not code. It tells the repo two things: (1) keep
  the higher (more abstract / institutional) layers of the WAM *advisory and verifier-scored*, because
  abstract models are exactly where exploitation bites; (2) a static, fixed-interval temporal
  abstraction is a defensible simplification (it avoids the option-collapse and moving-target problems)
  when the goal is observation/prediction rather than peak control performance.
- **Research contribution (NOT this repo's)**: building and beating SOTA with a stacked HMBRL agent is
  the authors' research problem; the repo does not build an HMBRL controller. It is cited for the
  negative result and the diagnosis.

## Relevance to this repo (one line)

The best recent evidence that hierarchical world models are promising but unproven (a careful stacked
HMBRL did not beat flat RL, failing via abstract-level model exploitation), which both tempers
institutional-layer claims and justifies keeping the higher WAM layers advisory and verifier-scored.
