## Primary-source facts

- The problem: deep RL is evaluated by comparing POINT ESTIMATES (mean or median normalized score)
  aggregated over a handful of training runs, ignoring the statistical uncertainty from using a
  finite number of runs. The shift to compute-heavy benchmarks pushed the field to 3-10 runs per task
  (5 runs on 50+ Atari games already needs >1000 GPU training days), which "exacerbates the
  statistical uncertainty in point estimates."
- The core argument, verbatim spirit: "any performance estimate based on a finite number of runs is a
  random variable" and should be treated as such. A reported point estimate "evades the question:
  would similar findings be obtained with new independent runs under different random conditions?"
- The headline empirical demonstration (Atari 100k case study): re-analysing published results with
  proper statistics reveals "substantial discrepancies between conclusions drawn from point estimates
  alone versus a more thorough statistical analysis." The sampling distribution of the MEDIAN,
  computed by resampling 100,000 subsets of runs, shows reported point estimates "severely
  overestimate or underestimate the expected median." 95 percent CIs for the median remain wide even
  with 50 runs.
- The three recommended tools (Table of recommendations):
  1. Report INTERVAL ESTIMATES via STRATIFIED BOOTSTRAP confidence intervals, not point estimates.
  2. Report PERFORMANCE PROFILES (score distributions): plot the empirical tail distribution
     Fhat(tau) = fraction of runs scoring above tau, over all runs across tasks. Statistically
     unbiased, robust to outliers, readable at any percentile, and need fewer runs for small
     uncertainty.
  3. Use the INTERQUARTILE MEAN (IQM) as the aggregate metric: the mean over the middle 50 percent of
     combined runs. It is robust to outliers (unlike the mean, which a few outlier tasks dominate) yet
     far more statistically efficient than the median (which "requires a large number of runs to claim
     improvements" and is unchanged by zero scores on nearly half the tasks). Supplement with
     PROBABILITY OF IMPROVEMENT and OPTIMALITY GAP.
- On significance: the paper recommends CIs and effect sizes and explicitly DISCOURAGES dichotomous
  statistical-significance tests (p < 0.05) because of their binary nature and common
  misinterpretations (e.g. absence of a significant result is not evidence of no effect). When CIs
  overlap, compute CIs for the score DIFFERENCE rather than eyeballing overlap.

## Interpretation (my inference, labeled)

- This is the methodology spine for evaluating a STOCHASTIC predictor on FEW seeds, which is exactly
  the regime the repo lives in: LLM-driven social runs are expensive, so the repo will have a handful
  of runs per scenario, not hundreds. The repo must not report a single mean delta-accuracy number; it
  must report IQM with stratified-bootstrap CIs and a performance profile across scenarios/seeds, or
  it will draw the same false conclusions rliable documents.
- The "point estimate is a random variable" frame applies directly to any social-WAM metric: a single
  "trust-prediction accuracy = 0.72" computed over 5 runs is uninterpretable without an interval. The
  repo's verifier makes the per-transition label cheap, but RUNS are still expensive and stochastic
  (LLM sampling, partner behavior, world seed), so the run-level uncertainty rliable targets is the
  binding constraint, not the label cost.
- rliable's anti-median stance matters for social metrics with many zeros: if a predictor gets many
  social deltas exactly right (delta = no change) and a few hard ones wrong, the median can hide the
  hard-case failures; IQM and the performance profile expose them.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Mechanically useful:
  - The `rliable` library is directly usable: feed it the per-scenario, per-seed delta-prediction
    scores and get IQM, stratified-bootstrap CIs, performance profiles, probability-of-improvement,
    and optimality-gap out of the box.
  - The recommendation set is a ready reporting checklist for any model-comparison claim ("predictor A
    is better calibrated than predictor B") the repo wants to make defensibly.
- Research contribution (not the repo's to claim): rliable's contribution is the few-run RL evaluation
  methodology and the Atari re-analysis. The repo reuses the methodology; the novelty is applying it to
  verifier-grounded social-material prediction, where the "task suite" is a held-out scenario set and
  the per-task score is delta-prediction accuracy / calibration against the verifier.
