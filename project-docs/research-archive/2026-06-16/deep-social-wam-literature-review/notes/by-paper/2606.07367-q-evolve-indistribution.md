## Primary-source facts (what the paper states/shows)

- **Research question**: how to make an LLM agent self-evolve *stably* over long-horizon tasks with sparse, delayed rewards, without expensive human process labels and without the loop being destabilized by distribution shift?
- **Model architecture / loop**: Q-Evolve runs iterative "in-distribution evolving loops." Each iteration:
    - learns an **in-distribution critic** (value function) from a *hybrid off-policy dataset* mixing expert demonstrations with agent-generated trajectories, using a weighted Implicit Q-Learning (IQL) objective (upweighting steps from successful trajectories and steps near episode termination);
    - derives **step-wise process rewards** from the critic via Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE), "filling in" missing intermediate rewards via Bellman propagation - no environment backtracking and no human annotation;
    - updates the policy via behavior-proximal policy optimization (with a KL regularizer to preserve base language ability), keeping each update inside the support of the hybrid offline data.
- **Action space**: text actions in interactive text environments (AlfWorld, WebShop, ScienceWorld).
- **Observation / state representation**: text environment observations.
- **Dataset shape**: hybrid - expert demonstrations + agent-collected trajectories, refreshed each evolving iteration with new experience.
- **Training objective**: weighted IQL for the critic; behavior-proximal policy optimization for the policy; the two co-evolve with the data.
- **Evaluation protocol + metrics**: AlfWorld, WebShop, ScienceWorld; reports gains over strong baselines in sample efficiency, robustness, and overall task performance.
- **The central caution this paper raises (key for this lane)**: process-level reward signals are **distribution-sensitive** - a learned reward (PRM/critic) is only reliable on the state-action distribution it was trained on. As the evolving policy drifts, it "generates unseen actions even when the starting state is sampled from the PRM's training set," and dynamic environments push the agent into out-of-distribution states, "leading to a catastrophic distribution shift that invalidates the PRM's feedback." Q-Evolve's whole design (in-distribution constraint, hybrid expert grounding) is a fix for this failure.
- **Code/weights**: project page exists; code availability not confirmed from source. Reproducibility: partial / claim-with-page.

## Interpretation (flagged as inference)

- Q-Evolve is the lane's sharpest statement of *why a self-improving loop driven by a learned scorer can go wrong even when nobody is cheating*: the scorer silently becomes unreliable off-distribution. This is distinct from reward hacking (an adversarial agent gaming the verifier, see DGM 2505.22954 and 2604.15149); it is a *passive* failure of the success signal as the loop moves.
- The mitigation - keep updates inside a trusted distribution and anchor with expert/external data - rhymes with SAIL's "keep the internet prior" and the escaping-collapse result (2510.16657) that an external information source is what prevents degeneration.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful for this repo**: the warning is the borrow. If an autoresearch loop ever uses a *learned* social/material WAM as its scorer (instead of the runtime verifier), Q-Evolve says that scorer's reliability decays exactly where the loop pushes the policy - the new, unseen social situations. Concrete guard: keep the **runtime verifier (physical truth) as the success signal** wherever possible, and treat any learned advisory WAM score as in-distribution-only; flag/abstain when the current state is far from what the WAM was calibrated on.
- **Research contribution NOT to copy**: Q-Evolve's claim is stable RL self-evolution via in-distribution critic optimization. The repo does not train critics/weights. Borrow the *distribution-shift diagnosis and the in-distribution-trust principle*, not the IQL/BPPO machinery.
- **Where it complicates the thesis**: the thesis assumes the verifier-scored transitions are a sound success signal. Q-Evolve shows a *learned* signal is sound only in-distribution; a self-improving loop systematically explores out-of-distribution, which is precisely where a learned scorer fails. The repo's runtime verifier (deterministic checks on physical state) is far more robust to this than a learned WAM scorer would be - which is an argument *for* keeping the verifier, not the WAM, as the loop's ground truth.

## WAM layer(s) informed

Method-level, cross-layer, but most pointed at the Social/Material layers, where one might be tempted to use a *learned* WAM as the scorer. The lesson: a learned scorer is trustworthy only in-distribution; the open-ended loop is out-of-distribution by construction. Prefer the verifier on physical truth as ground truth.
