# SimBench: Benchmarking the Ability of Large Language Models to Simulate Human Behaviors

- title: SimBench: Benchmarking the Ability of Large Language Models to Simulate Human Behaviors
- authors: Tiancheng Hu et al.
- year: 2026 (ICLR 2026 submission)
- arxiv_id: 2510.17516
- urls: paper https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.17516 ; code https://github.com/pitehu/SimBench_release ; data https://huggingface.co/datasets/pitehu/SimBench ; project http://simbench.tiancheng.hu/
- source read: LaTeX (papers/latex/2510.17516/iclr2026_conference.tex), abstract + intro + creating + experimental setup

## Primary-source facts

- Research question: how well do LLMs simulate human behavior, measured in a *standardized, reproducible* way (vs the fragmented patchwork of bespoke tasks/metrics)?
- Construction: unify **20 datasets** (moral dilemmas, economic games, psychological assessments) across 130+ countries; harmonize into single-turn multiple-choice items; aggregate to **group-level probability distributions** (the simulation target = ground-truth response distribution to predict). 10,930,271 question-group targets; two splits — SimBenchPop (7,167 cases, broad populations) and SimBenchGrouped (6,343 cases, specific demographic groups).
- Elicitation: base models → first-token logit probabilities; instruction-tuned → verbalized distributions ("Option A: 30%, Option B: 70%"). 45 LLMs, 0.5B–405B.
- Metric: fidelity of predicted vs true group response distribution (total-variation based), scored to 100.
- Findings (six RQs):
  - **Best LLMs reach only "meaningful but modest" fidelity: top score 40.80/100.**
  - Performance scales **log-linearly with model size** but is **NOT improved by inference-time compute** (more "thinking" doesn't help simulation).
  - **Alignment-simulation tradeoff**: instruction tuning's mode-seeking objective improves low-entropy (consensus) questions but degrades high-entropy (pluralistic) ones — decomposed into a beneficial instruction-following effect and a harmful entropy-reduction effect.
  - Models struggle most with **religious/ideological** demographic groups.
  - Simulation ability correlates most strongly with **knowledge-intensive reasoning (MMLU-Pro, r=0.939)** — i.e. with general knowledge, not anything social-specific.
- Explicit scoping caveat: SimBench predicts *static group-level response distributions*; the authors state this is "a foundational proxy" and that "full human simulation inherently includes interactive and complex dynamics not fully captured by static response distributions."

## Interpretation (my inference, labeled)

- SimBench is the strongest quantitative anchor for "plausibility ≠ validity": the best simulators score 40.8/100 even on the *easiest* form of the task (matching a marginal distribution, no interaction). This caps optimism about any richer interactive claim.
- Two findings transfer as direct guidance for the repo's model-comparison story: (a) "more reasoning/compute doesn't improve simulation" (same as S3AP's stronger-agent caveat and MineExplorer's thinking-mode caveat) — so the repo should not assume a bigger/reasoning model yields better social trajectories, and should measure social behavior as behavior; (b) the alignment-simulation tradeoff = the concrete mechanism behind "mean alignment can hide variance collapse / homogenized behavior" — instruction-tuned models converge to the consensus answer and lose diversity.
- SimBench's own scoping admission (static distributions ≠ interactive dynamics) is the repo's opening: the repo is on the interactive, world-grounded side that SimBench explicitly does not cover.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Mechanically useful:
  - The standardization discipline (harmonize, report one comparable fidelity score) is a model for how the repo should report cross-model results so they are comparable across seeds/models.
  - The entropy framing (consensus vs pluralistic items) suggests the repo should track whether models collapse to a single "default" social move vs producing varied, scenario-appropriate responses.
- Avoid / overclaim:
  - Do not equate a high distribution-matching score with valid social behavior; SimBench shows even that ceiling is ~41/100 and is not interactive.
  - Do not assume scaling model size/compute solves social fidelity.

## WAM layer(s) informed

- Validity instrument across all layers. Mainly bounds claims: it tells the repo what a *defensible* fidelity statement looks like and that the bar for "matches humans" is low even in the simplest setting.
