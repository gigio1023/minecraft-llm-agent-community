- authors: Ariel Flint Ashery, Luca Maria Aiello, Andrea Baronchelli
- year: 2024 (arXiv); Science Advances 2025 (vol 11, eadu9368)
- venue/source: Science Advances 2025; arXiv 2410.08948
- arxiv_id: 2410.08948
- urls: paper https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.08948 ; journal https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.adu9368
- source availability: LaTeX (`papers/latex/2410.08948/resubmit_sci_adv_final.tex`), read abstract + setup + emergence/bias/critical-mass results.

## Primary-source facts (what the paper states)

- Research question: do *universal social conventions* spontaneously emerge in
  decentralized populations of LLM agents that only have local pairwise incentives,
  the way they do in humans? Three sub-questions: (1) spontaneous emergence, (2) effect
  of individual bias, (3) robustness to a committed adversarial minority (critical mass).
- Method: the **naming game** (a standard model of convention formation). At each step two
  random agents from a population of N each output a "name" (a letter) from a pool of W
  options. They are rewarded if their names MATCH and penalized if not; the score change is
  symmetric. Crucially there is **no incentive for global consensus**, only local
  coordination, and the prompt never tells the agent it is part of a population. Each agent
  has a memory of its last H interactions (own choice, partner choice, success/failure,
  cumulative score). Tested on Llama-2-70B-Chat, Llama-3-70B, Llama-3.1-70B, Claude-3.5-Sonnet.
  Defaults N=24, W=10, H=5; robustness checked up to N=200 and W=26.
- Results:
  1. **Spontaneous global convention**: populations undergo a disorder-to-order
     (symmetry-breaking) transition to a single shared name, despite only local incentives.
     A theoretical naming-game model fits the LLM dynamics. Consensus by ~population round 15
     for all but the weakest model, and the convergence speed is comparable even at N=200.
  2. **Collective bias without individual bias**: which name wins is neither uniform nor
     deterministic. Some conventions are far more likely to be adopted. This collective bias
     can emerge even when individual agents (tested in isolation, first interaction) are
     statistically unbiased; it arises from the *interaction dynamics* themselves. (When the
     full alphabet including "A" is available, individual agents do show a strong prior for "A".)
     Microdynamics: an agent that succeeds keeps its name ~99.4% of the time (win-stay); an
     agent that fails switches ~97.3% of the time (lose-shift); asymmetric memory-state
     responses by the third interaction produce a "strong" vs "weak" convention split.
  3. **Critical mass / tipping point**: a small committed minority that always pushes a novel
     alternative can overturn an established convention once it reaches a threshold. The paper
     situates this in critical-mass theory: models suggest 10-40%, a controlled human
     experiment supports ~25%, and real-world observations range widely (some as low as 0.3%).

## Interpretation (my reading, labeled inference)

- This is the cornerstone "emergent norms/conventions in LLM populations" source for the
  lane. It is the LLM-population analogue of the human naming-game and convention literature
  (Lewis, Bicchieri; the wave-1 sociology theme owns the human-theory side). It shows that
  norm-like regularities arise bottom-up from local LLM interactions, which is exactly the
  Institutional-WAM phenomenon the project wants to *predict* (will a convention form? what
  will it be? how robust is it to a newcomer?).
- The collective-bias result is a sharp caution for the project: a settlement-level
  convention can be biased even when no individual actor is, so an Institutional WAM
  prediction must be a property of the *population dynamics*, not summed from individuals.
- The committed-minority/critical-mass result is the formal counterpart to GovSim's
  (2404.16698) greedy-newcomer probe: it quantifies when a minority flips a norm.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Mechanically useful for this repo:
  - The naming-game protocol (local pairwise coordination, memory of last H interactions,
    win-stay/lose-shift) is a minimal, runnable design for *observing* convention emergence
    among the repo's actors, e.g. a shared label for a worksite, a tool-sharing custom, or a
    storage-location convention, which the repo could log and an Institutional WAM could
    predict.
  - The critical-mass framing gives a concrete robustness test: introduce K committed actors
    pushing a different practice and measure the threshold at which the settlement convention
    flips (the repo's `weak_commons` dispute scenarios are natural hosts).
  - Win-stay/lose-shift is a cheap baseline model of how an actor updates a convention from
    success/failure, useful as a non-LLM sanity baseline for WAM predictions.
- What this repo should avoid / overclaim risk:
  - The naming game is an ABSTRACT coordination game with no physical substrate, the same
    limitation as GovSim. In Minecraft a "convention" must be anchored to verifiable behavior
    (who actually uses which chest, who actually repairs the shared furnace), not to a declared
    label, or it regresses to dialogue plausibility.
  - Do NOT over-read "AI agents form a society": the result is convention formation in a
    naming game, the proto-social end of the repo's ladder, not settlement or society. Avoid
    civilization-scale framing (the repo's explicit caution).
  - Collective bias means a settlement WAM cannot infer the convention from individual priors;
    do not claim per-actor predictions compose into a correct group prediction.

## WAM layer(s) informed

- **Institutional / Settlement WAM** (convention emergence, norm robustness, tipping points).
- **Social WAM** secondarily (the local win-stay/lose-shift coordination is dyadic expectation
  formation).
- **Validity** (the collective-bias-without-individual-bias finding is a methodological warning
  for how group-level predictions must be evaluated).

## Reproducibility

- Partial: protocol, parameters, and four model families are specified, and a theoretical
  naming-game model is provided; results depend on specific LLM snapshots (including one
  closed model). Transfers as protocol + theoretical baseline; exact convergence numbers are
  model-version dependent.
