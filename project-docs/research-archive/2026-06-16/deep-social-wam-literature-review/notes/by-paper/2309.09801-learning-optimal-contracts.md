# Learning Optimal Contracts: How to Exploit Small Action Spaces

- title: Learning Optimal Contracts: How to Exploit Small Action Spaces
- authors: Francesco Bacchiocchi, Matteo Castiglioni, Alberto Marchesi, Nicola Gatti
  (Politecnico di Milano)
- year: 2023 (arXiv); ICLR 2024
- venue/source: arXiv 2309.09801
- urls: paper https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.09801 ; hf https://huggingface.co/papers/2309.09801 (verified)
- source availability: LaTeX read (`papers/latex/2309.09801/`: abstract.tex,
  introduction.tex, preliminaries.tex).

## Primary-source facts (what the paper states)

- Object: the hidden-action principal-agent problem. A principal commits to a
  contract, an outcome-dependent payment scheme defining a payment for every outcome,
  to induce an agent to take a costly, unobservable action that stochastically yields
  outcomes (which determine the principal's reward). The principal cannot observe the
  action, only the realized outcome.
- Generalization studied: the principal repeatedly interacts with the agent over many
  rounds and has NO prior information about the agent (costs, outcome distributions per
  action). It must learn an (approximately) optimal contract by observing only the
  per-round realized outcome. This is the sample-complexity / online-learning version
  of contract design.
- Main results (stated):
  - An algorithm that, when the number of agent actions is constant, learns an
    approximately-optimal contract with high probability in a number of rounds that is
    polynomial in the size of the outcome space. This solves an open problem of Zhu et
    al. 2022 (whose general bound grows almost linearly in T when the number of
    outcomes m is large).
  - The same algorithm yields a tilde-O(T^{4/5}) regret bound in the online setting
    where the principal maximizes cumulative utility, improving prior regret bounds.
- Technical mechanism: the algorithm "approximately identifies" a covering of the
  contract space into best-response regions (sets of contracts where a given agent
  action is a best response), refining upper/lower bounds until they coincide. Because
  the principal observes only a stochastically-determined outcome, not the action, it
  cannot identify which action was played; it overcomes this with "meta-actions"
  (groups of agent actions with similar outcome distributions) that are discovered
  online, restarting the covering each time a new meta-action appears.
- Applications named: crowdsourcing platforms, blockchain smart contracts, healthcare.

## Interpretation (my reading, labeled inference)

- This is the formal mechanism-design backbone behind the applied contract papers in
  this lane (2208.10469 contracts-mitigate-social-dilemmas; the contract-as-obligation
  framing). It states precisely the hard structure the repo's obligation/credit ledger
  lives in: the principal (or the obligation holder) observes outcomes (verified
  deltas), not the counterparty's hidden action or intent, and must reason about
  incentives from observable outcomes alone. That outcome-only observability is exactly
  the repo's verifier regime.
- The "contract = outcome-dependent payment" definition is the formal version of the
  repo's credit slip: an obligation that pays out (trust up, debt cleared) contingent
  on a verified outcome (the borrowed tool returned), not contingent on the
  counterparty's unobservable intention. The honesty point the theory makes for free:
  you can only contract on, and only learn from, observable outcomes, the same reason
  the repo refuses to score internal mental state.
- The online-learning result is suggestive (labeled inference): an advisory WAM that
  must predict whether an obligation will be honored is in a structurally similar
  position to a principal learning a contract from outcomes only, so the sample
  complexity of getting such predictions right is bounded by how much observable
  outcome evidence the verifier supplies, not by access to intent.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Mechanically useful:
  - The hidden-action / outcome-only formalism is the right model for the repo's
    obligation prediction: condition obligations and their fulfillment on verifiable
    outcomes, never on hidden action or intent.
  - The best-response-region covering and meta-action grouping are an existence proof
    that contracts can be learned from outcomes alone with provable guarantees, useful
    if the repo ever wants to predict which obligation structures elicit cooperation.
- Research contribution (where the repo adds / where this stops):
  - This is pure theory in an abstract outcome space; there is no physical substrate,
    no multi-actor commons, and the principal ENFORCES payment. The repo neither
    enforces payment nor learns a contract to deploy; it predicts and scores whether a
    voluntary obligation was kept against verifier evidence. So the transferable
    content is the formalism and the outcome-only-observability discipline, not the
    algorithm as a runtime component.
  - The literature stops well short of the repo's instance: no verifier-grounded,
    physical, multi-actor obligation ledger appears here; that grounding is the repo's
    surface, not a citable result.
