## Primary-source facts (what the paper states)

- CausaLab is an environment for evaluating whether an LLM agent can do **interactive causal discovery**: run its own experiments (interventions) and recover a hidden causal mechanism, not just predict an answer.
- Episode setup: a hidden Structural Causal Model (SCM) `M = (U, V, F, P(U))` is sampled per episode (DAG over observable properties O plus a target Y = `frequency`; structural equations are linear or quadratic). The agent sees prior measurement records, may intervene on a controllable subset of properties on a "manipulator crystal," observes results, then must predict the held-out `frequency` of a "reactor crystal" governed by the SAME SCM but with different property values. Because values differ, the agent cannot copy an observed answer; it must infer a transferable mechanism.
- Interventions are **shift-style** (soft): setting a controllable variable shifts its intercept while keeping parent contributions, rather than hard `do(X=v)` that severs incoming edges. Some graph families add an unobserved disturbance H resampled after each intervention (tests distinguishing stable mechanism from post-intervention noise).
- The agent emits a DSL hypothesis (directed graph + `frequency` equation + coefficients) at each step, giving a trajectory of evolving hypotheses.
- **Dual evaluation (the design point)**: task success = exact prediction accuracy of the reactor's hidden `frequency`; mechanism recovery scored SEPARATELY by graph precision/recall/F1, structural Hamming distance (SHD), coefficient F1, and root-node identification. The paper states explicitly: "an agent may predict the held-out frequency without recovering the SCM, or recover the qualitative graph while missing the coefficients."
- Models tested: GPT-5-mini, GPT-5.2-high, Qwen3.5-Thinking, Qwen3.5-Non-thinking, on 3-7 node graphs, temperature 0.1, fixed budgets.
- Headline findings:
    - **RQ1 (prediction != mechanism)**: prediction accuracy is "necessary but not sufficient evidence of mechanism recovery." Controls: swapping linear for hard-quadratic mechanism on fixed 4-node topologies cuts GPT-5-mini accuracy 48% -> 24% while root-node F1 RISES (0.559 -> 0.829) and edge precision holds, but frequency-weight (coefficient) F1 collapses (0.589 -> 0.251). The paper's phrase: "agents lose the mechanism, not the graph."
    - **RQ2 (interaction regime)**: observation-only gives higher prediction accuracy (92%) but much lower graph-recovery F1 (0.47); mixed observation+online-intervention gives 80% accuracy but 0.80 graph F1. Pure intervention is weak. Offline intervention traces (the "Golden" control: inject a good intervention chain without letting the agent choose) improve prediction but hurt edge recovery: the VALUE is in the agent's own online intervention choice, not just in seeing intervention data.
    - **RQ4 (failure mode)**: a major weakness is **premature stopping** (failed runs leave about half the intervention budget unused). A simple **consistency-verification** check mitigates it.

## Interpretation (flagged as inference)

- This is the load-bearing BOUND for the lane's thesis. It shows that when you let an LLM agent run experiments to discover a world model, hitting the prediction target does NOT prove it learned the dynamics. For the repo, "the WAM predicted the next state correctly on this scenario" is necessary but not sufficient evidence that the WAM captured the mechanism that would transfer to a new actor pair or a new scene. The 92%-accuracy / 0.47-F1 split is the quantitative warning.
- It also supports a constructive half of the thesis: agent-CHOSEN online interventions recover more structure than passively consuming data (mixed > observation-only on F1; online > offline-injected). That is direct evidence that an intervene-to-learn loop beats observation-only model fitting for mechanism recovery, which is the affordance/causal lane's open extension made concrete.
- The premature-stopping finding plus the consistency-check fix is a transferable operational lesson: an autoresearch loop needs a stopping/consistency criterion or it quits with budget unspent and a wrong model.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful**: CausaLab's dual-axis evaluation (score the prediction AND, separately, score the recovered mechanism against ground truth) is exactly the evaluation discipline the repo should adopt for any discovered/self-improved WAM. The repo's runtime verifier gives ground-truth `(state, action, next-state)`; a CausaLab-style protocol would also check whether the WAM's IMPLIED rule (which deltas it attributes to which action and state) matches the verifier's observed structure, not just whether the single predicted delta was right. The consistency-verification-against-premature-stopping pattern is a concrete loop-control mechanism.
- **What to avoid / overclaim**: CausaLab is a synthetic SCM micro-world (3-7 nodes, linear/quadratic, clean shift interventions). It is NOT a Minecraft or social result. Do not claim its numbers transfer. Use it as the evaluation template and the honesty bound, not as evidence that LLM agents can discover Minecraft social mechanisms (they were below 0.5 edge-F1 even in a clean 6-node world).

## Limitations (theirs + spotted)

- Theirs: controlled synthetic benchmark; 3-7 node SCMs, mostly linear; finite set of LLM agents/prompts/budgets; analyses focus on final prediction and final hypothesis rather than every trajectory diagnostic.
- Spotted: shift-style (soft) interventions are weaker than hard do() and may understate the difficulty of true interventional discovery; the "AI scientist" framing is aspirational given sub-0.5 edge-F1 in the cleanest setting. Social state (hidden intent, partner-dependent effects) is far outside the tested SCM class.

## Reproducibility status
- partial: environment is synthetic and described in detail (SCM generation, DSL, intervention semantics, budgets); evaluated on proprietary frontier models (GPT-5.x) plus open Qwen3.5, so exact-number reproduction depends on model access.
