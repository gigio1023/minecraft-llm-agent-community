# DeepSeek-R1: Incentivizing Reasoning Capability in LLMs via Reinforcement Learning

- **title**: DeepSeek-R1: Incentivizing Reasoning Capability in LLMs via Reinforcement Learning
- **authors**: DeepSeek-AI (Daya Guo, Dejian Yang, Haowei Zhang, Junxiao Song, Peiyi Wang, Zhihong Shao, et al.)
- **year**: 2025 (arXiv Jan 2025; Nature 2025)
- **venue/source**: arXiv 2501.12948 (also Nature 2025)
- **arxiv_id**: 2501.12948
- **urls**: paper https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.12948 ; models https://huggingface.co/deepseek-ai/DeepSeek-R1
- **source availability**: latex (papers/latex/2501.12948/)

## Primary-source facts (what the paper states/shows)

- The canonical large-scale RLVR (Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards) result.
  DeepSeek-R1-Zero applies RL (GRPO) directly to a base model with **no SFT cold start**, and
  reasoning ability (long chain-of-thought, self-verification, reflection) emerges from the reward
  signal alone.
- **Reward design for reasoning is rule-based, not neural**. Two rewards:
    - **Accuracy reward**: deterministic correctness checks. Math answers must be in a box and are
      matched by rule; code is run through a compiler against predefined test cases.
    - **Format reward**: enforces `<think>...</think>` tags.
- Explicit design statement: "we abstain from applying neural reward models -- whether
  outcome-based or process-based -- to reasoning tasks. This decision is predicated on our
  observation that **neural reward models are susceptible to reward hacking** during large-scale
  reinforcement learning."
- For general (non-reasoning) data they DO use trained preference reward models, and they
  **observed reward hacking** with the helpful reward model; they limited preference-based reward
  to the final 400 of 1700 RL steps because "more training steps with the model based preference
  reward signal may lead to reward hacking."
- Limitation/future-work statement (verbatim sense): "The success of pure RL depends on reliable
  reward signals ... If the reward signal is assigned by a model instead of predefined rules, it
  becomes more susceptible to exploitation as training progresses ... for complex tasks that
  cannot be effectively evaluated by a reliable reward model, scaling up pure RL methods remains
  an open challenge." For such tasks (e.g. writing) they fall back to human-annotated SFT and only
  brief RL.
- Appendix ("importance of verifiers"): rule-based RMs and "LLMs to assess an answer's correctness
  against a predefined ground-truth" both "serve as robust mechanisms for mitigating reward
  hacking," but LLM-based evaluation "exhibits limited generalizability to more complex tasks,
  including open-ended generation and long-form writing, where the notion of correctness is
  inherently more subjective and nuanced."
- Implementation: a "Rule-based Reward Module" with a unified interface over "code executor, answer
  matcher, format checker."

## Interpretation (flagged as inference)

- DeepSeek-R1 is the principled name and the at-scale evidence for the admissible side of the
  bright line: improvement driven by a verifiable, rule-based reward is stable; improvement driven
  by a learned/model reward is gameable. The paper states this in its own voice, twice (the design
  choice and the limitation).
- The paper also draws the exact boundary the repo lives on: verifiable rewards are mature for
  checkable domains (math, code) and the open problem is precisely "tasks that cannot be evaluated
  by a reliable reward model ... open-ended generation ... where correctness is subjective." That
  is the repo's Social/Institutional regime. R1's answer for those tasks is to NOT scale pure RL
  and to use limited human supervision instead, which is a conservative precedent for the repo.
- "RLVR with a rule-based verifier" is the formal description of what the repo's runtime offers:
  the verifier checks typed deltas (inventory, block, durability) deterministically, like R1's
  answer-matcher / code-executor. R1 is the bridge from "the repo has a verifier" to "the repo's
  verifier is a verifiable reward suitable for a self-improvement loop."

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful**: justification, at frontier scale, for designing the repo's
  self-improvement signal as a rule-based verifiable reward and for refusing a learned/LLM-judge
  reward as the primary scorer. The unified rule-based reward interface (executor / matcher /
  checker) is a clean design pattern for the repo's verifier-as-reward adapter. The "limit neural
  reward to the last few steps and watch for hacking" practice is a concrete guardrail.
- **Research contribution it is NOT**: R1's contribution is emergent reasoning from large-scale
  RLVR on a frontier LLM. The repo is not training a frontier reasoner; it borrows the
  verifiable-reward principle and the reward-hacking caution, not the training program.
