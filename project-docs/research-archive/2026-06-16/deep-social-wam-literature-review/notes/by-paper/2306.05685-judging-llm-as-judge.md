## Primary-source facts

- **Research question**: open-ended chat outputs have no reference answer, so ROUGE/BLEU
  and rule-based checks fail. Can a strong LLM act as a judge and match human preference?
  What are the failure modes?
- **Three judge modes**: pairwise comparison (which of two is better / tie), single-answer
  grading (absolute score), reference-guided grading (judge given a reference solution).
  Single-answer grading is less stable than pairwise (absolute scores fluctuate more).
- **Positive headline (agreement)**: on MT-bench, GPT-4 vs human-expert agreement reaches
  **85%** (without ties), *higher than human-human agreement (81%)*. Chatbot Arena shows
  the same trend. Critically, **agreement rises with the quality gap**: from ~70% on
  close pairs to nearly 100% when models differ a lot. (Inference: the judge is reliable
  on easy/large-gap calls and much weaker on near-ties.)
- **Documented biases** (the part the project must heed):
  - **Position bias**: judges favor an answer by its position. On near-identical answers,
    GPT-4 is only **65%** consistent under order-swap; weaker judges worse (Claude-v1
    biased toward the first answer 75% of the time). Suspected roots: training data or the
    left-to-right causal-transformer architecture.
  - **Verbosity bias**: judges favor longer answers. Under a "repetitive list" attack
    (pad an answer with restated items, no new info), failure rate is **91.3%** for
    Claude-v1 and GPT-3.5, **8.7%** for GPT-4.
  - **Self-enhancement bias**: judges may prefer their own outputs (GPT-4 +10% own win
    rate, Claude-v1 +25%), though the authors call a controlled conclusion inconclusive.
  - **Limited math/reasoning grading**: even when a judge *can* solve a problem alone, it
    is "misled by the provided answers" and grades wrong.
- **Mitigations they propose**: swap positions and only count a win if consistent both
  ways; few-shot judging (consistency 65% -> 77.5%, but 4x more expensive and may add new
  bias); chain-of-thought judging (helps but the judge often repeats the answer's own
  mistake); **reference-guided grading** (the judge first solves independently, then
  grades against its own reference): math failure rate **70% -> 15%**. Also a fine-tuned
  (Vicuna-13B) judge as a cheaper option.

## Interpretation (labeled inference)

- This is the foundational LLM-as-judge paper and the source of the bias vocabulary the
  project must guard against (position, verbosity, self-enhancement, misleadability). It
  is *also* the paper most often cited to justify using an LLM judge (85% > 81% human
  agreement), so the project must hold both halves: an LLM judge is useful but biased, and
  reliable mainly when differences are large.
- The "reliable on large gaps, unreliable on near-ties" finding is decisive for the repo:
  social distinctions the project cares about (did Bob *really* return the pickaxe, is
  this trust increase real) are often *close calls*, exactly the regime where the judge is
  least reliable. This, with the validity theme's over-rating-at-long-context results, is
  why the LLM judge cannot be the primary social score.
- Reference-guided grading (judge predicts the expected answer first, then compares) is
  structurally the advisory-WAM loop: predict expected state, then compare to evidence.
  The repo's twist is that the "reference" is the *runtime artifact*, which is far more
  trustworthy than an LLM-generated reference.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful**:
  1. The concrete bias checklist (position, verbosity, self-enhancement, misleadability)
     as mandatory tests/mitigations for *any* LLM-judge axis the repo adds.
  2. Order-swap consistency and reference-guided grading as cheap reliability guards.
  3. The agreement-rises-with-gap result, used as a rule: trust an LLM secondary judgment
     only on clearly-separated cases; treat near-ties as unresolved.
- **NOT the repo's research contribution**: this is evaluation methodology / a judge, i.e.
  support. And it is the strongest single citation for the caution that an LLM judge must
  not be the primary social score (cross-link `benchmark-validity-and-evaluation.md` s6).
- **Overclaim to avoid**: do not cite the 85% agreement as license to score social
  behavior with an LLM judge. That number is single-turn chat-preference on MT-bench with
  expert labelers and large quality gaps; it does not transfer to long-horizon embodied
  social trajectories, and the same paper documents the biases that make it unsafe there.

## Reproducibility

- **Reproducible**: MT-bench questions, ~3K expert votes, ~30K Arena conversations, and
  code (FastChat) all released. Bias experiments are re-runnable; absolute numbers depend
  on the judge model version.
