# MuZero: Mastering Atari, Go, Chess and Shogi by Planning with a Learned Model (Schrittwieser et al., 2019)

The value-equivalence cornerstone: learn only the reward/value/policy-relevant dynamics, no
observation reconstruction, and plan with Monte-Carlo Tree Search. Source: LaTeX deep-read at
`papers/latex/1911.08265/main.tex`.

## Plain-language: what it introduced and why it mattered

Earlier model-based agents (World Models, PlaNet, Dreamer) learn a model that tries to predict
*observations* (pixels or latents that reconstruct pixels). MuZero asked a sharper question:
to plan well, do you need to predict the observation at all? Its answer, the **value-equivalence
principle**: a learned model only needs to predict the quantities that matter for choosing
actions, namely the **reward, the value, and the policy**, and nothing else. It does **not**
reconstruct the observation, and its internal "state" carries no obligation to match the real
environment state; it just has to support accurate predictions of reward/value/policy.

- **Value equivalence**: a model is value-equivalent if planning in it gives the same returns
  as planning in the real environment, i.e., the cumulative reward of a trajectory through the
  abstract (learned) model matches the cumulative reward in reality, even if the model's hidden
  states look nothing like real states.
- Three learned functions:
    - **Representation** `h`: encodes past observations into an initial hidden state `s0`.
    - **Dynamics** `g`: given a hidden state and a hypothetical action, predicts the next hidden
      state and the immediate reward. (Mirrors an MDP transition+reward model, but the hidden
      state "has no semantics of environment state attached to it.")
    - **Prediction** `f`: from a hidden state, predicts the policy and the value.
- It plans with **Monte-Carlo Tree Search (MCTS)** over these learned functions (search builds a
  tree of hypothetical action sequences, scored by the model's reward/value/policy), the same
  search engine as AlphaZero, but now over a *learned* model instead of the true game rules.

Why it mattered: MuZero matched AlphaZero on Go, chess, and shogi **without being given the
rules of the game**, and set a new state of the art on Atari (a domain where model-based planning
had historically struggled). It proved you can plan superhumanly with a model that never
reconstructs what it sees, only what it needs to act. That "predict only what matters for
acting" principle is the conceptual ancestor of decoder-free models like TD-MPC2 and the
JEPA-style implicit models the WAM survey highlights.

## Definitions (first use)

- **Model-based vs model-free RL**: model-based RL learns an explicit model of the environment
  (transitions and/or rewards) and uses it to plan or imagine; model-free RL learns a policy or
  value directly from trial and error without such a model.
- **Monte-Carlo Tree Search (MCTS)**: a planning algorithm that incrementally builds a search
  tree of future action sequences, using a mix of the model's value/policy estimates and visit
  counts to decide where to search next, then picks an action from the resulting visit
  distribution.
- **Bootstrapping (n-step)**: estimating a value by summing a few observed rewards and then
  adding the model's value estimate for the state reached, rather than waiting for the full
  episode return; trades a little bias for much lower variance.
- **Reanalyze**: a sample-efficient MuZero variant that re-runs MCTS on old stored trajectories
  with the latest network weights to generate fresh, improved training targets.

## Primary-source facts

- **What the model predicts**: at each hypothetical step `k`, the policy `p^k` (approximating the
  MCTS search policy), the value `v^k` (approximating the n-step bootstrapped return), and the
  immediate reward `r^k` (approximating the observed reward). Trained end-to-end by
  backpropagation-through-time to match these three targets. "There is no direct constraint or
  requirement for the hidden state to capture all information necessary to reconstruct the
  original observation ... nor any requirement for the hidden state to match the unknown, true
  state of the environment." The hidden states are "free to represent state in whatever way is
  relevant to predicting current and future values and policies."
- **Lineage of value equivalence (stated)**: the Predictron (Silver et al.) first introduced
  value-equivalent models for *prediction* (value without actions); TreeQN, Value Iteration
  Networks, and Value Prediction Networks extended value-equivalent models to *control* (value
  with actions). MuZero adds policy prediction and full MCTS, and scales to Atari + board games.
- **Planning**: AlphaZero-style MCTS generalized to single-agent domains and intermediate,
  discounted rewards. Search outputs a recommended policy and value; an action is sampled from
  the search policy.
- **Dynamics are deterministic** in this paper; stochastic transitions are left to future work.
- **Results**: matches AlphaZero on Go, chess, shogi without game rules; new state of the art on
  57 Atari games. The sample-efficient MuZero Reanalyze reaches 731% median human-normalized
  Atari score (vs 192% IMPALA, 231% Rainbow, 431% LASER) using 200M frames.
- **Cost (per the DreamerV2 paper's contrast)**: large compute; MuZero training was reported as
  impractical for most labs and not publicly implemented at the time.

## Interpretation (labeled inference)

- MuZero is the genealogy's "you do not need to reconstruct the world to act in it" pivot. It is
  the cleanest statement of value-equivalence: the model is judged only by whether planning in it
  produces correct returns. For a newcomer, the contrast to teach is reconstruction-based latent
  models (World Models, PlaNet, Dreamer, which decode pixels at least as a training signal) vs
  value-equivalent/decoder-free models (MuZero, then TD-MPC2), which never reconstruct.
- This branch and the Dreamer branch reach Atari competence around the same time from opposite
  design philosophies (MuZero: value-equivalent planning with MCTS at high compute; DreamerV2:
  reconstruction-based latent imagination on a single GPU). The lineage is two converging
  branches, not one line (see the theme file's timeline).
- MuZero is the WAM survey's natural reference for "what a model should predict": the survey's
  open challenge that "removing the future prediction head at test time does not necessarily
  degrade downstream control" is the same insight MuZero acted on years earlier (cross-ref
  `wam-foundations.md` sections 3 and 7).

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful (conceptually)**: the value-equivalence principle is the single most
  useful idea for a structured-state predictor: predict only the consequences that matter for the
  decision (here, social-material deltas and their costs), not a full reconstruction of the world.
  This is an argument for a lean, outcome-focused predictor, not a heavy generative simulator.
- **Research contribution (not this repo's)**: MCTS-with-a-learned-model and superhuman planning
  are research, and the heavy compute makes MuZero itself impractical to reproduce. The borrow is
  the principle (predict what matters for acting), not the MCTS planner as a runtime action
  selector, which would violate the advisory-WAM rule.

## Relevance to this repo (one line)

Source of value equivalence ("predict only reward/value/policy-relevant consequences, never
reconstruct the world"); the principle argues for a lean outcome-focused social-material predictor,
while MCTS planning and the compute cost are out of scope.
