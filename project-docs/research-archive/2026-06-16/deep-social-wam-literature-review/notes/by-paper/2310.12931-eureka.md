## Primary-source facts (what the paper states/shows)

- **Problem (Reward Generation Problem)**: formalized from the Reward Design Problem of Singh et al. 2010. Given a world model `M = (S, A, T)`, a task string `l`, and a coding LLM, output reward function CODE `R` such that the policy `pi := A_M(R)` trained on `R` maximizes a *fitness* function `F(pi)`. `F` is the GROUND-TRUTH task metric, accessed only by querying trained policies (e.g. a sparse success indicator). `R` is the dense shaped reward being searched; `F` and `R` are distinct (problem-setting.tex lines 4-12).
- **Three algorithmic components** (method.tex):
    1. **Environment as context**: feed the raw environment source code (with the reward code stripped out, if any) to the LLM. Requirement: the code exposes state and action variables. An automatic script extracts only the env-state/action-exposing snippets so the prompt fits the context window and does not leak simulator internals.
    2. **Evolutionary search**: each iteration samples K i.i.d. reward-code candidates from the LLM; because samples are i.i.d., the probability that ALL are buggy decays exponentially in K (K=16 always yields >=1 executable in iteration 1). Then in-context "reward mutation" takes the best reward + its reflection + a mutation prompt and generates K more. 5 iterations, K=16, 5 random restarts per environment.
    3. **Reward reflection**: an automated textual summary of policy training dynamics. Because Eureka rewards expose their individual components as a dictionary, reflection reports the per-component scalar values and the fitness at intermediate checkpoints. This gives credit assignment that the scalar fitness `F` alone cannot (it tells the LLM *why* a reward worked).
- **Results**: on 29 IsaacGym tasks (10 robot morphologies, plus the 20-task Bidexterous Manipulation suite), Eureka outperforms expert human-engineered rewards on **83%** of tasks, average normalized improvement **52%**. With curriculum learning it achieves (for the first time, page-stated) simulated Shadow Hand pen-spinning. Backbone GPT-4 (gpt-4-0314); degrades but stays near human-level with GPT-3.5.
- **Reward novelty / weak correlation**: Eureka rewards are mostly weakly (sometimes NEGATIVELY) correlated with human rewards yet outperform them; the harder the task, the less correlated. Interpretation by the authors: humans are farther from optimal on hard tasks, leaving room for Eureka to "discover novel reward design principles."
- **RLHF-by-reflection**: when `F` does not fully capture human intent, a human can supply textual reward reflection ("Eureka-HF"); a user study (20 raters) prefers the human-guided agent (safer/more stable gait). The paper explicitly states (experiments.tex line 133): "in many open-ended tasks, `F` may not be available in the first place [citing MineDojo 2206.08853]."

## Interpretation (inference, labeled)

- Eureka is the canonical instance of the PROPOSE-A-CODE-CHANGE mechanism this lane owns: an LLM authors *reward code*, and an environment-grounded fitness `F` selects good edits over an evolutionary loop. It is the reward-search analog of ENPIRE's reset-rollout-verify-refine (`enpire`): the LLM proposes, the environment-queried fitness is the success signal, and the loop hill-climbs against that signal, not against the LLM's prose.
- The Eureka/ENPIRE parallel is precise on the success-signal point: both keep the scorer OUTSIDE the proposing LLM (Eureka's `F` is the benchmark's metric; ENPIRE's verifier is physical reset+verify). The repo's verifier plays the same role.
- The weak/negative-correlation result is the double edge: genuine discovery of non-obvious rewards, but also the exact mechanism by which a misspecified `F` would be gamed. Eureka is safe only because `F` is a well-posed dense/binary physics metric.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful for this repo**:
    - The loop shape (sample K code candidates, evaluate by rollout, reflect on per-component dynamics, mutate the best) maps onto "search over advisory-WAM consequence-predictors, scored by verifier-agreement on the repo's already-emitted (state, action, next-state) transitions." The repo gets the "evaluate by rollout" step at near-$0 because the runtime verifier auto-labels transitions.
    - **Reward reflection -> verifier-disagreement reflection**: expose a candidate WAM predictor's sub-predictions (per typed delta) and feed back, per delta, where the prediction matched vs missed the verifier. This is the credit-assignment idea, retargeted from "why a reward worked" to "where a predictor was wrong."
    - **Expose-components discipline**: requiring the generated code to expose a labeled component dict is what makes reflection and inspection possible. A generated WAM predictor should likewise emit per-layer, per-delta sub-claims, not one opaque score.
- **What would be an overclaim / what to avoid**:
    - Eureka REQUIRES a fitness `F` and its own paper says open-ended tasks may lack one. The repo's Physical/Material layers have a clean verifier-derived `F`; the Social/Institutional layers do not. Do NOT claim Eureka-style search transfers to social-reward search without first defining a verifier-grounded score there. This is the lane's key bound.
    - Do not adopt the "discover novel rewards weakly correlated with human intent" framing as a goal for a SOCIAL predictor: in a contested social setting, weak correlation with human judgment is as likely to be metric-gaming as discovery, and there is no clean `F` to adjudicate.
    - Reward-code search is engineering substrate (support), not a research contribution to claim.
