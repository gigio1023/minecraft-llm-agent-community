## Primary-source facts

- Research question: can LLM-empowered agents replace rule-based and RL-based
  agents in macroeconomic ABM, producing more realistic macro phenomena without
  hand-engineered decision rules or heavy per-agent network calibration?
- Positioning (their own genealogy): traces macro modeling from DSGE (assumes a
  "perfect world") to ABM (Tesfatsion, Farmer-Foley), splitting ABM into (1) early
  rule-based models (oversimplified behavior) and (2) learning-based models
  (Trott/Zheng AI Economist, Mi TaxAI) that need large behavioral data and have
  parameter-explosion / calibration pain (cites Windrum 2007 on calibration). The
  pitch: LLMs supply heterogeneous, human-like decisions "without the need for
  specially designed decision rules."
- Architecture (three modules per agent):
  - **Perception**: each agent gets a real-world profile (name, age from 2018 US
    age distribution, job/wage calibrated to 2018 US wage Pareto and tax brackets,
    explicitly citing the AI Economist calibration). The economic state is rendered
    into prompts with macro keywords (e.g. "you became unemployed and had no
    income", "shortage of goods") so the LLM perceives dynamics.
  - **Memory**: a pool of the last L months of (environment, decision) pairs; at
    each quarter-end the agent is prompted to "reflect" on labor/consumption/
    financial markets and adjust strategy.
  - **Action**: the LLM outputs a propensity in [0,1] for working and for
    consumption, accounting for living costs, aspirations, economic trends.
- Environment: labor + consumption market dynamics driven by work/consume
  decisions, plus fiscal and monetary policy. N = 100 agents, 20-year episodes,
  GPT-3.5-turbo-0613 as the LLM, L = 1 works well.
- Baselines: rule-based LEN (Lengnick 2013) and CATS (Gatti 2011), their
  Composite, and the RL-based AI Economist (AI-Eco). The AI-Eco baseline produced
  an "unreasonable unemployment rate (around 46%)" so its indicators were not
  fully reported.
- Findings (their numbers, not reproduced here):
  - EconAgent reproduces classic macro phenomena more plausibly: inflation stays in
    roughly +/-5% after year 3 vs baselines' >20% swings; unemployment 2-12%.
  - EconAgent uniquely recovers two macro regularities with the correct sign: the
    Phillips Curve (unemployment vs wage inflation, Pearson r = -0.619, p<0.01) and
    Okun's Law (unemployment growth vs real GDP growth, r = -0.918, p<0.001). A
    rule-based baseline gets the Phillips Curve sign *wrong* (positive).
  - Ablations (RQ2): perception and reflection modules both matter. Interpretability
    (RQ3): the LLM correctly reasons that consumption should fall when unemployed.
    External intervention (RQ4): the sim responds to policy shocks.
- Stated limitations (theirs, from Limitations section): an LLM-only economy is
  still a simplification; the work is a first step toward LLM macro simulation.

## Interpretation (my inference, labeled)

- EconAgent is the clearest demonstration that an **LLM actor can stand in for a
  hand-tuned behavioral rule in an ABM** and yield more plausible aggregate
  dynamics "even without fine-grained calibration." For the repo this is the
  precedent that the LLM can be the decision policy while the *environment*
  (here the labor/consumption/financial market accounting; in the repo,
  Mineflayer + verifiers) owns the dynamics. The split is exactly the repo's
  "provider proposes, runtime owns truth."
- The validation move is the economics gold standard the repo can reuse: do not
  claim realism by eye; show the simulation recovers a known law (Phillips, Okun)
  with the correct sign and significance. The repo's analog is recovering a known
  material regularity (e.g. scarcity raises the "price"/effort of a contested
  resource; specialization by tool endowment) before claiming social structure.
- The limit for the repo: EconAgent's "economy" is still scalar (work propensity,
  consume propensity, money), not embodied. Nothing physically moves; there is no
  inventory, location, durability, or access. The repo's contribution would be to
  ground the same kind of LLM-driven economic decision in *verified physical item
  transfers*, which EconAgent does not have.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Mechanically useful:
  - The perception/memory/reflection/action module split is a clean template for an
    LLM economic actor; the repo already has analogs (ActorSoul, memory, CycleGoal,
    Actor Turn). The "render economic state + keywords into the prompt" pattern maps
    to the repo's bounded typed facts in `current_state`.
  - The "recover a known macro regularity to validate" protocol is directly
    adoptable as a low-cost validity check.
  - Census calibration of personas (age/wage/job) is a precedent for seeding a
    Minecraft population with heterogeneous starting endowments/skills.
- Avoid / overclaim:
  - Do not equate "reproduces Phillips Curve" with "the model is valid for new
    questions". Concordia's validity hierarchy (note 2312.03664) calls consistency
    with prior theory a *lower* rung than human-generalization. EconAgent shows
    plausibility, not transfer.
  - Do not adopt scalar work/consume propensities as the economic action space; the
    repo's economy is physical items, not numbers.
  - Watch self-evaluation: EconAgent's plausibility is judged against known laws,
    not against held-out human behavior, so "human-like" is an inference.

## WAM layer(s) informed

- Material/Economic WAM (primary): labor, consumption, money, market clearing as

- Institutional WAM (secondary): fiscal/monetary policy and external interventions.
