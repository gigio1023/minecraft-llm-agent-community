# Norms from public sanctions, A learning agent that acquires social norms from public sanctions in decentralized multi-agent settings

- title: A learning agent that acquires social norms from public sanctions in decentralized multi-agent settings
- authors: Eugene Vinitsky, Raphael Köster, John P. Agapiou, Edgar A. Duéñez-Guzmán, Alexander S. Vezhnevets, Joel Z. Leibo (DeepMind)
- year: 2021 (arXiv); published Collective Intelligence, 2023
- venue/source: arXiv 2106.09012
- urls: paper https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.09012 ; hf https://huggingface.co/papers/2106.09012
- source availability: read via `hf papers read 2106.09012` (markdown).

## Primary-source facts (what the paper states)

- Research question: can beneficial social norms *emerge* in a decentralized
  multi-agent system where the only shared public signal is sanctioning, with no
  reward sharing or policy sharing and no externally defined "correct" behavior?
- Two stated functions of social norms (citing Ullmann-Margalit, Lewis,
  Bicchieri): (A) stabilize cooperation in social dilemmas by letting sanctions
  reshape payoffs into a game with new equilibria; (B) equilibrium selection (the
  norm is public knowledge agents condition on to coordinate).
- Formal model: an N-player partially observed general-sum **Markov game with
  public sanctions**:
  - **Sanctioning** is dyadic (one agent acts on another); all agents have common
    knowledge of which events are sanctioning events and their valence
    (approval/disapproval).
  - **Sanctioning opportunity** J(s) ⊆ I²: (i,j) is in J(s) when agent i can
    sanction agent j (in their environments, i is physically in range to "zap" j).
  - **Disapproval event** = i chooses to punish j; **approval event** = i had the
    opportunity but did not. They focus on negative sanctioning (citing Bicchieri
    et al.).
  - **Public sanction-observation function** B(s): broadcasts (i, j, context,
    z=did-disapprove) of the prior step to all agents, interpreted as "gossip"
    diffusing who transgressed, or a shared database.
- Core conceptual move (verbatim emphasis): "the things the agents do in their
  world do not have any objective normative status. The classification of whether
  or not a given behavior constitutes a transgression is determined entirely by
  whether the group has sanctioned similar behavior in the past."
- Architecture: each agent has a **Classifier Norm Model (CNM)**, its own learned
  classifier Ψ_φ predicting whether the group would approve/disapprove of an
  observed behavior, trained by binary cross-entropy on the public sanction
  stream. A **pseudoreward** (intrinsic motivation) rewards the agent for
  sanctioning when its classifier predicts the group would disapprove (+α) and
  penalizes sanctioning when the group would approve (−β). Policy and classifier
  are separate networks (conv backbone + MLP; policy adds LSTM). Decentralized:
  no parameter sharing; A3C + V-Trace + a contrastive auxiliary loss.
- Non-stationarity caveat the authors raise: once a behavior is fully suppressed,
  the classifier stops getting examples of it → catastrophic forgetting. Their fix
  is to freeze the classifier learning rate to 0 after a fixed number of steps.
- Environments: Allelopathic Harvest (agents replant/eat differently colored
  berries, can zap) and Clean Up with a startup problem (public-good cleaning +
  zapping). "Silly rules" (arbitrary, non-harmful norms) are discussed as a
  studied phenomenon.

## Interpretation (my reading, labeled inference)

- This is the cleanest existing formalization of the repo's own caution: "Norms =
  remembered expectations grounded in observed history + violation/repair events,
  not hardcoded global strategy." The CNM literally *is* a remembered,
  history-grounded expectation of what the group sanctions.
- The dyadic sanctioning-opportunity + public observation function is directly
  translatable to a Minecraft event schema: a sanction event needs an offender, a
  sanctioner, a context (the observed transgression), and a valence, all
  loggable, all world-anchored (the "zap" analogue in Minecraft is a refusal,
  a reclaimed chest, a public callout, an exclusion from a worksite).

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Mechanically useful:
  - Schema: a `SanctionEvent` / norm-violation ledger entry (sanctioner, target,
    observed-context-ref, valence, evidence_refs) is a minimal, faithful
    operationalization. The repo's existing `RelationshipEventKind` already has
    `took_shared_resource`, `fake_progress_rejected`, `returned_shared_value` , 
    these are sanction-context candidates.
  - The empirical-expectation side of Bicchieri (what others do/sanction) can be
    *learned from the sanction stream* rather than hardcoded, this is the honest
    way to give an actor a norm without a global rule.
- Research-contribution caution:
  - The CNM is trained with MARL over millions of steps; this repo's actors are
    LLM-proposed turns, not gradient-trained policies. Borrow the *concept and
    schema* (norm = classifier over sanction history), not the training method.
  - Do NOT claim the actor "internalized a norm." The defensible claim is: "the
    actor's behavior changed after observed sanction events recorded in the
    ledger." Internal normative state is not observable.

## WAM layer(s) informed

- **Institutional / Settlement WAM** (norm emergence, sanctioning, equilibrium
  selection) and **Social WAM** (dyadic approval/disapproval changes future
  behavior and relationship state).

## Reproducibility

- Partial: standard MARL methods (A3C/V-Trace) and Melting-Pot-style substrates
  are reproducible in principle, but the specific trained agents are not a drop-in
  for an LLM-turn runtime. Transfers as formal model + schema, not as code.
