# Don't Trust Generative Agents to Mimic Communication on Social Networks Unless You Benchmarked their Empirical Realism

- urls: paper https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.21974 ; html https://arxiv.org/html/2506.21974

## Primary-source facts

- Research question: when can LLM "generative agents" validly replicate real human communication on social networks (X), and how should that validity be measured?
- Contribution: a formal framework for simulating a "Twin World Online Network" (TWON) and a notion of **empirical realism** - "the degree to which simulated behaviors match observed human behaviors." Subtasks: Posting (content creators) and Replying (reactive users), reflecting the power-law participation of social networks.
- Method: instantiate the framework to imitate real X users in English and German; compare in-context prompting vs fine-tuning; measure realism with BLEU, n-gram precision, embedding distance, length ratio, TweetEval sentiment, etc. Realism scores L_b (behavior) and L_r (system) are defined as "a confidence score for any empirical findings obtained by simulating social networks and should be reported together with" the findings.
- Findings / prescriptions:
  1. **Validate empirical realism BEFORE conducting simulations**; always report results relative to the realism of all components.
  2. **Simulate in the same setting the components were fitted/validated**: English models matched real users far better than German (embedding distance, BLEU, stability); changing setting without retraining gives unreliable outcomes.
  3. **In-context prompting is often insufficient**; fine-tuning is required for adequate realism.
  - The impossibility argument: "you cannot have both: a valid and provable realistic social simulation without using real-world user data to model agents and measure their empirical realism." Synthetic personas cannot prove empirical realism; proving it needs individual-level real data; once you have that you again have a real-data replica.

## Interpretation (my inference, labeled)

- This is the sharpest "plausibility ≠ validity" statement in the lane and the cleanest counter to believable-demo skepticism. Its core move - realism must be *measured in the fitted setting and reported as a confidence score alongside any claim* - is directly importable as the repo's reporting discipline: every social score should travel with the evidence/uncertainty that qualifies it.
- The English-vs-German collapse is the concrete face of "changing the setting breaks the simulation," which maps to the repo's partner/seed/world generalization concern: a social result fitted on one seed/partner should not be claimed for another without re-measurement.
- The impossibility argument bounds the repo's ambition correctly: the repo does NOT claim to reproduce real human society (which would require real-human ground truth it doesn't have). It claims to measure *grounded Minecraft social trajectories* - a behavioral, world-verified claim, not a human-fidelity claim. This is why the repo's narrower frame is more defensible.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Mechanically useful: the "report a realism/confidence score with every finding" pattern -> attach an evidence-completeness / uncertainty tag to every social-transition score in reports.
- Avoid / overclaim: do not claim human-fidelity for Minecraft NPC traces; do not generalize a result across settings (seed/partner/world) without re-measuring; do not rely on prompting alone to claim realistic behavior.
