# OPRO: Large Language Models as Optimizers (Optimization by PROmpting)

- **title**: Large Language Models as Optimizers
- **authors**: Chengrun Yang, Xuezhi Wang, Yifeng Lu, Hanxiao Liu, Quoc V. Le, Denny Zhou, Xinyun Chen (Google DeepMind)
- **year**: 2023 (arXiv 2023-09-07; ICLR 2024)
- **venue/source**: ICLR 2024
- **arxiv_id**: 2309.03409
- **urls**: paper https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.03409 ; code https://github.com/google-deepmind/opro
- **source availability**: latex (tarball extracted; approach + experiment + conclusion deep-read incl. overfitting section)

## Primary-source facts (LaTeX-verified)

- **Method = Optimization by PROmpting (OPRO)**: describe the optimization task in natural language; each step the LLM generates new candidate solutions from a meta-prompt that contains previously evaluated (solution, score) pairs (the optimization trajectory, sorted ascending); new solutions are scored and added back; repeat until the LLM stops improving or a step budget is hit. No gradients.
- **Meta-prompt design**: two parts. (1) Problem description + meta-instructions (e.g. "generate a new instruction that achieves a higher accuracy"; optionally an informal regularizer like "the instruction should be concise and generally applicable"). (2) Optimization trajectory: past solutions with their scores, so the LLM can spot what high-scoring solutions share without an explicit update rule.
- **Stability handling**: the LLM generates multiple solutions per step (in-context learning is sensitive to low-quality entries in the trajectory, "especially at the beginning"). Sampling temperature is tuned for exploration vs exploitation.
- **Prompt-optimization setup**: task has train/test splits; training accuracy is the objective during search; test accuracy is measured only after optimization finishes. During search the score is computed on a training subset to "balance the evaluation cost with the generalization performance."
- **Headline result**: best OPRO-optimized prompts beat human-designed prompts by up to 8% on GSM8K and up to 50% on Big-Bench Hard. The famous discovered GSM8K prompt is "Take a deep breath and work on this problem step-by-step" (PaLM 2-L), an instruction no human would have predicted to be optimal.
- **Instruction sensitivity (their own measurement, a landmark fact)**: on GSM8K with PaLM 2-L scorer, "Let's think step by step." = 71.8, "Let's solve the problem together." = 60.5, but "Let's work together to solve this problem step by step." (a semantic combination of the two) = only 49.4. A plausible-looking merge of two good prompts is WORSE than both.
- **Overfitting analysis (dedicated section)**: "Overfitting may result in training accuracy being much higher than the validation/test accuracy. It is difficult to avoid overfitting, but overfitting is less harmful when each candidate solution overfits to a similar extent." With a held-out validation set the same size as training, validation accuracy curves trend up and down ALONGSIDE training curves. But: "our training accuracies are often 5%-20% higher than our test accuracies, despite that our test and overall accuracies are still mostly higher than human-written counterparts. Setting aside a larger training set and optimizing for fewer steps (early stopping) may help reduce overfitting."
- **Conclusion-stated limitations**: (a) the optimizer LLM does not effectively use error cases in the training set to infer improvement directions; (b) prompt optimization requires a training set "at least ... tens of samples, so that the optimized prompt does not severely overfit"; (c) sensitivity to initialization, and exploration/exploitation balance, remain hard. Reproducibility statement: public benchmarks, gpt-3.5-turbo-0613 and gpt-4-0613, text-bison.

## Interpretation (flagged as inference)

- OPRO is the cleanest evidence for the lane's core warning. Its own overfitting section measures a 5-20% train/test gap, and its own instruction-sensitivity numbers show that human plausibility is NOT the right selection criterion (a sensible-looking merge scores 49.4 vs 71.8). Together these say: the score you optimize must be a held-out verifier number, not a human's or an LLM judge's sense of a good prompt.
- "Take a deep breath and work on this problem step-by-step" beating hand-written prompts is the canonical example that auto-search finds things humans would not, AND the reason the score must be external: the win is only legible through the verifier, not through reading the prompt.
- The "overfitting is less harmful when candidates overfit to a similar extent" point is a subtle, honest nuance: relative ranking can survive even when absolute scores overfit. That partially rescues search, but only when the validation set is real and held out.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful**: (1) the meta-prompt = task description + sorted (solution, score) trajectory is a trivially reusable pattern for optimizing an Actor Turn or advisory-WAM prompt against verifier scores; (2) generate-multiple-per-step for stability; (3) the explicit train/validation/test discipline and early-stopping-to-reduce-overfitting; (4) measuring the train/test gap as a health metric of the search.
- **What would be overclaim**: OPRO optimizes a single instruction string against a numerical answer-checker on QA/math. Do not claim it tunes embodied multi-step social policies. The borrow is the optimizer pattern and the overfitting discipline, not the result.
- **Honest bound for thesis**: OPRO literally documents that optimized prompts overfit the training set; the mitigation is a real held-out set and early stopping. For this repo: any prompt OPRO-tuned against the runtime verifier must be re-scored on held-out scenarios, and the train/held-out gap tracked, before it is adopted (and even then only advisory, verifier-gated).
