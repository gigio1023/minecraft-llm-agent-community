## Primary-source facts

- **Research question**: to train reliable models we can use **outcome supervision**
  (feedback on the final result) or **process supervision** (feedback on each intermediate
  reasoning step). Which trains a more reliable *reward model* (verifier)? (Scope note:
  they train only the reward model; they do not RL-tune the generator.)
- **Definitions (used field-wide since)**:
  - **ORM** (Outcome-supervised Reward Model): trained to predict whether a whole solution
    is correct, from the final answer only; at test time, score = prediction at the final
    token.
  - **PRM** (Process-supervised Reward Model): trained to predict the correctness of each
    *step* (a single token after each step; standard LM pipeline). Solution score = the
    **product of per-step correctness probabilities** (probability every step is correct).
  - Verifiers are evaluated by **best-of-N search**: sample N solutions from a fixed
    generator, pick the one the verifier ranks highest, grade its final answer.
- **Headline result**: process supervision trains a much more reliable verifier. The
  large-scale PRM solves **78.2%** of a representative MATH subset under best-of-1860,
  vs **72.4%** for the ORM and **69.6%** for majority voting. The PRM's lead *widens as N
  grows* (better at searching a large candidate set).
- **Why (credit assignment)**: ORM faces a hard credit-assignment problem (it must infer
  *where* a wrong solution failed; on hard problems most solutions are wrong somewhere, so
  a negative outcome label carries little signal). PRM gives a richer signal: how many
  leading steps were correct and the exact location of the first error.
- **Alignment argument** (directly relevant to the project): process supervision is "more
  likely to produce interpretable reasoning," "directly rewards an aligned chain-of-thought
  rather than relying on outcomes as a proxy," and is harder to reward-hack. Outcome
  supervision regularly rewards models that reach the right answer via *incorrect
  reasoning* (false positives). The paper reports process supervision incurs a **negative
  alignment tax** (it is both safer and more capable here).
- **Data (PRM800K)**: ~800K step-level human labels (positive/negative/neutral) over ~75K
  solutions to ~12K MATH problems (filtered from ~1.08M raw labels). Released. Supervision
  given only up to the first incorrect step. Active learning: surface "convincing
  wrong-answer" solutions (rated high by the current best PRM but reaching a wrong answer),
  giving a **2.6x** data-efficiency improvement.
- **Generalization**: on 224 held-out fresh STEM exam questions (AP/AMC, post-dating
  pretraining), the PRM still beats ORM and majority voting (tolerates modest
  distribution shift).
- **Honest caveats (theirs)**: MATH test-set contamination cannot be fully ruled out (but
  expected to affect all methods equally, and uncontaminated STEM results agree); final-
  answer grading mislabels spurious correct-answer/wrong-reasoning solutions (a known ORM
  confound). They expect that in harder domains, final answers will not be easy to check.

## Interpretation (labeled inference)

- This paper is the academic home of the distinction the project's advisory WAM lives
  inside: **scoring intermediate state vs scoring only the end outcome.** The project does
  not score a single end-of-cycle success bit; it wants to predict and check a *sequence*
  of social-material deltas. Process-style, per-step (per-delta) checking is the
  better-aligned, less-hackable design, exactly Lightman et al.'s finding.
- The "right answer via wrong reasoning" failure is the verifier-side twin of the
  validity theme's belief-behavior gap and Project Sid's "says it will share, does not."
  An outcome-only check would pass a cycle where the agent ended in a plausible-looking
  state by an invalid path. Per-step grounding against runtime artifacts is the corrective.
- The PRM's "product of per-step probabilities" scoring is a clean template for an
  advisory social-material score that is the conjunction of many small verified deltas
  rather than one global judgment.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful**:
  1. The ORM-vs-PRM frame as the design axis for the project's evaluation: prefer
     per-delta/per-step checking over outcome-only, for interpretability and
     reward-hacking resistance.
  2. The "convincing wrong-answer" active-learning recipe: to build a social-transition
     verifier, surface cases the current predictor rates as fine but that the runtime
     refutes (negative mining), the highest-information training data.
  3. Step score = product of step correctness as an aggregation pattern for a multi-delta
     cycle score.
- **What this repo can NOT take as its own contribution**: a learned PRM is *evidence
  tooling*; per the shared contract it is support, not the research contribution. Also the
  PRM here grades *text reasoning steps* with a learned model; the project's runtime grades
  *physical/material truth* with deterministic verifiers and artifacts. The repo should not
  replace its deterministic Mineflayer verifier with a learned PRM. The learned predictor
  is advisory on *social/relational* deltas that have no deterministic checker, and even
  there it is scored against world evidence, never the source of truth.
- **Overclaim to avoid**: 78.2% is MATH-specific with a strong GPT-4 generator and 800K
  human labels; do not assume a step-verifier transfers cheaply to embodied social deltas.
  The paper itself flags that easy final-answer checking will not hold in harder domains,
  which is precisely the project's regime (no ground-truth "answer key" for social moves).

## Reproducibility

- **Partial**: PRM800K dataset and grading code released; method fully specified. But
  large-scale models are GPT-4-finetunes (not public weights), so the exact 78.2% is not
  independently reproducible; the relative ORM/PRM ordering is reproducible at small scale
  with the released data.
