## Primary-source facts (what the paper states/shows)

- **Research question**: many RL goals (robustness, transfer, unsupervised RL, emergent complexity) require a distribution of training environments, but hand-authoring one is error-prone. Domain randomization (DR) cannot generate structure or adapt difficulty to the agent; minimax adversarial training drives the environment to worst-case and often unsolvable. How do you auto-generate structured, solvable environments at the agent's frontier?
- **The formalism (Unsupervised Environment Design, UED)**: a developer supplies an *underspecified* environment (free parameters theta); an *environment policy* Lambda maps the current agent policy to a distribution over fully-specified solvable environments. The paper casts three UED rules in classical decision theory: DR = Laplace's "principle of insufficient reason" (uniform), minimax adversary = Wald's maximin, and their method = Savage's **minimax regret**.
- **The mechanism (PAIRED, Protagonist Antagonist Induced Regret Environment Design)**: introduce a third agent. An environment-generating *adversary* builds an environment; a *protagonist* and an *antagonist* both act in it. The adversary's reward is REGRET, approximated as the antagonist's return minus the protagonist's return. If the environment is unsolvable, both agents score the same and the adversary gets zero, so it has no incentive to build impossible levels. If the antagonist can solve it but the protagonist cannot, the adversary scores positively. As the protagonist learns, the adversary must find harder-but-feasible environments, producing an automatic curriculum in the protagonist's "zone of proximal development."
- **The success theorem (the load-bearing theory)**: Theorem (paper Section formalisms, proof in appendix). If achievable rewards split into a SUCCESS band and a FAILURE band that are sufficiently separated, and there exists a policy that succeeds whenever success is possible, then minimax regret will choose a policy with that property. The appendix shows by counterexample that neither DR nor maximin has this property. Quote: "whenever a task has a sufficiently well-defined notion of success and failure it chooses policies which succeed ... whenever there is a policy which ensures success, it will not fail where others could succeed."
- **Setup**: partially-observed grid maze, 15x15 tiles with a wall border, so 13x13 = 169 free tiles. The adversary places the agent (step 0), the goal (step 1), then up to 50 obstacles. Compared against DR, minimax, and population-based minimax (PBT, "our closest approximation of POET").
- **Numbers / results as stated**:
  - PAIRED is "the only method that continues to increase the passable path length to create more challenging mazes," producing agents that solve longer-shortest-path mazes than DR or minimax (measured over 5 seeds, 95% CI).
  - Zero-shot transfer to hand-designed held-out mazes (Empty, Cluttered, FourRooms, SixteenRooms, Labyrinth, Maze): PAIRED retains the ability to solve complex test tasks (labyrinth, maze) where DR and minimax cannot.
  - MuJoCo robustness variant: after constraints on the adversary are removed at iteration 300, the full minimax adversary makes the task unsolvable and the agent's reward "is driven to zero for the rest of training," while PAIRED keeps the task solvable and transfers to unseen parameters even without careful tuning.
- **Honest caveats the paper itself records**:
  - **Regret is noisy** (appendix): training agents on the environment reward itself, rather than on regret, "appears to be more effective for learning complex behavior," because "the performance of the other agent is stochastic" and the agent gets no observation of the other agent. Regret still gave good transfer; the transfer plots used a regret-optimizing agent.
  - **Coordination assumption**: the success guarantee assumes the antagonist and adversary coordinate (reach a Nash equilibrium). "Flexible PAIRED" (antagonist = current best agent, no fixed antagonist) and a combined population both underperformed the original, because they break that coordination.
  - **Parameterization matters for feasibility**: one adversary parameterization that placed objects by scanning the grid scored an average solved path length of ~2 vs ~15 for the used parameterization, because "sampling from its random policy is more likely to produce impossible environments." Design guidance: ensure a random adversary policy can still produce feasible environments.

## Interpretation (flagged as inference)

- PAIRED is the canonical statement of the lane's central question made principled: regret is a "learnable AND solvable" surrogate with a proof that it picks succeeding policies when success is possible. For the repo, this is the theoretical license for using a regret-like signal to schedule which social-material scenario to probe next, instead of hand-tuning difficulty.
- The mechanism that prevents impossible environments (unsolvable -> adversary scores zero) is exactly the property the repo needs from a scenario proposer: it must not be able to "win" by generating ungradeable or impossible social setups. But PAIRED earns this with a second trained agent (the antagonist) as the feasibility oracle, which the repo does not have for social scenarios; the repo's runtime verifier plays a related but weaker role (it labels what happened, it does not certify a-priori solvability).
- The "regret is noisy, env reward worked better" caveat is the strongest transferable warning: a regret/learnability signal computed from another agent's stochastic outcome is high-variance, and in a social world where the outcome is contested it would be far noisier. This supports keeping any learnability signal advisory and the success label deterministic-verifier-owned.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful**: (1) the minimax-regret framing as a difficulty auto-tuner (propose the easiest scenario the actors currently fail) is portable as a scheduling heuristic; (2) the "unsolvable -> zero adversary reward" design rule is the structural guard against a proposer gaming itself; (3) the parameterization-feasibility lesson (a randomly initialized generator must still produce solvable scenarios) is a concrete constraint on how the repo encodes scenario parameters; (4) the SUCCESS/FAILURE-band theorem gives the condition under which regret is trustworthy, namely a clean separation between success and failure, which the repo's verifier provides only at the Physical/Material layers.
- **Not a contribution to copy / overclaim risk**: PAIRED needs two co-trained agents and an antagonist that coordinates with the adversary; importing the full algorithm for social Minecraft is a method transplant, not a contribution, and the social layer lacks the clean SUCCESS/FAILURE bands the theorem requires. The repo should not claim regret-based UED produces "solvable" social scenarios, because solvability of a social scenario (was the promise keepable, the commons maintainable) is not a single separated reward band.
