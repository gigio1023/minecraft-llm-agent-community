# STaR: Bootstrapping Reasoning With Reasoning (Self-Taught Reasoner)

- **title**: STaR: Bootstrapping Reasoning With Reasoning
- **authors**: Eric Zelikman, Yuhuai Wu, Jesse Mu, Noah D. Goodman (Stanford University, Google Research)
- **year**: 2022
- **venue/source**: arXiv 2203.14465 (NeurIPS 2022)
- **arxiv_id**: 2203.14465
- **urls**: paper https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.14465 ; code https://github.com/ezelikman/STaR
- **source availability**: latex (papers/latex/2203.14465/)

## Primary-source facts (what the paper states/shows)

- The first technique to let a pretrained LLM iteratively improve its own reasoning by training
  on self-generated rationales. The loop: (1) few-shot prompt the model to generate a rationale +
  answer for many problems; (2) **filter to keep only rationales whose answer matches the
  ground-truth answer** `1[y_hat = y]`; (3) fine-tune on the kept rationales; (4) repeat with the
  improved model.
- **Rationalization** (the second mechanism): for problems the model gets wrong, give it the
  correct answer as a hint, let it generate a backward-justifying rationale, then add that
  rationale to training WITHOUT the hint. This provides signal on hard problems the forward loop
  would never solve, and prevents the loop from stalling.
- **Formal framing**: STaR is "an approximation to an RL-style policy gradient objective." The
  model is a latent-variable model `p(y|x) = sum_r p(r|x) p(y|x,r)`; with indicator reward
  `1(y_hat = y)`, the policy-gradient discards all rationales not leading to the correct answer,
  which IS the STaR filter. STaR greedily decodes to reduce variance and takes multiple steps per
  batch.
- **Result**: on CommonsenseQA, STaR beats a few-shot baseline (+35.9%) and a directly-fine-tuned
  baseline (+12.5%), and matches a 30x larger model (72.5% vs 73.0%). Also improves arithmetic and
  GSM8K-style math word problems.
- Each outer iteration retrains from the original base model `M` (not continually) to avoid
  overfitting.

## Interpretation (flagged as inference)

- STaR is the founding "learn from self-generated data, selected by an external check" method, and
  it sits squarely on the admissible side of the bright line. The DATA is self-generated (cheap,
  no human rationales), but the SELECTION SIGNAL is the ground-truth answer in the dataset, i.e.
  an external verifier of the outcome. STaR does NOT let the model decide which of its own
  rationales are correct; the known answer decides.
- This is the cleanest template for the autoresearch thesis at the level of objective: a
  verifier-filtered self-improvement loop is exactly a policy gradient with a verifiable indicator
  reward. The repo's runtime verifier supplies that indicator `1[transition matched prediction]`
  for free on every cycle, so a STaR-style loop (keep the actor's self-authored action/skill
  attempts that the verifier marks successful, fine-tune or few-shot on those, repeat) is the most
  defensible self-improvement recipe available to the repo.
- The honest limit STaR itself names: without rationalization the loop stalls because it gets no
  signal from failures. V-STaR (2402.06457) later shows the discarded failures carry signal for a
  verifier. For the repo this means: do not throw away verifier-FAILED transitions; they train the
  advisory WAM to predict failure.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful**: the STaR loop maps directly onto a near-zero-cost repo loop. Run the
  actor; keep the (state, action, next-state) transitions the runtime verifier scores as success;
  use them to refine prompts, few-shot exemplars, or (if ever trained) a policy; repeat. The
  rationalization trick (provide the known good outcome, let the model reconstruct the path) is a
  borrowable way to mine hard scenarios. The "retrain from base each round" hygiene avoids
  compounding drift.
- **Research contribution it is NOT**: STaR's contribution is the bootstrapping method and its RL
  interpretation. The repo borrows the filter-by-verifier loop structure as engineering, not as a
  novel claim.

## WAM layer(s) informed

Method-level. Most transferable at layers where the verifier gives a clean correctness label
(Physical, Material). At Social/Institutional the "correct answer" is contested, so STaR-style
filtering needs a proxy verifier whose reliability is itself in question (see the bright-line
cautions: 2310.01798, 2505.21444).
