## Primary-source facts (what the paper states/shows)

- **Research question**: designing an environment *curriculum* requires expert effort repeated per domain. Since environments are often naturally represented as code, can LLM code generation automate environment-curriculum design at a scale prior UED methods (which need low-dimensional, hand-parameterized spaces) cannot reach?
- **Method (agent-environment co-evolution)**:
  - Environments are generated as **code**: a parkour obstacle course = a height field (2-D grid H of ground heights) + a list of 8 goal coordinates, both written as Python with for-loops and NumPy. A simple validity check (max height, max height-difference thresholds) filters "obviously impossible or incorrect" terrains.
  - Initial generation: the LLM (GPT-4o) emits an initial i.i.d. set of environments.
  - Co-evolution loop: alternate (a) train a *population* of N RL policies, each on its own library of J environments, in parallel; (b) evaluate every policy on the union of all environments so far and select the best policy pi_best; (c) the next round of environments is generated by the LLM *evolving the environments that trained pi_best* (these are known-useful), asking for variations that keep challenging pi_best without being too difficult. Policies are warm-started from pi_best each iteration.
  - Maintaining a *population* of policies hedges against bad generated environments (some policy is always trained on useful ones), and union-evaluation selects the most general policy.
- **Domain**: quadrupedal robot parkour (gaps, hurdles, boxes, ramps, A-frames, stepping stones, etc.). 5 iterations, 8 parallel policy runs of 2000 steps each, plus a pre-trained walking gait. ~24h on 8 A6000 GPUs, ~$15 OpenAI API per run.
- **Evaluation**: a 20-task held-out simulation benchmark (each at 10 difficulty scales), *not* shown to the LLM, plus several real-world courses.
- **Results**: Eurekaverse-trained policies improve continuously without plateauing; baselines trained on a fixed set (human- or LLM-designed) overfit and generalize worse. Eurekaverse outperforms baselines/ablations on the 20 held-out sim tasks and **transfers to the real world** (more robust/adaptive than limited or manually-designed training courses).

## Interpretation (flagged as inference)

- Eurekaverse is the **verified-transfer** cornerstone of my lane: it is the clearest evidence that an LLM can generate a real *curriculum of environments as code* and that the resulting skill transfers to a physical robot, beating human-designed courses. For the wave-4 thesis, it shows the "LLM proposes the environment, the environment-as-code is the artifact, training grounds it" loop works end-to-end when the success signal is clean (traverse the course or fall).
- The "environments are naturally code" insight is the same one OMNI-EPIC uses and the same one the repo could use: a social-material scenario can be expressed as a typed setup (who holds what, what is scarce, which station is shared) that the runtime instantiates and the verifier scores.
- The honest bound is the domain: parkour has a near-binary, resettable, physically-verified success (did the quadruped cross?). That is the Physical layer, where the analogy to robot manipulation (and to ENPIRE) holds best. A social scenario has no such crisp pass/fail, and cannot be reset as cheaply. Eurekaverse's success is precisely *because* the success signal is physical.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful**: (1) **generate scenarios as code/typed-spec + a validity check** that filters impossible setups before they cost a rollout (the repo's analog: schema/permission gates reject malformed scenario specs before Actor Turn). (2) **Co-evolution: evolve the scenarios that trained the best actor**, i.e. branch the next scenarios from the ones that produced competence (the same parent-eligibility idea as POET, but LLM-driven). (3) **Population + union-evaluation** to avoid overfitting to a few generated scenarios and to pick the most general actor/predictor. (4) Concrete **cost anchor** (~$15 / ~24h-8GPU per curriculum run) for budgeting an LLM-driven generation loop, though the repo's no-paid-provider rule means this is a reference, not a plan.
- **Not a contribution to copy / overclaim risk**: Eurekaverse's result is locomotion sim-to-real; the repo must not claim social sim-to-real or that social scenarios transfer like parkour. The clean physical success that makes Eurekaverse work is absent at the Social/Institutional layers. The LLM-generation loop here also uses a paid provider; the repo's loop must be designed around its own verifier and (per the no-paid-provider rule) cannot rely on a frontier API.
- **Limitation (theirs + spotted)**: single domain (parkour); success is physically clean (the easy case); validity check is a hand-coded threshold (domain-specific); relies on a paid frontier LLM; no notion of multi-actor social structure. The transfer claim is strong but specific to locomotion geometry.

## WAM layer(s) informed

Primarily Physical (environment geometry + physical success), with the method (environment-as-code + co-evolution) generalizing to Institutional/settlement scenario scheduling. It is the strongest "the loop works when the verifier is clean" evidence, which bounds where the repo can trust an analogous loop (Physical/Material, not contested Social).
