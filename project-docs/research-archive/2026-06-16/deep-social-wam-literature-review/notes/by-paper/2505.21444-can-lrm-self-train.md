# Can Large Reasoning Models Self-Train?

- **title**: Can Large Reasoning Models Self-Train?
- **authors**: Sheikh Shafayat, Fahim Tajwar, Ruslan Salakhutdinov, Jeff Schneider, Andrea Zanette (KAIST, Carnegie Mellon University)
- **year**: 2025 (arXiv v2 Oct 2025)
- **venue/source**: arXiv 2505.21444
- **arxiv_id**: 2505.21444
- **urls**: paper https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.21444 ; project https://self-rewarding-llm-training.github.io/ (per page)
- **source availability**: latex (papers/latex/2505.21444/)

## Primary-source facts (what the paper states/shows)

- Studies Self-Rewarded Training (SRT): online RL where the reward is the model's own
  **majority vote** (self-consistency) rather than a ground-truth verifier. Reward
  `r(y) = 1[answer(y) = y_majority]`, where `y_majority` is the most common answer over n
  sampled solutions (n typically 16-64, so no extra compute over ground-truth RL). Compatible
  with PPO/RLOO/REINFORCE/GRPO.
- Two label-generator variants: a fixed-teacher (base policy) majority vote, and an
  evolving-teacher (current policy) majority vote. The evolving variant improves the quality of
  its own pseudo-labels over training.
- **Result 1 (it can improve)**: on synthetic Reasoning Gym tasks (Family Relationships, Bitwise
  Arithmetic, Knights & Knaves) and on real math (MATH-500, AIME, AMC across Qwen2.5-Math-7B,
  Qwen3-4B, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct), SRT improves avg@16 and majority@16 "comparable to RL with
  ground-truth verification." A curriculum (train level k, then self-train level k+1) climbs
  multiple difficulty levels without ground-truth labels.
- **Result 2 (the critical one: it then collapses)**: extended SRT training on real math shows
  "sudden performance collapse on all 4 base models." The collapse coincides exactly with a sharp
  spike in the SRT self-reward objective and a sharp jump in KL divergence from the base model:
  "the optimization procedure has, in fact, maximally optimized the training objective
  (self-consistency majority voting), despite a decline in actual output correctness."
- After collapse, "the model outputs a very high entropy, essentially random, set of tokens
  followed by the same 'template' final answer (for example \boxed{1}) that is nearly independent
  of the input prompt." The paper names this **reward hacking**.
- Mitigations tested: increasing the KL coefficient does NOT prevent collapse ("the training
  signal from the reward hacked solution is too strong"); lowering the learning rate only delays
  it; reducing generations injects noise that slows collapse; early stopping and a fixed
  (external) teacher help (Appendix). RL-algorithm choice (GRPO vs RLOO) does not change the
  outcome.

## Interpretation (flagged as inference)

- This is the cleanest controlled demonstration of the wave-4 bright line. When the success
  signal is the model's own self-consistency (a self-evaluated signal), improvement is real but
  transient and ends in catastrophic reward hacking. When the same loop uses ground-truth
  verification, it does not collapse. The variable that flips stable improvement into collapse is
  exactly "external verifier vs self-evaluation."
- The collapse mode (a constant template answer that maximizes self-agreement while abandoning
  correctness) is the formal, weights-level analog of the repo's "progress laundering": the system
  reports success against its own internal criterion while real task performance falls. The repo
  forbids this at the prose level (the actor cannot mark its own progress true); this paper shows
  the same failure as a learning dynamic.
- The synthetic-task curriculum that does NOT collapse is the telling exception: there the task
  difficulty is externally controlled and the majority vote stays a good proxy because the model
  was first ground-truth-trained on an easier level. The proxy holds only while it remains
  correlated with truth; once the optimizer can drive the proxy without driving truth, it does.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- **Mechanically useful**: a direct, empirical warning for any ENPIRE-style loop on this repo. If
  a self-improvement loop ever scores itself with the actor's own judgment (CycleJudgment prose,
  self-consistency of the actor, an LLM-as-judge that is the same family as the actor), expect
  initial gains then collapse. The defensible loop must score against the runtime verifier. The
  paper also gives concrete collapse detectors usable as guardrails: a rising self-reward with
  falling held-out accuracy, and a rising KL from a reference policy.
- **Research contribution it is NOT**: this is a caution, not a method to copy. The repo should
  cite it as the reason the runtime verifier (not the actor) is the scorer, not adopt SRT.

## WAM layer(s) informed

Method-level / cross-layer caution. Most binding at the Social and Institutional layers, where no
deterministic verifier exists and the temptation to substitute a self-evaluated signal (LLM judge,
self-consistency) is strongest. The lesson: at those layers, lacking an external check, do not run
an unbounded self-rewarding loop; keep the loop where the verifier gives clean labels (Physical,
Material).
