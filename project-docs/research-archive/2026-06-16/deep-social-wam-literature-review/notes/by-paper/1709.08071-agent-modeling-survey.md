## Primary-source facts (what the paper states)

- Scope: a unified survey of methods that let an autonomous "modelling agent" build
  models of "modelled agents" to predict their actions, classes, goals, or beliefs.
  A model is "a function which takes as input some portion of the observed interaction
  history, and returns a prediction of some property of interest." "Opponent modelling"
  is the older name (from competitive games), used interchangeably.
- The most important use of a model is to inform the modeller's own decision making
  (feed predicted actions into planning, or pick a counter-strategy for a predicted class).
- Assumptions axis (used to organize every method): deterministic vs stochastic action
  choices; fixed vs changing/learning behavior; decision factors known vs unknown;
  independent vs correlated action choices; common vs conflicting goals; plus
  environment assumptions (move order, discrete/continuous, full vs partial observability).
- Seven salient modelling-method families (the taxonomy):
  1. **Policy reconstruction**: directly model the agent's action probabilities; assume
     a model structure (e.g. automaton, decision tree) and fit parameters from observed
     actions. (Can be expensive; needs many observations.)
  2. **Type-based reasoning**: assume the agent is one of several known "types" and keep
     Bayesian beliefs over types; fast adaptation if the true type is in the set, but
     fails silently if the type space is wrong.
  3. **Classification**: supervised ML to predict an agent's behavior class or identity.
  4. **Plan recognition**: infer goals/plans via hierarchical action descriptions or
     plan libraries (plan-hypothesis space can grow exponentially).
  5. **Recursive reasoning**: explicit nested beliefs ("I believe that you believe...");
     approximate the infinite regress to a fixed depth and simulate the other's reasoning.
  6. **Graphical models**: make dependence structure explicit (Influence Diagrams,
     Multi-Agent Influence Diagrams, graphical games) for compact models / efficient inference.
  7. **Group modelling**: jointly model a group of agents rather than each individually.
  Plus "other methods": implicit modelling, hypothesis testing for model adequacy, safe
  best-response.
- Recursive-reasoning lineage (relevant to WAM-style imagine-then-respond): fictitious
  play and rational learning (game theory) -> Recursive Modeling Method (RMM) ->
  Interactive POMDP (I-POMDP), where the environment state is augmented with *models of
  other agents*, agents are "sub-intentional" (fixed mapping) or "intentional"
  (themselves I-POMDPs), solved by finite recursion. Qualitative alternative: dynamic
  epistemic logic. Empirically, behavioral game theory finds humans reason at average
  recursion depth ~1.5, and artificial recursive agents see little benefit beyond depth 2.
- Open problems (the ones most relevant here):
  - **Modelling in open multiagent systems**: almost all surveyed work assumes a CLOSED
    system (fixed agent set, all present from the start). OPEN systems, where agents
    enter and leave at any time without notice, remain a significant open challenge.
  - **Autonomous model contemplation and revision**: methods do not tell an agent whether
    its chosen modelling method/structure is even adequate; an agent may use a misleading
    model "without ever realising it." They argue strict "correctness" is too strong and
    that *task-adequacy* (does the model help me complete my task) may be the right notion.

## Interpretation (my reading, labeled inference)

- This survey is the field map for the lane: "agent modelling = opponent modelling =
  predicting another agent's policy/goals/beliefs." It predates LLM agents but its
  taxonomy is exactly the vocabulary needed to position newer LLM-ToM and emergent-norm
  work, and to name what a Social WAM is doing (it is a learned, advisory agent model).
- Two open problems are unusually on-target for this project:
  - Open multiagent systems == the project's open-world entrant / "greedy newcomer"
    (GovSim 2404.16698) problem: actors arrive without shared norm history. The survey
    confirms this is a recognized hard open problem, not a solved one.
  - Task-adequacy over strict correctness == the project's stance that a WAM is advisory
    and judged by whether its predictions help (scored against verifier evidence), not by
    matching an unobservable ground-truth mental state.

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Mechanically useful:
  - The 7-family taxonomy gives a clean way to classify any agent-modelling component the
    repo might add. A repo Social WAM is closest to "policy reconstruction + type-based
    reasoning" expressed in natural language (predict the actor's next move; infer which
    role/type/strategy they are), with optional shallow "recursive reasoning" (Bob expects
    Alice to refuse).
  - The assumptions checklist is a ready-made spec sheet: before predicting another actor,
    state whether you assume their behavior is fixed or changing, whether you can observe
    their actions directly (in Minecraft, only if in view), and whether goals are common
    or conflicting. This forces honest scoping.
  - "Task-adequacy not correctness" is a defensible evaluation rubric for an advisory WAM.
- Research-contribution caution:
  - The survey describes methods, not an LLM implementation; it is a positioning tool, not
    a borrowable algorithm. The novelty for this repo would be instantiating
    agent-modelling as natural-language hypotheses scored against Minecraft verifier
    evidence (Hypothetical Minds 2407.07086 is the closest existing such instantiation).
  - Recursion depth: do not build deep nested-belief machinery; the literature says depth
    1-2 is what humans use and what helps. Keep any Social WAM recursion shallow.
