\paragraph{Glucose Monitoring.} 
Consider an RL agent that optimizes solely for a patient's health, without considering the economic costs of its treatment plans. In this case, the proxy reward is based off of a glycemic risk measure, which reflects the likelihood that a patient will suffer an acute hypoglycemic episode, developed by the medical community~\citep{kovatchev2000diabetes}. %The medical community has defined a notion of glycemic risk . Previous work has adapted this glycemic risk measure into a reward function to train a continuous glucose controller~\citep{magni2015glucoserisk}. In our case, the proxy reward is this precise reward function.

However, a less economically-privileged patient may opt for the treatment plan with the least expected cost~\citep{herkert2019insulincost,fralick2019insulin}, not the one with the least amount of risk. From this patient's perspective, the true reward is the expected cost of the treatment plan, which includes the expected cost of hospital visits and the cost of administering the insulin. 

Although larger model treatments reduce hypoglycemic risk more smaller model treatments, they administer more insulin. Based on the average cost of an ER visit for a hypogylcemic episode ($\$1350$ from~\citet{bronstone2016hypoglycemic_cost}) and the average cost of a unit of insulin ($\$0.32$ from~\citet{lee2020insulin}), we find that it is actually more expensive to pursue the larger model's treatment. %This is an example of reward hacking without any phase transition, as the larger models gradually use more insulin.
%In the medical community, glycemic risk is a linear combination of hyperglycemic risk and hypoglycemic risk, with the latter being weighted higher. This is typically done because hypoglycemic episodes pose an immediate medical emergency~\citep{kovatchev2000diabetes} while hyperglycemic episodes have long-term consequences. 
%For this environment, we optimize the policy model for proxy measure of glycemic risk which balances these two risks~\citep{magni2015glucoserisk}. For the true reward, we measure expected treatment cost incurred by the patients which are administered the dosage specified by the RL agent. This is a form of ontological misspecification as managing diabetes risk can have different meaning depending on a person's economic status. 

%We observe that although this proxy risk measure is considered a good reflection of the medical risk, it causes larger policy models to administer greater insulin dosages. Based on the average cost of a ER visit for a hypogylcemic episode (around $\$1350$ from~\citet{bronstone2016hypoglycemic_cost}) and the average cost of a unit of insulin (around $\$0.32$ from~\citet{lee2020insulin}), we calculated the expected cost of each treatment plan and found that over-optimizing for the glucose risk actually increased the expected cost (see Figure~\ref{fig:g_ont}).
