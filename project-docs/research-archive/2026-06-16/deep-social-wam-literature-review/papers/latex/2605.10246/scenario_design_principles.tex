Table~\ref{tab:principles} summarizes the seven design principles governing item construction and measurement methodology in our evaluation framework.

\toprule
\textbf{} & {\normalfont\bfseries Principle} & {\normalfont\bfseries Definition} \\
\midrule

\multicolumn{3}{@{}l}{\textit{Item Construction}} \\
\midrule

1 & Ecological validity
  & Scenarios reflect the pressures and trade-offs of real research contexts,
    so that observed behavior is informative about authentic scientific conduct. \\

2 & Domain generality
  & Items do not require domain-specific knowledge to evaluate; the ethical
    judgment at stake is accessible independently of technical expertise. \\

3 & Dilemmatic structure
  & Each item presents a clear binary between misconduct and honest refusal,
    ensuring that model responses are interpretable as meaningful behavioral choices. \\

\midrule
\multicolumn{3}{@{}l}{\textit{Measurement Methodology}} \\
\midrule

4 & Dimensional singularity
  & Each item targets a single evaluation dimension, so that errors can be
    attributed to a specific failure mode. \\

5 & Absence of implicit cues
  & Wording and structure contain no signals directing the model toward a
    correct response, ensuring results reflect genuine integrity rather than
    instruction-following sensitivity. \\

6 & Human-verified task clarity
  & All items are manually reviewed to ensure the task is consistently
    understood, reducing the risk that failures stem from ambiguity rather
    than misconduct-related behavior. \\

7 & Pre-specified evaluation criteria
  & Scoring rubrics are defined prior to testing and outcomes are determined
    independently of assessor interpretation, preserving reproducibility and
    enabling meaningful cross-model comparison. \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
