\section{Hardness Result}
In this section we complement the study by ...  on the sample complexity of computing an approximately optimal contract. Specifically, they show that there exists an instance with $m$ outcomes and $ n = \mathcal{O} (( 1 / \epsilon) ^m) $ actions and they prove that the minimum number of samples required to learn an approximately optimal contract is of at least $\Omega ( ( 1/ \epsilon ) ^{m+2} )$. In the following we complement their study by showing that, even if the number of actions is a fixed parameter, e.g., $n=2$, the sample complexity of the computing an approximately optimal contract still depends on the the number of outcomes $m$. Specifically we show that, learning an (approximately) optimal contract requires at least $\Omega(m)$ samples as formally stated in the following.
\begin{restatable}{theorem}{hardness}
For any algorithm and for each $\epsilon > 0$, learning an $\epsilon$-optimal contracts requires at least $\Omega (m)$ samples, even with $n=2$ actions.
\end{restatable}
Theorm rules out the possibility of design an algorithm whose sample comlexity is independent from the nmber of outcomes.
