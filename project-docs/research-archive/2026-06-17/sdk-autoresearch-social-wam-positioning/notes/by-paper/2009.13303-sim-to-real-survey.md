# Sim-to-Real Transfer in Deep RL for Robotics: a Survey (and the 2025 foundation-model follow-up)

This note covers two surveys together because they make the same point for the lane: the
**reality gap** is intrinsic to robotics simulation, and closing it is the cost robotics
pays for the thing it can claim (real-world physical transfer).

- (A) title: Sim-to-Real Transfer in Deep Reinforcement Learning for Robotics: a Survey
  - authors: Wenshuai Zhao, Jorge Pena Queralta, Tomi Westerlund
  - year: 2020 - venue: IEEE SSCI 2020 - arxiv_id: 2009.13303
  - url: https://arxiv.org/abs/2009.13303
- (B) title: A Survey of Sim-to-Real Methods in RL: Progress, Prospects and Challenges with Foundation Models
  - authors: Longchao Da, Justin Turnau, Thirulogasankar Pranav Kutralingam, Alvaro Velasquez, Paulo Shakarian, Hua Wei
  - year: 2025 - venue: arXiv cs.LG - arxiv_id: 2502.13187
  - url: https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.13187 ; repo https://github.com/LongchaoDa/AwesomeSim2Real
- source read: abstracts via WebFetch (both verified live). Bodies not deep-read; numbers
  here are from abstracts/standard survey framing, marked claim-only where a figure is
  quoted second-hand.

## Primary-source facts

- The **reality gap / sim-to-real gap** is the mismatch between a simulator and the real
  world. Survey A (abstract): "the gap between the simulated and real worlds degrades the
  performance of the policies once the models are transferred into real robots." Survey B
  (abstract): training in simulation "guarantees safety in learning but introduces an
  **inevitable** sim-to-real gap in terms of deployment, thus causing degraded performance
  and risks in execution."
- Why simulation at all (survey B framing): "limited real-world data and unbearable
  consequences of taking detrimental actions" restrict RL to simulators. Real robot data
  is sample-inefficient, slow, costly, and unsafe to collect.
- Solution categories (survey A): **domain randomization, domain adaptation, imitation
  learning, meta-learning, knowledge distillation**. Survey B reframes around MDP
  components (state, action, transition, reward) and adds foundation-model-based methods.
- Standard cited causes of the gap (general robotics-sim knowledge, not a single quote):
  omitted physical phenomena, inaccurate physical-parameter estimation (mass, friction,
  contact, latency), and discretized numerical integration in the solver.

## Cost / throughput context (claim-only, from web benchmarks, not these surveys)

- MuJoCo is widely rated the most accurate and fastest single-robot physics engine for
  manipulation/control; MuJoCo MJX reaches very high parallel throughput (millions of
  steps/sec on TPU; ~650k steps/sec on an Apple M3 Max, per user benchmarks).
- Isaac Sim (NVIDIA Omniverse/OpenUSD) adds photorealistic rendering and is ~20x slower
  than MuJoCo single-env per user reports, but reaches ~82k-94k FPS with 4096 parallel
  environments on GPU.
- These numbers are real engineering achievements; the point for the lane is that this
  throughput buys **physical fidelity** (contact dynamics, photorealism) so a policy can
  cross the reality gap, not cheap **social-material bookkeeping**.

## Interpretation (labeled, distinction-relevant)

- The reality gap is the defining feature of robotics simulation: a robotics sim is
  valuable precisely because (and to the extent that) a policy trained in it transfers to
  a physical robot. Every solution category above exists to fight the gap. So robotics
  sim's scientific claim is **physical-world transfer**, and the gap is the price.
- This is the clean complement to Minecraft. A Minecraft world has **no reality gap of its
  own**: the game engine is the ground truth there is nothing more real to transfer to.
  That means Minecraft cannot claim real-world physical transfer (its physics are
  game-stylized), but it also pays none of the reality-gap cost: state is exactly,
  deterministically what the engine says, and a runtime verifier can read it for free.
- For social-scale study the asymmetry is decisive. Robotics sim spends its compute budget
  on contact physics and rendering to keep a small number of embodied agents transferable.
  A material-consequence social substrate wants the opposite trade: many agents, exact
  discrete state (who holds what, who owes whom), and near-zero verification cost. Robotics
  sims are not built for that and would pay heavily to host many high-fidelity humanoids
  (see Habitat 3.0's per-humanoid cost in `2310.13724-habitat-3.md`).

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Useful: domain randomization as a robustness idea (vary irrelevant details, keep the
  causal structure) maps loosely onto the social literature's robustness axis (vary prompt
  wording / partner / seed). The "simulate where you will deploy" discipline matches the
  social literature's "re-measure per setting" rule.
- Avoid / overclaim: do not import sim-to-real transfer as a goal for the Minecraft repo;
  there is no physical robot to transfer to, so a "sim-to-real" framing would be a category
  error. Minecraft's defensible claim is grounded behavior inside a deterministic material
  world, not transfer to physical reality.

## WAM layer(s) informed
