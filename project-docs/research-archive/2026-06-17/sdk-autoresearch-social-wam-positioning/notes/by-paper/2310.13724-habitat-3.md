- title: Habitat 3.0: A Co-Habitat for Humans, Avatars and Robots
- authors: Puig, Undersander, Szot, Cote, Yang, Partsey, Desai, Clegg, et al. (Meta FAIR)
- year: 2023 - venue: ICLR 2024
- arxiv_id: 2310.13724

- source read: HTML abstract + body via `hf papers read 2310.13724` (full LaTeX not fetched; numbers below are from the abstract and the speed paragraph in the body, marked as such).

This is the lane's robotics-simulator anchor for "what a physically faithful social
simulator does and does not ground." It is the closest robotics-sim analog to a social
substrate, which is exactly why its limits are informative.

## Primary-source facts (abstract + quoted speed paragraph)

- Research question: a simulation platform for **collaborative human-robot tasks in home
  environments**. Three stated contributions: (1) accurate humanoid (avatar) simulation
  of deformable bodies and diverse appearance/motion at high speed; (2) human-in-the-loop
  infrastructure (real human drives a humanoid via mouse/keyboard or VR while a learned
  robot policy runs); (3) two collaborative tasks.
- Two tasks (the entirety of its "social" surface):
  - **Social Navigation**: the robot must **locate and follow** a humanoid avatar in an
    unseen environment.
  - **Social Rearrangement**: a humanoid and a robot **rearrange a scene together** (move
    household objects to target configurations).
- Terminology note the paper fixes: "avatar"/"humanoid" = a virtual person in simulation;
  "human" = a real person in the world.
- Simulation speed (quoted from body): a robot runs at **245 +/- 19 FPS** in a single
  environment, a humanoid at **188 +/- 2 FPS**; the humanoid is slower because of its
  larger joint count. With 16 parallel environments on a single GPU, robot-humanoid
  reaches **1191 +/- 3 FPS**; two robots reach **1345 FPS** (humanoid adds little
  overhead). [claim-only: from the abstract/body, not independently re-derived.]
- Results (abstract): learned robot policies complete tasks efficiently with **unseen**
  humanoid partners and with real humans in the loop; **emergent behaviors** appear, e.g.
  the robot **yielding space** when it blocks a humanoid. Automated humanoid evaluation
  gives a usable relative ordering of policies that roughly matches human-in-the-loop
  ordering.

## Interpretation (labeled, distinction-relevant)

- Habitat 3.0's "social" is **physical co-presence and motion coordination**, not material
  consequence. Both tasks reduce to "where is my partner and how do I move/manipulate
  without colliding or duplicating effort." There is **no possession ledger, no transfer
  with an obligation, no trust/credit/repair state, no claim over a resource, and no
  post-task continuation**. "Yielding space" is collision-avoidance courtesy, not an
  obligation that persists into a later episode.
- It is a **Physical-WAM-layer** substrate with a thin Material-layer (objects get moved
  to target poses) and effectively **zero** Social/Institutional layer in the repo's
  four-layer sense (physical -> material/economic -> social -> institutional). Its
  contribution to the social layers is co-presence and a moving partner, not consequence.
- The human-in-the-loop tool is its real novelty for social study: a **real** human can
  partner a learned robot. But the *measured* outcome is still task efficiency
  (rearrangement success, follow success), not a verified social-material delta.

## Why it constrains social-scale study (the lane's point)

- High-fidelity humanoid physics caps the agent count. Throughput is reported for **one
  humanoid + one robot** (a dyad), or two robots. The paper does not demonstrate a
  society of many interacting embodied avatars; the deformable-body humanoid is the
  expensive component (188 FPS single-env vs 245 for the robot). Physical fidelity buys
  realistic motion and real-world relevance and pays for it in per-agent cost, which is
  the opposite of what society-scale social study needs (many cheap, deterministic
  actors).

## Mechanically useful vs research contribution

- Useful: the **human-in-the-loop** evaluation pattern (real human partners a policy) is a
  good methodology reference for partner-generalization; "evaluate with unseen partners"
  matches the partner/seed axis the social literature insists on.
- Avoid / overclaim: do not cite Habitat 3.0 as evidence that robotics sims already do
  social-material modeling. Social Navigation/Rearrangement are physical-coordination
  tasks; calling them "social" in the repo's sense would be an overclaim. Robotics sims
  are the place to claim **real-world physical transfer**, which Minecraft cannot claim;
  they are not the place to claim cheap society-scale material-consequence study.

## WAM layer(s) informed

Physical-WAM (humanoid + robot motion), thin Material (object rearrangement). The Social
and Institutional layers are essentially unaddressed: co-presence and motion coordination,
not obligation, possession, trust, or continuity.
